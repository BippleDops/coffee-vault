# Ethiopia Yirgacheffe Gedeb Natural

## Overview

The Ethiopia Yirgacheffe Gedeb Natural represents one of the most celebrated expressions of naturally processed Ethiopian coffee, showcasing the remarkable terroir and indigenous coffee varieties of the Gedeb district within the legendary Yirgacheffe region. This coffee exemplifies the explosive fruit-forward profile and complex floral aromatics that have made naturally processed Ethiopian coffees some of the most sought-after and distinctive in the specialty coffee world.

Gedeb, a woreda (district) in the Gedeo Zone of Southern Ethiopia, sits at the southern boundary of the Yirgacheffe region and has emerged in recent years as a distinct micro-origin producing coffees with their own recognizable character. While sharing many of the classic Yirgacheffe hallmarks—bright acidity, tea-like body, and floral complexity—Gedeb naturals often display an even more pronounced fruit intensity and wild, wine-like fermentation notes that set them apart from their washed Yirgacheffe counterparts and even from naturals produced in other parts of the region.

This particular lot comes from the Gedeb Cooperative Union, a network of smallholder farmers who cultivate coffee in garden plots alongside other crops in the traditional Ethiopian fashion. The cooperative model has been instrumental in maintaining quality standards while ensuring fair compensation for producers in a region where coffee farming has been practiced for centuries but where modern specialty coffee infrastructure is relatively recent.

The coffee consists entirely of Ethiopian heirloom varieties—a catchall term for the dozens or even hundreds of indigenous Coffea arabica cultivars that grow in Ethiopia's forests and smallholder gardens. The exact genetic makeup of any given heirloom lot remains somewhat mysterious, as comprehensive botanical cataloging of Ethiopia's coffee diversity is still ongoing, but this genetic richness is precisely what makes these coffees so endlessly fascinating.

When executed properly, as with this lot, natural processing creates a clean yet explosively aromatic coffee with remarkable depth and complexity.

## Origin Details

### Geographic Context

The Gedeb district sits in the southern portion of the Yirgacheffe region, itself part of the larger Gedeo Zone in Ethiopia's Southern Nations, Nationalities, and Peoples' Region (SNNPR). This area lies approximately 400 kilometers south of Addis Ababa, in the Great Rift Valley highlands that create the distinctive topography and microclimates essential to specialty coffee production.

The landscape of Gedeb is characterized by steep volcanic hillsides covered in dense vegetation, with coffee growing in the shade of indigenous trees and other crops. The soil here is rich volcanic loam, dark red in color and exceptionally fertile due to centuries of volcanic activity. This mineral-rich soil contributes essential nutrients that support healthy coffee trees and influence the flavor complexity found in the final cup. The high organic matter content and excellent drainage characteristics of volcanic soil create ideal conditions for root development and nutrient uptake.

Water sources are abundant in Gedeb, with numerous streams and rivers flowing through the valleys between coffee-growing hillsides. These water sources are essential not only for irrigation during dry periods but also for the processing of washed coffees (though this particular lot is naturally processed and doesn't require washing infrastructure). The availability of water also supports the diverse ecosystem that surrounds coffee cultivation, including the shade trees and other vegetation that create the microclimate beneficial to coffee quality.

The climate in Gedeb is characterized by moderate temperatures year-round, with daytime highs typically reaching 20-25°C (68-77°F) and nighttime lows dropping to 10-15°C (50-59°F). This diurnal temperature variation is particularly pronounced at the higher elevations where this coffee grows, and it's a key factor in developing the dense bean structure and complex sugars that translate to exceptional cup quality. The cool nights slow the coffee cherries' maturation, allowing more time for sugar development and concentration of flavor precursors.

Rainfall patterns in Gedeb follow a bimodal distribution typical of Ethiopian coffee regions, with a longer rainy season from March to May and a shorter one from September to November. Annual rainfall averages 1,600-2,000mm, providing ample moisture for coffee production without the excessive precipitation that can lead to disease pressure or processing challenges. The main harvest occurs from October through January, with peak picking typically happening in November and December when the majority of cherries reach optimal ripeness.

The altitude range of 1,900-2,200 meters above sea level places this coffee in the "strictly high grown" (SHG) category that's associated with the finest specialty coffees. At these elevations, atmospheric pressure is lower and oxygen is less concentrated, slowing photosynthesis and fruit development. The result is a denser coffee bean with more complex cellular structure and higher concentration of organic acids, sugars, and aromatic compounds. The correlation between elevation and quality is particularly pronounced in Ethiopia, where the country's high-altitude plateaus create some of the most elevated coffee-growing terrain in the world.

### Historical Coffee Context

Gedeb's emergence as a recognized micro-origin within Yirgacheffe is relatively recent in the timeline of specialty coffee, though coffee cultivation in the area dates back centuries. Historically, coffees from this district were simply labeled as "Yirgacheffe" without sub-regional differentiation, as the infrastructure and market knowledge necessary to distinguish micro-lots hadn't yet developed.

The recognition of Gedeb as a distinct origin began in the mid-2010s as specialty coffee buyers sought to identify and source the specific microclimates and processing styles that produced particularly exceptional lots. As exporters and cooperatives began to separate Gedeb lots from general Yirgacheffe offerings, a distinct profile emerged: naturally processed Gedeb coffees showed particularly intense fruit characteristics and complexity that distinguished them from neighboring areas.

This geographic specificity represents a broader trend in specialty coffee toward transparency and traceability. Where Ethiopian coffees were once marketed simply by region or grade, the modern specialty market increasingly values precise origin information and direct relationships between roasters and producer communities. The Gedeb Cooperative Union has been at the forefront of this movement, working with international buyers to maintain quality standards and receive premiums that reflect the exceptional cup quality their member farmers produce.

### Producer Community

The [[Producers/Gedeb-Cooperative-Union]] represents a network of several thousand smallholder farmers cultivating coffee in garden plots typically ranging from 0.5 to 2 hectares. This small-scale, diversified farming model is traditional throughout Ethiopia and particularly in Yirgacheffe, where coffee grows alongside food crops, fruit trees, and the indigenous shade trees that create the forest-like environment favored by heirloom varieties.

Most member farmers are multi-generational coffee producers, having learned cultivation and harvest practices from their parents and grandparents. This traditional knowledge includes selective hand-picking of only ripe cherries, a crucial quality control step that ensures consistent maturity and optimal sugar development. During harvest season, families mobilize to pick coffee, often making multiple passes through the same trees as different cherries reach peak ripeness over several weeks.

The cooperative structure provides essential services that individual smallholders couldn't access independently. The union operates centralized processing stations where members deliver their harvested cherries for natural processing on raised beds. Quality control experts monitor the drying process, turning the cherries regularly and removing any defective beans. The cooperative also handles milling, grading, and export logistics, negotiating with international buyers and managing the complex paperwork required for Ethiopian coffee exports.

Importantly, the cooperative model ensures more equitable value distribution. Member farmers receive payment based on cherry quality and volume, with premiums for exceptional lots. The cooperative also returns profits to members as dividends and invests in community development projects including schools, healthcare facilities, and agricultural training programs. This structure stands in contrast to traditional Ethiopian coffee marketing through middlemen, where farmers often received minimal compensation and had no connection to the final market price.

The Organic and Fair Trade certifications held by this cooperative reflect both traditional farming practices and deliberate commitments to sustainability. Most smallholder coffee in Ethiopia is grown organically by default—farmers simply lack access to synthetic fertilizers and pesticides—but formal organic certification requires documentation, training, and inspection that the cooperative facilitates. Fair Trade certification ensures minimum prices, sustainable production practices, and social premiums invested in community development.

## Variety Information

### Ethiopian Heirloom Varieties

The term "Ethiopian Heirloom" represents one of the most intriguing and complex aspects of Ethiopian coffee. Unlike coffee-growing regions where specific varieties like Bourbon, Typica, or Caturra can be identified and cataloged, Ethiopian coffees typically consist of mixed indigenous cultivars that have evolved over centuries in their native environment. This genetic diversity is both a defining characteristic of Ethiopian coffee and a significant factor in its exceptional cup quality and unique flavor profiles.

Ethiopia is the genetic birthplace of Coffea arabica, the species that accounts for approximately 70% of global coffee production and essentially all specialty coffee. Wild coffee forests still exist in southwestern Ethiopia, particularly in the Bale, Kaffa, and Bench Maji regions, where coffee grows as an understory plant in mountain forests. These wild populations represent the original gene pool from which all arabica coffee worldwide ultimately descends, making Ethiopia a living library of coffee genetic diversity.

Over thousands of years, Ethiopian farmers have selected and cultivated coffee from this wild genetic base, creating the semi-wild garden coffee systems that predominate in areas like Yirgacheffe. Unlike the genetic bottlenecks that occurred when coffee was transported to other growing regions—often from just a few parent plants—Ethiopian coffee retains tremendous genetic variation. Individual smallholder farms may contain dozens of genetically distinct coffee plants, each with slightly different characteristics in terms of bean size, cherry color, leaf morphology, and most importantly, flavor potential.

Research by organizations like the Jimma Agricultural Research Center has identified and cataloged hundreds of distinct Ethiopian varieties, but this work remains incomplete, and most coffee exported from Ethiopia is simply labeled as "heirloom" rather than with specific variety names. Recent genetic studies using molecular markers have revealed that Ethiopian coffee diversity far exceeds that found in other arabica-growing regions, with some populations showing genetic traits not found anywhere else in the coffee world.

This genetic diversity manifests in several important ways. First, it contributes to the remarkable range of flavors found in Ethiopian coffee—the floral notes, diverse fruit characteristics, and complex acidity profiles that make these coffees so distinctive. Different genetic expressions within a single lot create layered complexity that's difficult to achieve with monovarietal coffees. Second, the genetic diversity provides natural resilience to pests, diseases, and climate variability, as the mixed variety population includes individuals with different resistance characteristics. Third, it creates challenges for consistent replication—Ethiopian coffees can vary significantly from year to year and even lot to lot, though this variability is also part of their appeal to specialty coffee enthusiasts.

### Physical Characteristics

Ethiopian heirloom varieties display considerable variation in physical characteristics, but certain traits are common in the Yirgacheffe/Gedeb region. Coffee cherries tend to be smaller than those of varieties developed for commercial production, with intense red coloration at full ripeness. The cherries are dense and tightly attached to the branch, requiring careful hand-picking to avoid stripping unripe fruit.

The coffee beans themselves—the seeds inside the cherry—are typically smaller and more irregular in shape than cultivated varieties like Bourbon or Catuai. Screen sizes (the standardized measurement of bean size) commonly range from 14 to 16, with significant variation within a single lot. This size variation is actually a marker of genetic diversity and is considered normal and acceptable in Ethiopian coffees, whereas it would be seen as a quality defect in regions where uniform variety selection is practiced.

Bean shape varies from elongated and oval to more rounded, with the central crease (the seam where the two seeds meet within the cherry) ranging from straight to pronounced and wavy. Bean color in unroasted (green) coffee ranges from blue-green to yellow-green, with naturally processed coffees like this Gedeb lot often showing more yellow-green coloration due to the fruit processing method. The silver skin (the thin layer covering the green bean) is typically more difficult to remove in heirloom varieties, and you may notice more chaff during grinding compared to other origins.

### Cultivation Characteristics

Ethiopian heirloom varieties are exceptionally well-adapted to the growing conditions in their native environment, having evolved over millennia in the specific climate, soil, and altitude ranges where they're cultivated. This adaptation is a significant factor in their continued cultivation despite generally lower yields compared to modern hybrid varieties.

The coffee trees in Gedeb typically grow in the shade of indigenous trees—particularly the Cordia africana (wanza) tree, which is traditionally intercropped with coffee throughout southern Ethiopia. This shade-grown system mimics the forest environment where coffee naturally evolved, providing protection from intense sun, moderating temperature extremes, and contributing organic matter to the soil through leaf litter.

Tree density in Ethiopian gardens is typically lower than in intensively managed commercial plantations, with coffee plants spaced irregularly among other crops and vegetation. This lower density reduces competition for nutrients but also results in lower yields per hectare. Productivity is further limited by the minimal use of inputs—most farms receive little or no fertilizer, relying instead on organic matter and natural soil fertility. Disease and pest pressures are managed primarily through diversity (the variety of plants and vegetation creates a more stable ecosystem) rather than chemical interventions.

Despite these seemingly inefficient practices by industrial agriculture standards, the traditional Ethiopian cultivation system produces exceptional quality. The stress-free growing conditions, genetic diversity, and natural soil fertility combine to create complex, flavorful coffee that commands premium prices in the specialty market. This quality premium often more than compensates for the lower yields, making the traditional system economically viable for smallholder farmers.

### Future Variety Developments

The Ethiopian coffee sector faces important decisions about variety development and genetic resource management. While the heirloom diversity provides unique flavors and cultural significance, there are pressures to introduce higher-yielding varieties to improve farmer incomes and ensure economic sustainability. The Jimma Agricultural Research Center has developed improved varieties with higher productivity and disease resistance, but these generally lack the flavor complexity of traditional heirlooms.

The challenge is balancing productivity with quality and maintaining the genetic diversity that makes Ethiopian coffee valuable both commercially and as a biological resource. Coffee leaf rust, which has devastated coffee production in other regions, remains a concern in Ethiopia, and genetic resistance from the diverse heirloom population may be crucial for future resilience. At the same time, climate change is altering growing conditions, and the adaptability conferred by genetic diversity may be essential for coffee cultivation to continue in traditional regions.

For specialty coffee consumers, the preservation of heirloom varieties represents both an ethical choice and a sensory preference. Supporting coffees like this Gedeb natural—which explicitly value and showcase heirloom varieties—creates market incentives for farmers to maintain traditional cultivation practices and preserve genetic diversity. The premium prices paid for exceptional heirloom coffees demonstrate that quality can be as economically viable as quantity, offering a sustainable pathway that honors both tradition and market realities.

## Processing Method

### Natural Process Overview

The natural process, also called dry process, is the oldest method of coffee processing and remains the dominant technique in Ethiopia. This method involves drying whole coffee cherries in the sun, allowing the fruit to dry around the seed before the dried fruit is mechanically removed during milling. While conceptually simple, natural processing requires careful execution to produce clean, high-quality coffee, and when done well, it creates distinctive flavor profiles that are impossible to achieve through other processing methods.

For this Gedeb lot, the natural processing follows traditional Ethiopian methods refined through the Gedeb Cooperative Union's quality control systems. Coffee cherries are harvested at peak ripeness—indicated by deep red color and high sugar content—and delivered to the cooperative's processing station within hours of picking. This timing is crucial, as cherries begin to ferment immediately after harvest, and controlling the fermentation process is essential to quality.

### Cherry Reception and Sorting

Upon arrival at the processing station, cherries undergo initial sorting to remove unripe, overripe, and defective fruit. In the traditional Ethiopian system, this sorting is done by hand, with experienced workers examining the cherries and removing any that don't meet quality standards. Unripe green cherries are rejected because they lack the sugar content necessary for proper flavor development. Overripe or damaged cherries are removed because they can create off-flavors during fermentation and drying.

The Gedeb Cooperative Union employs multiple sorting stages to ensure exceptional quality. Initial field sorting happens at harvest, where pickers are trained to select only ripe cherries. A second sorting occurs at the washing station upon delivery, where cherries are floated in water tanks—ripe, dense cherries sink while lower-quality cherries and debris float and are removed. Even though this is a natural process coffee that won't be washed, the flotation step provides valuable quality control. A final hand-sorting happens before the cherries go to drying beds, removing any defects that escaped earlier stages.

### Drying Process

The sorted cherries are spread on raised African drying beds, which are wooden or metal frames covered with mesh screens elevated 1-1.5 meters above the ground. This elevation allows air circulation underneath the coffee, promoting even drying and preventing ground moisture from affecting the drying process. The beds are typically covered with shade netting during the hottest parts of the day to protect the cherries from direct sun, which can cause too-rapid drying and lead to flavor defects.

The cherries are spread in thin layers—typically 2-4 centimeters deep—to ensure even air exposure and prevent uneven fermentation or mold development. Thicker layers might dry more slowly on the bottom, creating conditions for unwanted microbial activity, while the top layer dries too quickly, potentially causing the cherry skin to seal and trap moisture inside.

During the drying period, which typically lasts 15-21 days depending on weather conditions, workers turn and rake the cherries multiple times daily. This frequent turning is labor-intensive but essential—it ensures even drying, prevents mold, and allows workers to examine the cherries for any signs of defects or fermentation problems. During turning, workers remove any cherries showing mold, insect damage, or other defects.

The drying beds are covered with plastic sheeting or tarps during rain and overnight to protect the cherries from moisture. Morning dew can rehydrate partially dried cherries and create conditions for mold growth, so the early morning coverage is particularly important. As the cherries dry, their color changes from vibrant red to dark purple-brown, and they become hard and somewhat brittle, with the dried fruit (now called cascara) forming a protective shell around the coffee bean.

### Fermentation Dynamics

While drying occurs, fermentation is simultaneously happening within the cherry. The natural sugars in the fruit pulp are metabolized by yeasts, bacteria, and other microorganisms, creating organic acids, alcohols, and aromatic compounds that permeate the coffee bean. This fermentation is what gives naturally processed coffees their characteristic fruit-forward flavor profiles and complex aromatics.

The fermentation in natural processing is aerobic (occurring in the presence of oxygen) and is influenced by numerous factors including temperature, humidity, microbial populations present on the cherries and in the environment, cherry sugar content, and how thickly the cherries are piled. Managing these variables is what separates excellent natural processing from defective or inconsistent results.

In Gedeb's high-altitude environment, the cool temperatures slow fermentation, allowing it to progress gradually and develop complexity without the sharp alcohol or vinegar notes that can result from uncontrolled fermentation in warmer climates. The cooperative's experience in managing the process—knowing exactly how thickly to spread cherries, how often to turn them, and when to provide shade versus sun exposure—is accumulated knowledge passed down and refined over years of production.

The aromatic compounds created during fermentation include esters (fruity notes), aldehydes (floral notes), ketones (wine-like notes), and various organic acids that contribute to the coffee's acidity profile. These compounds are absorbed into the coffee bean through the parchment layer, creating the intense fruit and floral characteristics that define this coffee's cup profile. The specific microbiological environment in Gedeb—the particular yeasts and bacteria present in the local environment—contributes to the distinctive flavor profile that makes Gedeb naturals recognizable.

### Moisture Content and Storage

The cherries are dried until they reach approximately 11-12% moisture content, measured with moisture meters at the processing station. This moisture level is crucial—too high and the coffee risks mold development during storage; too low and the beans become brittle and can break during milling. Achieving uniform moisture content throughout the bean mass requires the careful turning and monitoring described above.

Once dried, the coffee (still in its dried cherry form) is moved to storage warehouses where it rests in parchment until milling. This resting period allows moisture to equilibrate throughout the beans and can contribute to flavor development. The dried cherries are stored in clean, dry conditions with careful attention to ventilation and pest control.

### Milling and Export Preparation

Before export, the dried cherries go through dry milling, a mechanical process that removes the dried fruit (cascara) and parchment layer to reveal the green coffee bean. This milling is done at specialized facilities with equipment designed to minimize bean damage while effectively removing all the dried fruit material. The coffee passes through hullers, gravity separators, and density sorters to clean the beans and remove any remaining defects.

After milling, the green coffee undergoes final grading and sorting, often including electronic color sorting to remove discolored beans and hand-sorting on tables where workers remove any remaining defects. The Gedeb Cooperative Union's quality control at this stage is rigorous, as defects that make it through to export can significantly impact cup quality and the cooperative's reputation with international buyers.

The graded coffee is then packaged in GrainPro bags (specialized plastic bags that protect against moisture and oxygen) inside jute sacks for export. This packaging protects the coffee during transport and storage, maintaining the fresh, vibrant characteristics that make this Gedeb natural exceptional. From the cooperative's warehouse, the coffee travels to Addis Ababa, through export channels and quality control, and eventually to importers and roasters who purchase directly from the cooperative.

### Processing Method Impact on Flavor

The natural processing method is fundamental to this coffee's flavor profile. The intense blueberry, stone fruit, and floral jasmine notes are direct results of the fermentation and flavor compound development during natural processing. Washed processing of the same coffee from the same trees would produce a radically different cup—likely highlighting delicate florals, citrus acidity, and tea-like body rather than the berry intensity and syrupy mouthfeel found in this natural lot.

The processing method interacts with the terroir to create the final expression. The cool Gedeb climate moderates fermentation, preventing the overripe or boozy notes sometimes found in naturals from warmer regions. The high altitude contributes to the bright acidity that provides structure to the fruit-forward profile. The heirloom varieties provide aromatic complexity. And the careful processing by experienced cooperative members ensures clean fermentation and consistent quality.

Understanding the processing method helps in brewing decisions as well. Naturally processed coffees generally have more soluble material than washed coffees due to the fruit sugars and fermentation compounds absorbed during processing. This means they can become over-extracted more easily, and brewing parameters should account for this by using slightly coarser grinds, lower water temperatures, or shorter brew times compared to washed coffees. The higher sweetness and body also mean these coffees can handle slightly longer extraction in methods like French press or cold brew without becoming astringent.

## Brewing Recommendations

### General Brewing Principles

This Gedeb natural coffee responds well to a variety of brewing methods but has particular preferences and sensitivities that, when understood, allow you to achieve optimal results consistently. The key principles for brewing this coffee successfully are:

**Use water slightly cooler than standard**: The high concentration of soluble material from natural processing means this coffee can become over-extracted easily. Water temperatures of 88-92°C (190-198°F) work better than the standard 93-96°C often recommended for light roasts. The lower temperature slows extraction and helps prevent bitter or astringent notes while still fully expressing the fruit and floral characteristics.

**Grind slightly coarser than standard**: Related to the above, a grind that's slightly coarser than you'd use for a washed coffee of similar roast level will help prevent over-extraction. This is particularly important in immersion methods like French press where contact time is long, but applies to pour-over as well.

**Don't over-extract**: Pay attention to brew time and total dissolved solids (TDS) if you measure. This coffee reaches optimal extraction in the 18-22% range, lower than the 20-24% often targeted for washed coffees. Pushing extraction higher tends to bring out bitter, astringent, or muddy flavors that overwhelm the delicate fruit and floral notes.

**Fresh, filtered water is essential**: The delicate flavor profile of this coffee is particularly susceptible to off-flavors from water. Chlorine, heavy minerals, or other water contaminants will obscure the jasmine and bergamot notes. Use filtered water with moderate mineral content for best results.

**Allow proper degassing time**: As mentioned in the roasting section, this coffee benefits from 5-10 days rest post-roast. If brewing earlier, you may need to adjust parameters (coarser grind, longer bloom time) to account for the CO2 still in the beans.

### Pour-Over (Recommended)

Pour-over methods like V60, Kalita Wave, or Chemex are ideal for this coffee, as they provide clarity and brightness that showcase the complex fruit and floral notes while maintaining the silky body that makes the coffee approachable.

**Recommended ratio**: 1:16 to 1:17 (coffee:water). For example, 20g coffee to 320-340g water. The slightly higher water ratio compared to standard 1:15 helps prevent over-extraction while still achieving full flavor development.

**Grind size**: Medium-fine, slightly coarser than table salt. In grinder-specific terms: around 16-18 on a Baratza Encore, 4-5 on a Comandante, 7-8 on a Fellow Ode. Adjust based on brew time and taste.

**Water temperature**: 90°C (194°F), slightly cooler than standard for light roasts. This temperature fully develops sweetness and fruit notes while preventing sharp acidity or bitter extraction.

**Brewing process**:
1. Preheat your brewing device and rinse the filter
2. Add coffee and create a shallow well in the center
3. Bloom: Add 40-50g water (2-2.5x coffee weight), ensuring all grounds are saturated. Gently swirl or stir to ensure even saturation. Wait 30-45 seconds for bloom phase.
4. Main pour: Add water in slow, steady circular pours, keeping the water level consistent and avoiding pouring directly on the filter. Total water should be added over 2-2.5 minutes.
5. Target total brew time: 2:45-3:15 from first water addition to last drip

**Expected results**: Bright, clean cup with prominent blueberry and jasmine notes, silky body, long sweet finish. If the cup tastes sour or underdeveloped, grind finer or increase water temperature. If it tastes bitter or astringent, grind coarser, use cooler water, or shorten brew time.

**V60 specific notes**: The V60's fast flow rate works well for this coffee's high extractability. Use a slight pulse-pouring technique (3-4 pours after bloom) rather than continuous pouring for better control. The larger V60 (size 02 or 03) provides better heat retention for the light roast.

**Kalita Wave specific notes**: The Wave's flat bottom and slower flow rate create more even extraction and slightly more body than V60. It's more forgiving and may be easier for dialing in this coffee if you're new to pour-over. Use the same parameters but expect brew time to be slightly longer (3:00-3:30).

**Chemex specific notes**: The Chemex's thick filter creates the cleanest, brightest cup—ideal for showcasing the delicate jasmine and bergamot notes. However, it can be too clean for some tasters, losing some of the body and fruit intensity. Consider a slightly finer grind than you'd use for V60 to increase body slightly.

### Aeropress

The Aeropress is an excellent alternative for this coffee, offering versatility and the ability to emphasize different flavor aspects through various techniques.

**Standard Recipe**:
- 17g coffee, medium-fine grind (slightly finer than pour-over)
- 250g water at 88°C (190°F)
- Inverted method
- 1-minute steep time after stirring
- Gentle, slow press over 30 seconds

This standard approach creates a balanced cup with good body and clear fruit notes, slightly more intense than pour-over due to the immersion extraction.

**Bright and Fruity Recipe** (emphasizing acidity and fruit):
- 15g coffee, medium grind
- 250g water at 91°C (196°F)
- Standard orientation (not inverted)
- 10g bloom for 30 seconds
- Add remaining water and stir gently
- Press immediately after stirring (around 1:00 total time)
- Fast, gentle press

This recipe emphasizes the blueberry and stone fruit notes with vibrant acidity, creating a cup closer to pour-over in character.

**Rich and Sweet Recipe** (emphasizing body and sweetness):
- 20g coffee, medium-coarse grind
- 280g water at 85°C (185°F)
- Inverted method
- 2-minute steep with gentle stir at 1 minute
- Very slow, gentle press over 45-60 seconds

This approach creates a richer, sweeter cup with more body and less acidity—good for those who find the standard profile too bright.

### French Press (Immersion)

French press can produce excellent results with this coffee but requires careful attention to grind size and steep time to avoid over-extraction.

**Recommended recipe**:
- 30g coffee, coarse grind (coarser than you'd use for washed coffee)
- 500g water at 88°C (190°F)
- 1:16.7 ratio
- 4-minute steep time (shorter than standard 5-6 minutes)
- Gentle plunge, decant immediately

The coarse grind and shorter steep time prevent the muddy, over-extracted character that can develop in French press with naturally processed coffees. The result is a clean (for French press) cup with rich body, pronounced fruit sweetness, and softer acidity than pour-over methods. The silty texture from French press actually complements the natural processing, adding to the syrupy mouthfeel.

**James Hoffmann method adaptation**: Use the Hoffmann method (steep 4 minutes, break crust, wait 5 minutes, decant without pressing) with this coffee but reduce the total time slightly—break crust at 4 minutes, wait 3-4 minutes, then decant. The long extraction time in standard Hoffmann method can over-extract this coffee.

### Cold Brew

This coffee makes exceptional cold brew, with the natural processing creating intense fruit character that translates beautifully to cold extraction.

**Recommended recipe**:
- 80g coffee, coarse grind
- 1 liter water, room temperature or refrigerated
- 1:12.5 ratio (stronger than hot brewing)
- 16-18 hour steep time (shorter than standard 20-24 hours for cold brew)
- Filter through paper filter or cheesecloth for clarity

The shorter steep time prevents over-extraction while still achieving full flavor development. The result is a sweet, fruity cold brew with prominent blueberry notes and pleasant jasmine aromatics, much more complex than typical cold brew.

**Serving suggestions**: Serve over ice without dilution, or cut with water/milk to taste. The concentrate can be diluted 1:1 with water or milk and served over ice. Consider a splash of tonic water for a coffee tonic—the quinine and bubbles complement the fruit and floral notes beautifully.

**Flash brew alternative**: For a brighter, more nuanced cold coffee, try flash brewing (Japanese iced coffee method). Brew directly onto ice using pour-over method, using half ice half hot water in your ratio calculations. For example: 25g coffee, 150g ice in the carafe, 250g hot water (90°C) for brewing. This creates a bright, clear iced coffee that preserves more of the delicate floral aromatics than cold brew.

### Espresso (Advanced)

While this light-roasted natural Ethiopian isn't traditional espresso material, it can produce interesting and delicious shots for those with dial-in experience and patience.

**Recommended parameters**:
- 18g dose in 18-20g basket
- 1:2.5 to 1:3 ratio (45-54g liquid output)
- 88-90°C water temperature
- 9 bars pressure (or pressure profiling: 4-6 bars pre-infusion, 7-8 bars main extraction)
- 28-35 second extraction time
- Fine-medium grind (not as fine as you'd use for dark roast espresso)

The longer ratio and lower pressure help prevent over-extraction and sourness while achieving the extraction needed for balanced espresso. The result is a bright, intensely fruity shot with pronounced blueberry and jasmine notes—more like a concentrated pour-over than traditional espresso, but delicious in its own right.

**Milk drink considerations**: This coffee can work in milk drinks but isn't ideal for it—the delicate floral and fruit notes tend to be overwhelmed by milk. If you want to try it, use in a cortado or flat white with minimal milk that allows the coffee character to come through. The natural sweetness and fruit notes create a berry-cream character that some tasters enjoy.

### Troubleshooting Common Issues

**Sour or underdeveloped taste**: Grind finer, increase water temperature, extend brew time, or use more coffee. This issue typically indicates under-extraction.

**Bitter or astringent taste**: Grind coarser, decrease water temperature, shorten brew time, or use less coffee. Over-extraction is the likely cause.

**Muddy or muted flavor**: Often caused by old coffee, poor water quality, or water too cool. Check roast date, use fresh filtered water, and increase temperature slightly.

**Lack of sweetness**: May indicate under-extraction or coffee that needs more rest post-roast. Try grinding slightly finer or letting the coffee rest another few days.

**Thin or watery body**: Increase coffee dose (lower ratio like 1:15), grind slightly finer, or try a brewing method with more body like French press or Aeropress.
