## üìã Overview

Guatemala SHB (Strictly Hard Bean) represents Guatemala's highest altitude-grown coffee, classified by elevation rather than bean size. The "Hard Bean" designation refers to the density achieved through slow maturation at high altitudes, resulting in complex, vibrant, and sought-after coffees that showcase Guatemala's exceptional terroir.

**What "Strictly Hard Bean" Means**:
- **Not about hardness**: Refers to bean density, not physical hardness
- **Altitude requirement**: Must be grown at 1,350+ meters (4,500+ feet) above sea level
- **Slow maturation**: Cooler temperatures at altitude slow cherry development
- **Dense beans**: Slower growth creates denser, more flavor-packed beans
- **Quality indicator**: Generally correlates with superior cup quality
- **Premium grade**: Highest classification in Guatemalan grading system

**Guatemala's Grading System**:
1. **SHB** (Strictly Hard Bean): 1,350+ MASL - **Highest Quality**
2. **HB** (Hard Bean): 1,200-1,350 MASL
3. **SH** (Semi-Hard Bean): 1,050-1,200 MASL
4. **EPW** (Extra Prime Washed): 900-1,050 MASL
5. **PW** (Prime Washed): 750-900 MASL
6. **EGW** (Extra Good Washed): 600-750 MASL
7. **GW** (Good Washed): Below 600 MASL

**Why SHB Matters**:
- Altitude creates ideal stress conditions for flavor development
- Cooler temperatures slow maturation, allowing sugars to develop
- High diurnal temperature variation enhances complexity
- Dense beans roast more evenly and consistently
- International buyers specifically seek SHB designation
- Commands premium pricing in commodity and specialty markets

## üåç Guatemala's Coffee Geography

### Climate & Terroir

**Geographic Advantages**:
- **Location**: 14-15¬∞N latitude, ideal coffee belt
- **Topography**: Mountainous volcanic terrain
- **Altitude Range**: Sea level to 4,000+ MASL (coffee: 600-2,000 MASL)
- **Volcanoes**: 37 volcanic peaks provide mineral-rich soils
- **Microclimates**: Extreme diversity in small geographic area
- **Water**: Abundant rainfall and watershed from highlands

**Climate Characteristics**:
- **Temperature**: 16-24¬∞C (61-75¬∞F) in SHB growing zones
- **Rainfall**: 1,000-2,500mm annually, seasonal distribution
- **Dry Season**: November-April (harvest period)
- **Rainy Season**: May-October (growing season)
- **Diurnal Variation**: 10-15¬∞C daily temperature swings at altitude
- **Frost**: Rare but possible above 1,800 MASL
- **Humidity**: Moderate to high, varies by region

**Volcanic Soil Benefits**:
- **Fertility**: Rich in minerals (potassium, phosphorus, nitrogen)
- **Drainage**: Porous volcanic soil prevents waterlogging
- **Acidity**: Mineral composition affects cup acidity
- **Flavor**: Volcanic terroir imparts distinctive character
- **Sustainability**: Naturally fertile, less fertilizer needed

## üå± Varieties in Guatemalan SHB

### Traditional Varieties (Premium Quality)

**Bourbon** (30-40% of SHB production):
- Brought from R√©union Island to Guatemala in 1800s
- **Characteristics**: Sweet, complex, balanced
- **Yield**: Lower than modern varieties
- **Quality**: Exceptional cup quality
- **Challenges**: Susceptible to coffee leaf rust, low productivity
- **Flavor**: Caramel, chocolate, fruit, excellent acidity
- **Status**: Declining but valued for quality

**Typica** (20-30%):
- Original variety brought to Americas
- **Characteristics**: Tall plants, long beans, low yield
- **Quality**: Outstanding complexity and clarity
- **Challenges**: Very susceptible to disease and pests
- **Flavor**: Clean, sweet, complex, excellent acidity
- **Status**: Heritage farms maintain, small production

**Caturra** (20-30%):
- Bourbon mutation, compact plant
- **Characteristics**: Higher yield, good cup quality
- **Quality**: Excellent when grown at altitude
- **Challenges**: Rust-susceptible
- **Flavor**: Bright, clean, citrus, balanced
- **Status**: Common in modern farms

### Modern Varieties

**Catuai** (15-20%):
- Mundo Novo x Caturra hybrid (Brazil origin)
- **Characteristics**: Compact, high yield, strong branches
- **Quality**: Good cup quality, not as complex as Bourbon/Typica
- **Advantages**: Wind-resistant, mechanization-friendly
- **Flavor**: Balanced, sweet, nutty, chocolate
- **Status**: Increasing adoption

**Pache** (5-10%):
- Typica mutation discovered in Guatemala
- **Characteristics**: Very compact, lower yield
- **Quality**: Excellent when grown at altitude
- **Flavor**: Sweet, balanced, similar to Typica
- **Status**: Unique to Guatemala, niche production

**Catimor, Sarchimor** (<5%):
- Rust-resistant hybrids
- **Quality**: Lower cup quality than traditional varieties
- **Use**: Some farms for lower elevations or rust pressure
- **Flavor**: Less complexity, neutral
- **Status**: Limited in SHB zones (quality farmers avoid)

### Emerging & Experimental

**Gesha/Geisha** (<1%):
- Ethiopian variety, experimental in Guatemala
- **Regions**: Some farms in Huehuetenango, Antigua
- **Quality**: Exceptional, competition-winning
- **Flavor**: Floral, jasmine, bergamot, complex
- **Status**: Micro-lots only, very expensive

**Pacamara** (<1%):
- Pacas x Maragogipe hybrid (El Salvador origin)
- **Characteristics**: Very large beans
- **Quality**: Excellent complexity, unique character
- **Flavor**: Fruity, floral, complex, full body
- **Status**: Specialty micro-lots

## üîÑ Processing Methods

### Fully Washed (Traditional) - 85-90% of SHB

**Guatemala's Signature Process**:

1. **Selective Harvesting**
   - Hand-picking only ripe red cherries
   - Multiple passes during harvest season
   - December-April main harvest
   - Family labor or hired pickers

2. **Wet Mill Processing** (Same day as harvest)
   - **Depulping**: Skin removed mechanically
   - **Fermentation**: 12-48 hours (altitude-dependent)
     - Higher altitude = longer fermentation (cooler temps)
     - Tanks or fermentation channels
     - Monitored by touch and water clarity
   - **Washing**: Multiple rinses remove mucilage
     - Clean water channels traditional
     - Density separation (floaters removed)
   - **Soaking**: Some farms add final soak (12-24 hrs)

3. **Drying**
   - **Patio Drying** (Traditional):
     - Brick or concrete patios
     - 7-14 days depending on weather and altitude
     - Raked regularly for even drying
     - Covered at night and during rain
   - **Raised Beds** (Specialty farms):
     - African-style drying beds
     - Better airflow, slower drying
     - Premium quality development
     - 10-18 days
   - **Guardiolas** (Mechanical):
     - Used in wet climates (Cob√°n especially)
     - Rotating drum dryers
     - Faster but requires skill to avoid "baker" defect
     - 48-72 hours
   - **Target**: 10.5-12% moisture content

4. **Dry Milling**
   - Parchment removed (hulling)
   - Size grading
   - Density sorting
   - Color sorting (defect removal)
   - Hand-sorting for final quality control
   - Export preparation

**Flavor Impact of Washing**:
- Clean, bright, articulate flavors
- High acidity (citric, malic)
- Clarity of terroir and variety
- Balanced sweetness
- Medium to full body
- Classic Guatemalan profile

### Natural/Dry Process (5-10% of SHB, Growing)

**Modern Innovation**:
- Traditionally rare in Guatemala (rainy climate challenges)
- Increasing in dry regions (Huehuetenango, Antigua)
- Specialty micro-lots focus

**Process**:
- Whole cherries dried on patios or raised beds
- 20-30 days drying time
- Careful monitoring to prevent mold/fermentation defects
- Removed to dry mill when dried

**Flavor Impact**:
- Fruity, berry notes
- Full, syrupy body
- Lower acidity than washed
- Sweet, complex
- Not traditional for Guatemala but excellent when done well

### Honey/Pulped Natural (3-5%, Emerging)

**Recent Experimentation**:
- Skin removed, mucilage left on
- Dried with sticky mucilage intact
- 10-20 days drying

**Flavor Impact**:
- Between washed and natural
- Sweet, complex
- Fruit notes present but cleaner than full natural
- Medium-full body

### Roasting SHB Coffee

**Density Considerations**:
- SHB beans are very dense (high altitude = slow growth)
- Requires more heat energy to develop
- Longer roast times than lower-altitude coffees
- Risk of uneven roasting if heat too high
- Benefits from moderate, steady heat application

**Recommended Profile**:
1. **Charge Temperature**: 390-410¬∞F (199-210¬∞C)
2. **Drying Phase**: 5-6 minutes to yellow
3. **Browning Phase**: 4-5 minutes to first crack
4. **First Crack**: 10-12 minutes total time
5. **Development Time**: 
   - Light: 60-90 seconds post-crack
   - Medium: 90-150 seconds
   - Medium-Dark: 150-210 seconds
6. **Drop Temperature**:
   - Light: 405-412¬∞F (207-211¬∞C)
   - Medium: 415-425¬∞F (213-218¬∞C)
   - Medium-Dark: 425-435¬∞F (218-224¬∞C)

**Tips**:
- Don't rush SHB - density requires time
- Watch for scorching (high heat on dense exterior)
- Development phase critical for sweetness
- Guatemalan SHB forgives slight over-roasting better than under
