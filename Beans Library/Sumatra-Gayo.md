**Origin**: Sumatra, Indonesia (Aceh Province)  
**Region**: Gayo Highlands, Aceh

## üìã Overview

Sumatra Gayo coffee from the Aceh Province represents Indonesian coffee at its most distinctive - earthy, herbal, full-bodied, and processed using the unique wet-hulling method (Giling Basah) that creates the signature "Sumatra character" loved by dark roast enthusiasts and polarizing to bright coffee lovers.

**What Makes Gayo Coffee Special**:
- **Wet-hulling processing**: Unique to Indonesia, creates distinctive earthy profile
- **Full body**: Among fullest-bodied coffees in the world
- **Low acidity**: Gentle, smooth, stomach-friendly
- **Earthy/Herbal**: Cedar, tobacco, herbs, forest floor notes
- **Dark roast friendly**: Excels at darker roast levels
- **Aceh origin**: From politically autonomous, coffee-focused region
- **Fair trade common**: Strong cooperative structure
- **Unique terroir**: Volcanic soil, tropical climate, high humidity

**The Gayo People & Region**:
- **Gayo** = ethnic group native to central Aceh highlands
- Predominantly Muslim population
- Coffee introduced by Dutch colonial era (late 1800s)
- Coffee is primary cash crop and cultural identity
- Aceh has special autonomous status in Indonesia
- Gayo coffee funded resistance during conflicts
- Strong cooperative tradition and organic farming

## üåç Geographic Origin

### Aceh Province & Gayo Highlands

**Location**:
- **Aceh**: Northernmost province of Sumatra, Indonesia
- **Gayo Highlands**: Central Aceh, around Lake Tawar (Laut Tawar)
- **Altitude**: 1,100-1,600 MASL
- **Primary Towns**: Takengon (coffee capital), Bener Meriah
- **Access**: Remote, mountainous, limited infrastructure

**Climate & Environment**:
- **Temperature**: 15-25¬∞C (59-77¬∞F) in highlands
- **Rainfall**: 2,000-3,000mm annually (very high, tropical)
- **Humidity**: High year-round (70-90%)
- **Seasons**: No distinct dry season (challenge for drying)
- **Cloud cover**: Frequent mist and clouds
- **Soil**: Volcanic, rich in organic matter
- **Forests**: Shade-grown under native trees common

**Why Humidity Matters**:
- High humidity prevents traditional drying
- Led to development of wet-hulling (Giling Basah) method
- Wet-hulling creates signature Sumatra flavor
- Climate necessity became defining characteristic

### Volcanic Terroir

**Volcanoes Influencing Gayo Coffee**:
- Mount Burni Telong
- Mount Geurutee
- Surrounding volcanic chain

**Volcanic Soil Benefits**:
- Rich minerals, naturally fertile
- Good drainage despite high rainfall
- Contributes to full body
- Unique flavor compounds from minerals

## üå± Varieties in Gayo Coffee

### Traditional Varieties

**Typica** (30-40% of production):
- Original variety brought by Dutch
- Tall trees, low yield
- Excellent cup quality
- **Flavor**: Clean, complex, less earthy than typical Gayo
- Declining due to low productivity

**Bourbon** (10-20%):
- Brought later, less common than Typica
- Better yield than Typica
- Good quality
- **Flavor**: Sweet, balanced

### Hybrid Varieties (Dominant in Modern Plantations)

**Catimor** (40-50%):
- Timor Hybrid x Caturra
- Introduced for rust resistance and yield
- **Yield**: 2-3x higher than Typica
- **Quality**: Lower cup quality than Typica/Bourbon
- **Flavor**: Earthy, woody, less complexity
- **Status**: Increasingly dominant (economic necessity)

**Tim Tim (Timor Timor)** (10-20%):
- Timor Hybrid selection
- Similar to Catimor
- Rust-resistant, high-yield
- **Quality**: Commodity-level, lower than Catimor

**Ateng/Lini S** (Various hybrids):
- Indonesian hybrid selections
- Mix of resistance and productivity
- Varying quality levels

**Quality Consideration**:
- **Best Gayo**: Typica and Bourbon lots (rare, expensive)
- **Standard Gayo**: Catimor/hybrid blends (most common)
- **Commodity**: Tim Tim and lower-grade hybrids

## üîÑ Wet-Hulling (Giling Basah) Processing

### Indonesia's Unique Method

Wet-hulling is **nowhere else in coffee world** - unique to Indonesia (especially Sumatra).

**Why Wet-Hulling Exists**:
1. **High Humidity**: Traditional drying impossible (mold, fermentation)
2. **Economic**: Farmers need cash flow quickly
3. **Infrastructure**: Limited access to drying facilities
4. **Climate**: No dry season for extended patio drying
5. **Tradition**: Method developed over 100+ years

**Standard Washed (Most Countries)**:
- Depulp ‚Üí Ferment ‚Üí Wash ‚Üí Dry in parchment ‚Üí Hull

**Wet-Hulling (Giling Basah)**:
- Depulp ‚Üí Brief fermentation ‚Üí Wash ‚Üí **Partially dry** ‚Üí **Hull wet parchment** ‚Üí Final drying

### Wet-Hulling Step-by-Step

1. **Harvest**:
   - Selective picking of ripe cherries
   - Brought to farm or small collection point

2. **Pulping** (Same day):
   - Hand-crank or small motorized pulpers
   - Skin and most fruit removed
   - Mucilage remains on parchment

3. **Fermentation** (12-24 hours):
   - Short fermentation in tanks or bags
   - Breaks down some mucilage
   - Less controlled than Kenya or Colombia

4. **Washing**:
   - Mucilage washed off
   - Clean parchment coffee emerges

5. **Partial Drying** (1-2 days):
   - **Critical difference**: Dried only to 30-50% moisture
   - Parchment still wet, flexible, soft
   - Typical drying targets 10-12% before hulling - NOT in Sumatra
   - Stops here to sell to collector

6. **Wet Hulling** (The Key Step):
   - **Parchment removed while beans still wet**
   - Hulling machine scrapes off wet parchment
   - Naked green bean exposed (no protective parchment)
   - **Moisture**: Beans at 30-50% moisture (vs. normal 10-12%)
   - Beans are soft, vulnerable

7. **Final Drying** (3-7 days):
   - Naked beans dried on patios or tarps
   - Exposed to sun, air, environment
   - **Target**: 12-13% moisture (slightly higher than usual)
   - Irregular drying creates visual defects

8. **Result**:
   - **Appearance**: Blue-green color, often irregular, broken beans
   - **Flavor**: Earthy, herbal, full-bodied, low-acid profile
   - **Body**: Very full, almost syrupy
   - **Clean cup**: Lower than washed coffees (not a defect, just different)

### Why Wet-Hulling Creates "Sumatra Flavor"

**Flavor Impact**:

1. **Earthy/Herbal Notes**:
   - Naked beans absorb environmental flavors during final drying
   - Soil, air, local microorganisms influence flavor
   - Forest floor, mushroom, cedar, tobacco notes develop

2. **Full Body**:
   - Cellular structure changes during wet hulling
   - Beans more porous, absorb water differently during brewing
   - Extracts more soluble solids ‚Üí heavier body

3. **Low Acidity**:
   - Acids degrade during wet hulling exposure
   - Longer contact with moisture reduces brightness
   - Results in smooth, mellow cup

4. **Unique Character**:
   - No other coffee tastes like wet-hulled Sumatra
   - Instantly recognizable to experienced cuppers
   - Divisive: loved or hated, rarely neutral

**Appearance Defects** (Not Flavor Defects):
- Beans often split, cracked, irregular
- Blue-green color (from hulling stress)
- Mixed sizes
- Visual defects don't correlate with cup quality in Gayo

## ‚òï Flavor Profile

### Typical Gayo Characteristics

**Primary Flavors**:
- **Earthy**: Forest floor, wet soil, mushroom, earth
- **Herbal**: Dried herbs, sage, thyme, eucalyptus
- **Cedar**: Woodsy, cedar wood, sawdust (not negative)
- **Tobacco**: Pipe tobacco, dried leaves
- **Spice**: Black pepper, clove, cardamom
- **Chocolate**: Dark chocolate, cocoa (in darker roasts)

**Supporting Notes**:
- **Nuts**: Roasted nuts, walnut
- **Caramel**: Molasses, brown sugar (develops in roasting)
- **Malt**: Grain-like, malty sweetness
- **Smoke**: Subtle smoke, campfire (some lots)
- **Leather**: Aged leather, earthiness
- **Moss**: Green, mossy notes

**Sensory Profile**:

**Acidity**: Very Low ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Almost none** - signature of Sumatra
- Smooth, gentle, mellow
- No brightness or tartness
- Phosphoric hints at most
- Perfect for acid-sensitive stomachs
- Can seem "flat" to bright-coffee lovers

**Body**: Very Full to Extremely Full ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Heaviest body** among specialty coffees
- Syrupy, coating mouthfeel
- Viscous, almost chewy
- Heavy weight on palate
- Lingers, substantial
- Excellent for milk drinks

**Sweetness**: Moderate
- Brown sugar, molasses
- Earthy sweetness (not bright)
- Develops more in medium-dark roasts
- Subdued compared to washed coffees

**Complexity**: Moderate
- Layered earthy/herbal notes
- Interesting but not bright/fruity complexity
- Depth rather than breadth of flavors
- Savory complexity

**Balance**: Body-Dominant
- Not balanced in traditional sense
- Body overwhelms acidity
- But body-sweetness-earthiness harmonize
- Heavy, grounded profile

**Finish**: Long
- Earthy notes linger
- Coating aftertaste
- Herbal persistence
- Clean for Sumatra (no harsh bitter)

### The "Sumatra Controversy"

**Love It**:
- Full body enthusiasts
- Dark roast fans
- Low-acid seekers
- Earthy flavor preference
- French Press devotees
- Those seeking unique, bold flavors

**Hate It**:
- Bright coffee lovers
- Pour-over purists (generally)
- Those seeking fruit/floral notes
- Clean-cup seekers
- Light roast preferences

**Neutral**:
- Rarely neutral - Sumatra is polarizing
- Either embrace earthiness or avoid it

## üî• Roasting Gayo Coffee

### Optimal Roast Levels

**Light Roast** ‚≠ê‚≠ê **Not Recommended**
- Earthy notes can be grassy, vegetal
- Herbal can taste raw, green
- Loses Sumatra's strengths
- Accentuates visual defects
- Better varieties exist for light roasting

**Medium Roast (Full City)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Acceptable**: Earthiness becomes pleasant
- Chocolate notes emerge
- Body develops
- Still relatively low acidity
- Good for those exploring Sumatra

**Medium-Dark (Vienna)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **RECOMMENDED**
- **Sweet spot** for Gayo
- Earthy balanced with chocolate
- Full body, smooth
- Caramelization sweetness
- Herbal becomes pleasant spice
- Classic Sumatra profile

**Dark Roast (French, Italian)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **TRADITIONAL**
- **Traditional Indonesian roast**
- Oily, dark brown to black
- Chocolate, smoke, char
- Very full body
- Zero acidity
- Excellent for French Press, espresso
- Some consider ideal expression of Sumatra

**Verdict**: Medium-dark to dark roasts are where Gayo shines. Don't light roast Sumatra.

### Roasting Technique

**Gayo Roasting Characteristics**:
- **Density**: Lower than high-altitude coffees
- **Moisture**: Often higher than other origins (13% vs. 10-12%)
- **Irregularity**: Mixed bean sizes and moisture
- **Heat Absorption**: Absorbs heat quickly
- **Forgiving**: Hard to ruin Sumatra (going dark is safe)

**Recommended Profile** (Medium-Dark):
1. **Charge**: 375-395¬∞F (190-202¬∞C)
2. **Drying**: 4-5 minutes (faster than dense coffees)
3. **Browning**: 3-4 minutes to first crack
4. **First Crack**: 8-10 minutes total
5. **Development**: **120-180 seconds** (longer for Sumatra)
6. **Drop Temp**: 425-440¬∞F (218-227¬∞C) for Vienna/French

**Tips**:
- **Embrace development**: Sumatra benefits from longer development
- **Don't rush**: Even though less dense, benefits from time
- **Darker is safer**: Won't highlight defects like light roasts
- **Smell for herbals turning pleasant**: When herbs become spice, you're there
- **Expect 2nd crack**: OK to go into second crack for Sumatra

## üçµ Brewing Gayo Coffee

### Best Brewing Methods

**French Press** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **PERFECT**
- **Ideal**: Maximizes body, showcases Sumatra strengths
- **Ratio**: 1:15 to 1:14 (e.g., 30g : 420-450g)
- **Grind**: Medium-coarse
- **Water**: 200-205¬∞F (93-96¬∞C)
- **Steep**: 4:00-5:00 minutes
- **Why Perfect**: Full extraction, heavy body, smooth, rich
- **Result**: Classic Sumatra experience

**Espresso** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **EXCELLENT**
- **Dose**: 18-20g
- **Yield**: 36-40g (1:2 ratio)
- **Time**: 25-30 seconds
- **Temp**: 200-202¬∞F
- **Roast**: Medium-dark to dark
- **Why Excellent**: Syrupy body, thick crema, smooth, chocolatey
- **Milk Drinks**: Incredible in lattes (body cuts through milk)
- **Result**: Rich, bold, smooth espresso

**Moka Pot** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Traditional**: Strong, concentrated, full-bodied
- Medium-dark to dark roast
- **Grind**: Fine-medium
- **Result**: Bold morning coffee, Italian-style

**Drip/Auto-Drip** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Works well**: Reliable, consistent
- **Ratio**: 1:16
- **Grind**: Medium
- **Result**: Smooth, full-bodied, approachable

**Cold Brew** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Surprisingly Excellent**: Smooth, chocolate-forward
- **Ratio**: 1:8 (concentrate)
- **Grind**: Coarse
- **Time**: 16-24 hours
- **Result**: Ultra-smooth, naturally sweet, zero acidity

**Pour Over (V60, Chemex)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Works** but not ideal
- Clean filters reduce body (Sumatra's strength)
- Can highlight herbal/earthy notes unpleasantly if too light
- **Result**: Decent but better methods exist for Sumatra

**AeroPress** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Good**: Versatile, can emphasize body
- Short steep, moderate pressure
- **Result**: Clean but full-bodied
