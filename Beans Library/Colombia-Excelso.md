## üìã Overview

Colombia Excelso represents the standard export-grade Colombian coffee, comprising beans that pass through screen sizes 14, 15, and 16 (approximately 5.5-6.75mm diameter). This is Colombia's most widely exported coffee grade, accounting for the majority of Colombian coffee in the international market.

**Key Characteristics**:
- Standard Colombian export grade (not premium size like Supremo)
- Represents consistent Colombian quality at accessible pricing
- Smaller bean size than Supremo but equal or superior cup quality
- Often from slightly lower altitudes or mixed-grade lots
- Excellent value proposition in specialty market
- Foundation of Colombian coffee's global reputation

While "Excelso" sounds premium, it's actually the industry-standard size grade below Supremo. However, size doesn't determine quality - many exceptional Cup of Excellence and specialty lots are graded Excelso due to varietal characteristics or growing conditions that produce smaller beans.

**Misconception Alert**: Excelso is NOT inferior to Supremo in flavor. Bean size is independent of cup quality. Some of Colombia's finest coffees are Excelso grade.

## üìè Understanding the Excelso Grade

### Colombian Grading System Explained

Colombian coffee grading is based on **physical bean size**, not cup quality:

**Size Classifications**:
1. **Supremo**: Screen 17+ (‚â•6.75mm) - Largest beans
2. **Excelso**: Screen 14-16 (5.5-6.75mm) - Standard export
3. **UGQ** (Usual Good Quality): Mixed smaller sizes
4. **Caracol/Peaberry**: Round beans (separate classification)

**Screen Size Breakdown**:
- **Screen 16**: 6.3-6.75mm (upper Excelso, nearly Supremo)
- **Screen 15**: 5.95-6.3mm (mid-range Excelso)
- **Screen 14**: 5.5-5.95mm (lower Excelso, most common)

**Why Beans Are Excelso Size**:
1. **Variety characteristics**: Some varieties naturally produce smaller beans
2. **Altitude and stress**: Extreme altitude can produce smaller, denser beans
3. **Tree age**: Younger trees often produce smaller beans
4. **Growing conditions**: Nutrient stress or challenging conditions
5. **Crop load**: Heavy yields may result in smaller bean size
6. **Genetics**: Natural variation within varieties

**Important Note**: Cup quality is determined by altitude, variety, processing, and terroir - NOT bean size. Many exceptional coffees are Excelso grade.

## üå± Varieties Common in Excelso Coffee

### Dominant Varieties

**Castillo** (40-50% of Excelso production):
- Colombian rust-resistant hybrid (released 2005)
- Developed by Cenicaf√© (Colombian Coffee Research Center)
- Cross: Caturra x Timor Hybrid (Arabica x Robusta backcross)
- **Characteristics**: Disease resistance, good yield, decent cup quality
- **Size**: Tends toward Excelso rather than Supremo
- **Flavor**: Balanced, nutty, chocolate, moderate acidity
- **Importance**: Saved Colombian coffee industry from devastating rust epidemics

**Caturra** (30-40% of Excelso):
- Bourbon mutation, compact growth habit
- High-yielding compared to Typica/Bourbon
- Excellent cup quality at altitude
- **Size**: Variable, often produces Excelso-grade beans
- **Flavor**: Sweet, balanced, classic Colombian profile
- **Challenge**: Susceptible to coffee leaf rust
- **Status**: Declining but still valued for quality

**Colombia Variety** (15-25%):
- Earlier rust-resistant hybrid (released 1980s)
- Predecessor to Castillo
- **Characteristics**: Good resistance, moderate cup quality
- **Size**: Primarily Excelso grade
- **Flavor**: Nutty, caramel, balanced but less complexity than Caturra

**Traditional Varieties** (<10%, declining):
- **Typica**: Original Colombian variety, low yield, excellent quality
- **Bourbon**: Rich, sweet, complex but rust-susceptible
- **Tabi**: Colombian-bred variety, good quality and size
- **Status**: Mostly replaced by rust-resistant varieties

**Emerging Varieties** (<5%):
- **Cenicaf√© 1**: New rust-resistant variety (2016)
- **Gesha/Geisha**: Experimental micro-lots in Cauca, Nari√±o
- **Castillo variants**: Regional selections (Castillo Tambo, Castillo Regional)

## üîÑ Processing: Colombian Washed Method

### Traditional Wet Processing

Colombia's signature washed processing creates the clean, balanced profile Excelso is known for:

**Harvest Process**:
- **Timing**: Two annual harvests in many regions
  - **Main harvest**: October-February
  - **Mitaca** (smaller harvest): April-June
- **Method**: Selective hand-picking of ripe cherries only
- **Quality control**: Multiple passes to ensure ripeness
- **Challenge**: Labor-intensive on steep mountain slopes

**Wet Mill Processing**:

1. **Reception & Sorting** (Day 1, within hours of picking)
   - Cherries delivered to farm or cooperative wet mill
   - Floatation tank removes unripe/defective cherries
   - Immediate processing to prevent fermentation

2. **Depulping** (Day 1)
   - Mechanical disc pulpers remove skin and most fruit
   - Mucilage layer remains attached to parchment
   - Separation by density (ripe sink, unripe float)

3. **Fermentation** (12-36 hours, Day 1-2)
   - Beans in fermentation tanks with or without water
   - Natural enzymes break down mucilage
   - Duration depends on temperature and altitude
   - Higher altitude = longer fermentation (cooler temps)
   - Monitored by touch and visual inspection
   - Over-fermentation creates defects (vinegar, fermented flavors)
   - Under-fermentation leaves residual mucilage

4. **Washing** (Day 2)
   - Running water removes fermented mucilage
   - Washing channels with gradient
   - Final density separation (floaters removed)
   - Clean parchment coffee emerges

5. **Drying** (7-15 days, Day 2-17)
   - **Mechanical drying** (Guardiola dryers): Common due to rain
     - Rotating drum dryers, temperature-controlled
     - Faster but requires careful management to avoid "baker" defect
   - **Parabolic solar dryers**: Greenhouse-style, increasingly popular
     - Protects from rain, uses solar heat
     - Better quality than mechanical, faster than open air
   - **Raised beds**: Premium specialty processing
     - Slower drying, more labor
     - Best flavor development
     - Weather-dependent
   - **Target moisture**: 10-12% for export and storage

6. **Dry Milling** (At export facility)
   - **Hulling**: Parchment layer removed
   - **Screen sorting**: Beans separated by size (Excelso vs Supremo)
   - **Density sorting**: Catador (gravity separator)
   - **Electronic sorting**: Color and defect removal
   - **Hand sorting**: Final quality control for specialty lots
   - **Grading**: Classification as Excelso based on screen size 14-16

**Impact on Flavor**:
- Clean, articulate flavor profile
- Bright acidity (citric, malic acids)
- Clarity of terroir characteristics
- Balanced sweetness and body
- Minimal fruit/ferment notes (unlike natural processing)
- Consistent cup quality batch-to-batch

### Roast Development Tips

**Considerations for Excelso**:
- Smaller bean size than Supremo = faster heat penetration
- More even roast development potential
- Less risk of exterior scorching with under-developed interior
- Shorter development time needed post-crack
- Consistent batch roasting due to uniform size

**Roast Profile Suggestions**:
- **Charge temperature**: 375-400¬∞F (190-205¬∞C)
- **Turning point**: Around 150-160¬∞F (65-70¬∞C)
- **Drying phase**: 4-6 minutes to yellow
- **Browning phase**: 3-4 minutes to first crack
- **First crack**: Should occur 9-11 minutes total time
- **Development**: 60-90 seconds post-crack for medium roast
- **End temperature**: 410-425¬∞F (210-218¬∞C) for Full City

**Common Roasting Challenges**:
- **Baking**: Insufficient heat during browning phase
  - Solution: Increase heat/airflow in middle phase
- **Scorching**: Overheated exterior
  - Less common with Excelso than Supremo
- **Underdevelopment**: Grassy, vegetal notes
  - Solution: Extend development time post-crack

## üèÜ Quality Markers

### Identifying High-Quality Excelso

**Green Bean Indicators**:
- Uniform Excelso size (screen 14-16 consistent)
- Blue-green color (fresh crop)
- Dense beans (sink in water)
- Minimal defects (quakers, broken beans)
- Pleasant, sweet aroma from bag
- Moisture content 10-12%

**Labeling Information to Look For**:
- **Region specified**: "Huila Excelso" better than generic "Colombia Excelso"
- **Altitude**: 1,600+ MASL indicates quality
- **Variety**: Caturra or heirloom preferred over Castillo (though Castillo can be excellent)
- **Processing notes**: Washing station named, fermentation details
- **Farm/cooperative**: Traceability to producer level
- **Certifications**: Organic, Fair Trade, Rainforest Alliance (optional but indicate care)
- **Cup score**: 82+ good, 84+ excellent, 86+ exceptional for Excelso
- **Crop year**: Current or previous year (freshness)

**Red Flags**:
- No information beyond "Colombia Excelso"
- Extremely low price (below market rates)
- No roast date (roasted coffee)
- Generic descriptions only
- Very old crop (2+ years)

## üìö Historical Context

### Excelso's Role in Colombian Coffee

**Development of Grading System**:
- Established mid-20th century as Colombia industrialized coffee export
- Created to standardize quality for international trade
- Supremo/Excelso distinction based on European screen sizing
- Became global standard for Colombian coffee classification

**Export Volumes**:
- Excelso represents 60-70% of Colombian exports
- Supremo accounts for 25-30%
- Remaining is UGQ and specialty grades
- Excelso is Colombia's "workhorse" export grade

**Market Evolution**:
- Historically sold as commodity-grade bulk coffee
- Specialty movement revealed Excelso's quality potential
- Modern buyers recognize size-quality disconnect
- Premium Excelso now commands specialty prices
- Direct trade allows farmers to capture value of quality Excelso

### Cultural Significance

**Colombian Coffee Identity**:
- "Excelso" means "excellent" in Spanish (chosen deliberately)
- Part of Colombian national pride in coffee
- Supported by decades of FNC (Federaci√≥n Nacional de Cafeteros) marketing
- Juan Valdez campaign featured both Supremo and Excelso
