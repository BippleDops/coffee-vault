## üìã Overview

Colombia Supremo represents the highest grade of Colombian coffee based on bean size, with beans that pass through a screen size 17 or larger (approximately 6.75mm). This classification system is unique to Colombia and reflects not just size, but often correlates with quality, as larger beans typically come from higher altitudes and more mature plants.

**What Makes It Special**:
- Largest bean size classification in Colombia
- Typically from premium growing regions
- Classic Colombian flavor profile
- Consistent quality and availability
- Well-suited for various brewing methods
- Excellent introduction to specialty Colombian coffee

Colombia Supremo is the flagship coffee of one of the world's most recognized coffee-producing nations. While "Supremo" is primarily a size grading rather than quality grading, it has become synonymous with Colombian coffee excellence in international markets.

## üî¨ Bean Characteristics

### Size Grading System

Colombian coffee uses a precise screen size grading system:

**Size Classifications**:
- **Supremo**: Screen size 17+ (‚â•6.75mm diameter) - Largest
- **Excelso**: Screen size 14-16 (5.5-6.75mm) - Standard export grade
- **UGQ** (Usual Good Quality): Mixed sizes, lower grade
- **Caracol** (Peaberry): Round beans, separate classification

**Physical Characteristics**:
- Large, uniform beans
- Blue-green color when unroasted
- Oval shape, distinct center cut
- Dense structure from high-altitude growing
- Clean, minimal defects
- Consistent size within lot

**Why Size Matters**:
- Larger beans often from older, established trees
- Better heat distribution during roasting
- Generally associated with higher altitudes
- Easier to roast evenly
- More developed flavor potential
- Premium pricing in commodity market

### Quality Indicators

**Premium Supremo Traits**:
- Uniformity of bean size (minimal variation)
- Absence of defects (quakers, insect damage, broken beans)
- Vibrant blue-green color (fresh green coffee)
- Sweet, clean aroma from green beans
- High density (sinks in water, doesn't float)
- Moisture content 10-12% (ideal for storage)

## üå± Varieties & Genetics

### Common Varieties in Supremo Coffee

**Caturra** (40-50% of production):
- Bourbon mutation discovered in Brazil, thriving in Colombia
- Compact plant, higher yield than traditional Typica
- Excellent cup quality when grown at altitude
- Sweet, balanced, classic Colombian profile
- Medium-high acidity, good body
- Susceptible to coffee leaf rust (requires care)

**Castillo** (30-40% of production):
- Colombian-developed hybrid (Caturra x Timor Hybrid)
- Created for leaf rust resistance
- Released by Cenicaf√© (Colombian Coffee Research)
- Good cup quality with disease resistance
- Slightly less complex than pure Caturra
- Becoming dominant due to climate pressures

**Colombia Variety** (10-20% of production):
- Earlier rust-resistant hybrid
- Predecessor to Castillo
- Good quality, reliable production
- Regional variations in flavor

**Less Common**:
- **Typica**: Original variety, declining due to low yield
- **Bourbon**: Present in some heritage farms
- **Gesha/Geisha**: Experimental micro-lots
- **F1 Hybrids**: Emerging in progressive farms

## üîÑ Processing Method

### Colombian Washed Processing

Colombia is famous for wet processing (washed method), which defines the classic Colombian flavor profile:

**Step-by-Step Process**:

1. **Selective Picking** (October-February main harvest, April-June mitaca)
   - Only ripe red cherries picked
   - Multiple passes through farm
   - Hand-picking on mountain slopes

2. **Pulping** (Within 6-12 hours of picking)
   - Mechanical removal of skin and most fruit
   - Disc pulpers common on Colombian farms
   - Mucilage remains on parchment

3. **Fermentation** (12-24 hours)
   - Tanks or fermentation channels
   - Natural enzymes break down mucilage
   - Water may be added (Colombian-style fermentation)
   - Temperature and altitude affect fermentation time
   - Monitored by touch/feel for mucilage removal

4. **Washing** (Thorough rinsing)
   - Channels or washing tanks
   - Running water removes fermented mucilage
   - Clean parchment coffee remains
   - Density separation (floaters removed)

5. **Drying** (10-15 days depending on method)
   - **Mechanical Dryers** (guardiolas): Common due to humidity
   - **Parabolic Dryers**: Solar-powered, increasingly used
   - **Raised Beds**: Some specialty producers
   - **Patio Drying**: Traditional but weather-dependent
   - Target: 10-12% moisture content

6. **Dry Milling**
   - Parchment removed (hulling)
   - Sorting by size (screen sizing for Supremo grade)
   - Density sorting
   - Color sorting (modern mills)
   - Hand-sorting for defects
   - Grading and classification

**Why Washed Processing?**:
- Colombia's humid climate makes natural processing challenging
- Washed method produces clean, bright, consistent flavors
- Highlights terroir and bean characteristics
- Reduces defects and fermentation issues
- International market preference for Colombian washed

**Flavor Impact**:
- Clean, bright acidity
- Clear flavor notes
- Balanced sweetness
- Medium to full body
- Less fruit-forward than natural process
- Classic, approachable profile

## üî• Roasting Recommendations

### Optimal Roast Levels

**Light Roast (City)**:
- Highlights acidity and origin character
- Floral and citrus notes prominent
- Bright, clean cup
- Best for pour-over methods
- Can be too acidic for some palates

**Medium Roast (Full City)** ‚≠ê **RECOMMENDED**:
- Sweet spot for Colombia Supremo
- Balanced acidity and sweetness
- Caramel and chocolate develop
- Body increases, acidity mellows
- Versatile for all brew methods
- Mass appeal profile

**Medium-Dark Roast (Vienna)**:
- Chocolate notes intensify
- Acidity subdued
- Fuller body, richer mouthfeel
- Good for espresso and milk drinks
- Traditional Colombian roast style

**Dark Roast (French)**:
- Not recommended for Supremo
- Masks origin characteristics
- Overpowers delicate balance
- Use lower grades for dark roasts

### Roasting Tips

**Development Considerations**:
- Supremo's size requires attention to heat application
- Larger beans need longer development time
- Risk of scorching exterior while under-developing interior
- Moderate airflow and heat ramp recommended
- First crack: Medium to loud, distinct
- Development time post-crack: 90-120 seconds ideal

**Target Profiles**:
- Light: Drop at first crack + 30-45 seconds
- Medium: Drop at first crack + 90-120 seconds  
- Medium-Dark: Drop approaching second crack

## üìö Historical & Cultural Context

### Juan Valdez & Colombian Coffee Marketing

Colombia Supremo's prominence is partly due to decades of brilliant marketing:

- **1959**: Colombian Coffee Growers Federation creates "Juan Valdez" character
- **Goal**: Differentiate Colombian coffee as premium product
- **Success**: Colombia becomes synonymous with quality coffee
- **Reality**: Supremo grade became international standard for Colombian coffee
- **Legacy**: One of history's most successful agricultural marketing campaigns

### Colombian Coffee Culture

**Domestic Consumption**:
- Colombians drink coffee throughout day
- Traditionally strong, sweet, small servings ("tinto")
- Coffee breaks are social rituals
- Growing specialty coffee culture in cities (Bogot√°, Medell√≠n)

**Coffee's Economic Importance**:
- 500,000+ coffee-growing families
- 2+ million people employed in coffee sector
- Coffee is cultural identity of many regions
- "Eje Cafetero" is UNESCO World Heritage site

**Starting Point Recipe** (Pour Over):

- Grind: Medium-fine (table salt consistency)

**Adjustments**:
- **Too acidic/sour**: Grind finer, increase temperature, extend time
- **Too bitter**: Grind coarser, lower temperature, reduce time

- **Too strong**: Reduce dose or use coarser grind

**Espresso Dialing**:
- Start: 18g in, 36g out, 28 seconds
- Medium roast works best
- Adjust grind in small increments
- Sweetness should dominate, not acidity or bitterness
