**Origin**: Ethiopia (Gesha Village, Bench Maji Zone)

## üìã Overview

Ethiopia Gesha represents the original source of the world's most expensive and celebrated coffee variety - Geisha. While Panama Geisha captured global attention and astronomical prices, Ethiopian Gesha is the authentic, wild-type version still growing in its birthplace forests. This is coffee at its most ethereal, complex, and expensive.

**What Makes Ethiopian Gesha Extraordinary**:
- **Original wild-type**: Genetic source of all Geisha coffee worldwide
- **Rarest specialty coffee**: Extremely limited production from Ethiopia
- **Unmatched complexity**: Floral, tea-like, layered beyond any other coffee
- **Historical significance**: Birthplace variety, discovered 1930s
- **Price**: Among world's most expensive coffees ($50-200+/lb roasted)
- **Terroir**: Ethiopian origin provides unique expression vs. Panama
- **Cultural**: Reconnects Geisha to its Ethiopian roots

**The Gesha/Geisha Name**:
- Named for Gesha village and forest in southwestern Ethiopia
- "Gesha" = original Ethiopian name
- "Geisha" = misspelling that stuck in Panama (no connection to Japanese geisha)
- Both names refer to same variety from Ethiopian origins
- Coffee world uses both interchangeably

## üåç Origin: Gesha Village & the Bench Maji Zone

### Geographic Location

**Bench Maji Zone, SNNPR (Southern Nations)**:
- **Location**: Southwestern Ethiopia, near South Sudan border
- **Gesha Village**: Remote mountain village, 1,900-2,100 MASL
- **Forest**: Wild coffee forests, pristine biodiversity
- **Access**: Extremely remote, difficult terrain
- **Discovery**: 1930s by British consul Richard Whalley
- **Status**: Some of last wild Gesha-type coffee forests

**Growing Environment**:
- **Altitude**: 1,900-2,100 meters (very high for Ethiopia)
- **Climate**: Cool, misty, cloud forest conditions
- **Temperature**: 15-22¬∞C (59-72¬∞F), cooler than other Ethiopian regions
- **Rainfall**: Heavy, consistent moisture from cloud cover
- **Soil**: Rich forest soil, deep organic matter
- **Canopy**: Shade-grown under native forest trees
- **Biodiversity**: Wild flora and fauna, pristine ecosystem

**Why This Terroir Matters**:
- Cool temperatures slow cherry maturation = complex sugars
- High altitude and stress = dense, flavorful beans
- Forest shade = gentle growing conditions
- Wild genetics = maximum genetic diversity
- Original habitat = authentic terroir expression

### Historical Context

**Discovery & Collection** (1931-1936):
- British consul Richard Whalley collects coffee samples near Gesha
- Seeds sent to research station in Kitale, Kenya
- Originally collected for leaf rust resistance (found in wild Gesha)
- Stored in germplasm collection, forgotten for decades

**Journey to Panama** (1950s-1960s):
- Seeds from Kenya germplasm sent to CATIE (Costa Rica research center)
- Distributed to Central American farms 1960s-1970s
- Hacienda La Esmeralda (Panama) plants Gesha 1963 on farm edges
- Ignored for 40 years, considered low-yielding and unremarkable

**Rediscovery & Fame** (2004):
- Hacienda La Esmeralda separates Gesha lot, enters competition
- **2004 Best of Panama**: Gesha wins, sells for record $21/lb
- Coffee world stunned by floral, tea-like, unprecedented flavor
- Subsequent years: Prices skyrocket ($50, $100, $350+/lb green)
- **2019 Record**: Panama Gesha sells for $1,029/lb (Elida Estate)

**Return to Ethiopia** (2007-present):
- **Adam Overton & Rachel Samuel**: Create Gesha Village Coffee Estate
- Purchase land near original Gesha forest (2007-2011)
- Collect wild Gesha genetics from surrounding forests
- Plant "Gesha 1931" (from original collection) alongside wild types
- First Gesha Village Estate lots sold 2012
- Reintroduces Ethiopian Gesha to specialty market
- Reconnects variety to its birthplace terroir

## üå± The Gesha Variety

### Botanical Characteristics

**Plant Appearance**:
- **Height**: Tall, often 3-5 meters (10-16 feet) - distinctively tall
- **Leaves**: Long, elongated, bronze-tipped when young
- **Internodes**: Wide spacing between branches (distinctive identifier)
- **Cherries**: Elongated, pointed shape
- **Beans**: Long, narrow, boat-shaped
- **Growth**: Slow, lanky, sprawling habit

**Agricultural Challenges**:
- **Yield**: Very low compared to modern varieties (30-50% of Caturra)
- **Management**: Tall plants difficult to harvest and prune
- **Disease**: Surprisingly resistant to coffee leaf rust (why originally collected)
- **Spacing**: Requires more space due to size
- **Wind**: Susceptible to wind damage (tall, thin branches)
- **Economics**: Only viable at ultra-premium specialty prices

**Why Farmers Grow It Despite Challenges**:
- **Price**: $50-200+/lb roasted justifies low yields
- **Recognition**: Cachet of Gesha name
- **Quality**: Unmatched cup quality when grown well
- **Niche**: Ultra-premium specialty market demand

### Genetic Diversity

**Ethiopian Wild-Type Gesha** vs. **Panama Geisha (1931 Collection)**:

**Ethiopian Gesha (Wild-type)**:
- Diverse genetics from multiple wild trees
- **Population, not single clone**
- Continuous evolution in native habitat
- Varying plant morphology and cup profiles
- Maximum genetic diversity
- Represents natural Gesha expression
- Adaptable, resilient

**Panama Geisha (1931 Single Selection)**:
- **Genetic bottleneck**: From limited original seed collection
- Single line propagated for 90+ years
- Uniform plant characteristics
- Consistent (but narrow) cup profile
- Selected for specific flavors over decades in Panama terroir
- Less genetic diversity
- Famous "Panama Geisha" flavor profile

**Key Insight**: Ethiopian Gesha is more variable, Panama Geisha more consistent. Ethiopian offers terroir authenticity and genetic breadth; Panama offers refined, perfected expression of single genetic line.

## üîÑ Processing Methods

### Washed Processing (Primary Method) ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê

**Why Washed Dominates Gesha**:
- Highlights delicate floral and tea-like characteristics
- Extreme clarity of variety expression
- Clean, precise flavor articulation
- Traditional Ethiopian washed processing excellence
- Best method for showcasing Gesha's unique attributes

**Process**:
1. **Selective Hand-Picking**: Only fully ripe cherries
2. **Depulping**: Within hours of harvest
3. **Fermentation**: 24-36 hours (altitude and temperature dependent)
4. **Washing**: Multiple rinses, clean water
5. **Drying**: Raised African beds, 12-18 days
6. **Sorting**: Extensive hand-sorting, defect removal

**Flavor Impact**:
- **Floral**: Jasmine, orange blossom, bergamot, tea-like florality
- **Clarity**: Crystal-clear flavor articulation
- **Acidity**: Very high, vibrant, citric and malic acids
- **Body**: Light to medium, tea-like, delicate
- **Complexity**: Layered, evolving, multidimensional
- **Finish**: Long, floral, sweet, persistent

### Natural Processing (Emerging Specialty) ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê

**Recent Innovation**:
- Less common than washed, but increasing
- Gesha Village Estate and specialty producers experimenting
- Commands even higher prices (rarity + complexity)

**Process**:
- Whole cherry dried on raised beds
- 20-30 days drying time
- Careful monitoring to prevent defects
- Slow, controlled drying for quality

**Flavor Impact**:
- **Fruit**: Berry, tropical fruit, wine-like
- **Floral**: Jasmine, perfume, intense
- **Body**: Fuller than washed, syrupy
- **Complexity**: Extreme, overwhelming for some
- **Sweetness**: Very high, fruit-driven
- **Polarizing**: Some love, some find too intense

### Honey/Pulped Natural (Rare, Experimental)

- Experimental lots only
- Between washed and natural in character
- Very rare on market

## üî• Roasting Ethiopian Gesha

### Optimal Roast Levels

**Light Roast (City, City+)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **ESSENTIAL**
- **Only recommended roast for Ethiopian Gesha**
- Preserves delicate floral notes
- Maximizes complexity
- Highlights terroir and variety
- Bright acidity fully expressed
- **Any darker = waste of expensive coffee**

**Medium Roast (Full City)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Not recommended**
- Loses floral character
- Jasmine diminishes
- Acidity muted
- Defeats purpose of Gesha
- Save your money if roasting medium

**Medium-Dark or Dark** ‚≠ê **Never**
- Absolute waste of Gesha
- All unique characteristics destroyed
- Might as well use commodity coffee
- Roast flavors dominate completely
- Professional roasters will judge you

### Roasting Gesha: Challenges & Techniques

**Unique Roasting Characteristics**:
- **Low Density** (despite high altitude):
  - Long, narrow beans = lower density than expected
  - Absorbs heat quickly
  - Can scorch easily
- **Delicate Flavor Compounds**:
  - Floral compounds volatile, easily destroyed
  - Narrow roast window for optimal expression
  - Easy to over-roast even slightly
- **High Price = High Pressure**:
  - Every roast must be perfect (expensive mistakes)

**Recommended Light Roast Profile**:
1. **Charge Temperature**: 380-395¬∞F (193-202¬∞C) - moderate
2. **Drying Phase**: Gentle, 5-6 minutes to yellow
3. **Browning**: Moderate heat increase, 3-4 minutes to first crack
4. **First Crack**: 9-11 minutes total time
5. **Development**: **30-60 seconds post-crack only**
   - Extremely short development
   - Drop just into or very early in first crack
   - Preserves floral compounds
6. **Drop Temperature**: 400-408¬∞F (204-209¬∞C)
   - City to City+ roast level
   - Light brown color
   - No second crack approach

**Critical Tips**:
- **Less is more**: Under-develop rather than over-develop
- **Sample roast first**: Learn the coffee before committing
- **Small batches**: Easier control, less financial risk
- **Cool immediately**: Rapid cooling preserves florals
- **Rest 3-7 days**: Gesha needs rest but not too long
- **Seal and protect**: Oxidation destroys floral notes quickly

**Common Mistakes**:
- **Over-developing**: Most common error, ruins Gesha
- **Too much heat**: Scorching delicate beans
- **Too dark**: Even Full City is too dark for many Geshas
- **Rushing**: Needs proper time despite light roast
- **Stale roasting**: Old green coffee = diminished florals

## üçµ Brewing Ethiopian Gesha

### Best Brewing Methods

**Pour Over (V60, Kalita Wave)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **IDEAL**
- **The only method for Gesha**
- Highlights delicate florals and complexity
- Clean, precise extraction
- **Ratio**: 1:16 to 1:17 (e.g., 15g : 250g)
- **Grind**: Medium-fine (slightly coarser than standard)
- **Water**: 200-205¬∞F (93-96¬∞C)
- **Time**: 2:30-3:30 total
- **Water Quality Critical**: Use excellent water, Gesha shows impurities
- **Result**: Floral, tea-like, clean, everything Gesha should be

**Chemex** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Excellent**: Thick paper filter enhances clarity
- Similar parameters to V60
- Even cleaner cup than V60
- Reduces any oils that might mask florals
- **Result**: Crystal clear, delicate, refined

**Aeropress** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Can work** but not ideal
- Use inverted method, gentle pressure
- Short steep time (1:00-1:30)
- Can produce good results but less clarity than pour-over
- **Result**: Clean but less delicate than pour-over

**French Press** ‚≠ê‚≠ê **Not Recommended**
- Body too heavy for Gesha's delicate profile
- Oils and fines muddy clarity
- Florals get lost
- **Verdict**: Save Gesha for pour-over

**Espresso** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê (Controversial)
- **Divisive**: Some love, many consider wasteful
- Requires very light espresso roast
- Parameters: 18g in, 45-50g out (1:2.5+), 25-30s
- Temperature: 200-202¬∞F
- **Result**: Intense florals, syrupy body, complex BUT...
- **Arguments Against**:
  - Wastes Gesha's delicacy
  - Costs $5-10+ per shot
  - Pour-over shows Gesha better
- **Arguments For**:
  - Incredible when done right
  - Concentrated florals unforgettable
  - Unique experience
- **Verdict**: Try once if you can afford it, but pour-over is better value

**Cold Brew** ‚≠ê **Don't Even Think About It**
- Complete waste of Gesha
- Loses all florals
- Might as well use cheap coffee
- **Never cold brew Gesha**

### Brewing Tips for Gesha

**Water Quality** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê:
- **Critical**: Gesha shows water impurities more than any coffee
- Use filtered or remineralized water
- **TDS**: 80-120 ppm ideal
- **Hardness**: Low to moderate (50-80 ppm)
- Avoid distilled (under-extracts, flat)
- Avoid tap water (unless excellent quality)
- Third Wave Water packets excellent for Gesha

**Grind Size**:
- Slightly coarser than normal pour-over
- Avoid fines (muddy delicate flavors)
- Burr grinder essential (never blade)
- Consider hand grinder for best quality (e.g., Comandante)

**Temperature**:
- **200-205¬∞F** (93-96¬∞C) optimal
- Hotter = better extraction of complex compounds
- Cooler if acidity too intense for your palate
- Don't go below 195¬∞F (91¬∞C)

**Brew Time**:
- Not too fast (under-extraction = sour, thin)
- Not too slow (over-extraction = bitter, harsh)
- **Sweet spot**: 2:45-3:15 for most pour-overs

**Tasting Notes**:
- Let coffee cool to room temperature
- Gesha transforms as it cools (dramatic evolution)
- Best flavors often emerge at 140-160¬∞F (60-71¬∞C)
- Cold Gesha still delicious (unlike most coffee)
- Taste at multiple temperatures for full experience
