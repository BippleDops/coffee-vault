## üìã Overview

Kenya SL-28 represents one of coffee's most distinctive and celebrated varieties, producing intensely bright, berry-forward, wine-like coffees that define the Kenyan specialty coffee experience. Developed in the 1930s specifically for Kenyan conditions, SL-28 has become synonymous with the explosive, complex acidity and fruit-forward character that Kenya is famous for worldwide.

**What Makes SL-28 Special**:
- **Signature flavor**: Blackcurrant, berry, wine-like acidity - instantly recognizable
- **Kenyan terroir**: Bred specifically for Kenyan volcanic soil and altitude
- **Brightness**: Among highest acidity coffees in the world
- **Complexity**: Layered, dynamic, evolving flavor profile
- **Body**: Surprisingly full-bodied despite bright acidity
- **Clean cup**: Kenyan processing creates exceptional clarity
- **Premium**: Commands top specialty prices globally
- **Benchmark**: Standard for judging quality in Kenya

**The "SL" Name**:
- **SL** = Scott Laboratories (now Scott Agricultural Laboratories)
- British-owned coffee research lab in Kenya (1930s)
- Commissioned by Kenyan government to develop high-yield, quality varieties
- Selected coffee from Tanganyika (Tanzania) via R√©union Island
- **SL-28** = Selection #28 from the program
- Bourbon lineage, possibly with some Mokha (Yemeni) genetics
- Released for cultivation 1930s-1940s
- Became dominant quality variety in Kenya

## üåç Geographic Origin & Terroir

### Kenyan Coffee-Growing Regions

SL-28 thrives across Kenya's high-altitude volcanic regions:

**Nyeri County** (Central Highlands):
- **Altitude**: 1,500-2,100 MASL
- **Soil**: Rich red volcanic soil from Mount Kenya
- **Character**: Most famous SL-28 region, classic blackcurrant profile
- **Washing Stations**: Multiple renowned factories (Gaturiri, Kagumo, many others)
- **Recognition**: Globally renowned, competition-winning region

**Kirinyaga County** (Mount Kenya Slopes):
- **Altitude**: 1,500-1,900 MASL  
- **Soil**: Volcanic, highly fertile
- **Character**: Bright, complex, berry-forward, intense
- **Famous Factories**: Karatina, Ngariama, others
- **Quality**: Rivals Nyeri for SL-28 excellence

**Murang'a County**:
- **Altitude**: 1,450-1,900 MASL
- **Soil**: Red volcanic loam
- **Character**: Balanced, sweet, bright acidity
- **Recognition**: Traditional coffee region, high quality

**Kiambu County** (Near Nairobi):
- **Altitude**: 1,500-1,800 MASL
- **Character**: Clean, bright, complex
- **History**: Historic coffee region, close to capital

**Embu & Meru** (Eastern Mount Kenya):
- **Altitude**: 1,300-1,700 MASL
- **Character**: Fruity, wine-like, lower acidity than Nyeri
- **Emerging**: Growing specialty recognition

**Nakuru, Kericho** (Rift Valley):
- **Altitude**: 1,850-2,100 MASL (very high)
- **Character**: Extremely bright, vibrant, complex
- **Status**: Smaller production, boutique quality

### Why Kenyan Terroir is Unique

**Volcanic Soil**:
- Mount Kenya volcanic slopes
- Red volcanic loam, extremely fertile
- High phosphorus content (contributes to acidity)
- Excellent drainage
- Natural nutrients reduce fertilizer needs

**Altitude**:
- **1,500-2,100 MASL**: Ideal SL-28 range
- Cool temperatures slow maturation
- Dense beans from slow growth
- Intense diurnal temperature variation (10-15¬∞C swings)
- Temperature stress enhances complexity

**Climate**:
- **Two rainy seasons**: Long rains (March-May), short rains (October-December)
- **Two harvest seasons**: Main (October-December), Fly crop (June-July)
- **Equatorial location**: Consistent daylight hours year-round
- **Cool highlands**: 13-26¬∞C (55-79¬∞F) in coffee zones

## üå± The SL-28 Variety

### Genetic Background

**Origins & Development**:
- **1930s**: Scott Laboratories begins selection program
- **Parent Material**: Bourbon variety from Tanganyika (now Tanzania)
- **Possible Genetics**: Some believe Mokha (Yemeni) genetics mixed with Bourbon
- **Selection Criteria**: 
  - Drought resistance
  - High cup quality
  - Adaptability to Kenyan conditions
  - Disease resistance
  - Yield potential
- **Release**: 1930s-1940s to Kenyan farmers
- **Adoption**: Became dominant alongside SL-34

**Genetic Relationship**:
- **SL-28** and **SL-34** are siblings from the same selection program
- SL-28 selected from Drought-Resistant Coffee from Tanganyika
- SL-34 selected from Loresho Estate French Mission Bourbon
- Both are Bourbon-type varieties

### Plant Characteristics

**Morphology**:
- **Height**: Tall, 3-4 meters typical
- **Leaves**: Bronze-tipped when young (Bourbon characteristic)
- **Branching**: Medium density, relatively open
- **Cherries**: Medium to large, red when ripe
- **Beans**: Medium to large, elongated
- **Roots**: Deep, drought-tolerant

**Agricultural Traits**:
- **Yield**: Low to moderate (30-40% less than Ruiru 11 or Batian)
- **Spacing**: Wide spacing required (tall plants)
- **Disease**: Susceptible to coffee berry disease and leaf rust
- **Drought**: Excellent drought tolerance (original selection criterion)
- **Longevity**: Long-lived, productive 20-30+ years
- **Management**: Requires pruning, disease management

**Why Farmers Continue Growing SL-28 Despite Challenges**:
1. **Price premium**: 20-50% premium over other varieties
2. **Cup quality**: Unmatched for Kenyan character
3. **Recognition**: Buyers specifically seek SL-28
4. **Auction system**: SL-28 tops Nairobi Coffee Exchange prices
5. **Reputation**: Kenyan coffee fame built on SL-28
6. **Competition**: SL-28 wins Kenyan and international competitions

## üîÑ Kenyan Processing: Double Fermentation

### Why Kenyan Processing is Unique

Kenya's washed processing is more intensive than anywhere else:

**Standard Washed** (Other Countries):
- Depulp ‚Üí Ferment 12-24 hours ‚Üí Wash ‚Üí Dry

**Kenyan Double Fermentation**:
- Depulp ‚Üí Ferment #1 (12-24 hrs) ‚Üí Wash ‚Üí Ferment #2 (12-24 hrs) ‚Üí Final wash ‚Üí Soak ‚Üí Dry

**The Process** (Step-by-Step):

1. **Harvest** (Selective Hand-Picking):
   - Only ripe red cherries
   - Multiple passes during season
   - Delivered to washing station (factory) same day

2. **Reception & Sorting**:
   - Floatation tanks remove unripe/defective cherries
   - Grading by ripeness and size

3. **Depulping**:
   - Mechanical removal of skin and some fruit
   - Disc pulpers typical
   - Mucilage remains on parchment

4. **First Fermentation** (Dry Fermentation):
   - **12-24 hours** in fermentation tanks (no water added)
   - Natural enzymes break down outer mucilage layer
   - Altitude/temperature determines duration
   - Cooler highlands = longer fermentation
   - Monitored by touch/visual inspection

5. **First Washing**:
   - Beans washed in channels with fresh water
   - Removes loosened mucilage
   - Density sorting (floaters removed)

6. **Second Fermentation** (Dry or Wet):
   - **12-24 additional hours**
   - Removes remaining mucilage
   - Further flavor development
   - Critical for Kenyan brightness

7. **Final Washing**:
   - Thorough washing in clean water channels
   - Multiple rinses
   - Complete mucilage removal
   - Density sorting again

8. **Soaking** (Optional but Common):
   - 12-24 hour soak in clean water
   - Clarifies final cup, enhances brightness
   - Softens parchment for easier drying

9. **Drying**:
   - **Raised African drying beds** (standard in Kenya)
     - Mesh screens for airflow
     - 10-21 days depending on weather
     - Covered at night and during rain
     - Raked regularly for even drying
   - **Target moisture**: 10-12%
   - **Color**: Pale green-blue parchment when properly dried

10. **Dry Milling**:
    - Hulling (parchment removed)
    - Grading by size (AA, AB, etc.)
    - Density sorting (catador machine)
    - Hand sorting for defects
    - Preparation for auction or direct sale

**Impact on Flavor**:
- **Extreme Clarity**: Double fermentation creates exceptional cleanliness
- **Bright Acidity**: Processing enhances natural acidity dramatically
- **Complexity**: Extended fermentation develops flavor precursors
- **Clean Cup**: No defects, no off-flavors
- **Consistency**: Rigorous process ensures batch-to-batch uniformity

### The Famous "Kenyan Blackcurrant"

**What is it?**:
- Signature flavor note in Kenya SL-28
- Tastes like Ribena (blackcurrant cordial/juice)
- Blackcurrant jam, cassis, dark berry
- Instantly recognizable to those familiar
- **Not present in all Kenyan coffee** (SL-28/SL-34 specific)

**Why Does SL-28 Taste Like Blackcurrant?**:
- **Genetics**: SL-28 variety predisposition
- **Terroir**: Volcanic soil, high altitude, Kenyan climate
- **Processing**: Double fermentation accentuates it
- **Compounds**: Specific flavor compounds unique to SL-28
- **Combination**: All factors together create this signature

**Love it or Hate it**:
- Coffee enthusiasts: Often love it, seek it out
- New to specialty: Can be jarring, too intense
- Acquired taste: Often grows on people over time

## üî• Roasting Kenya SL-28

### Optimal Roast Levels

**Light Roast (City)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **RECOMMENDED**
- **Highlights**: Blackcurrant, berry, complexity, bright acidity
- **Best for**: Pour-over, showcasing SL-28 character
- **Result**: Explosive fruit, vibrant, clean, complex
- **Target**: Drop 30-60 seconds into first crack
- **Color**: Light brown, no oil

**Light-Medium (City+)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **ALSO EXCELLENT**
- **Balance**: Fruit + developing sweetness
- **Acidity**: Still very high but slightly mellowed
- **Body**: Fuller than pure light

- **Target**: Drop 60-90 seconds into first crack

**Medium (Full City)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê

- **Berry**: Still present but less intense
- **Body**: Full, syrupy
- **Use**: Espresso, those seeking less acidity
- **Result**: Still good, but some SL-28 magic lost

**Medium-Dark (Vienna)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Not Recommended**: Origin character significantly diminished
- Roast flavors begin dominating
- Blackcurrant mostly gone
- Use only if requested or blending

**Dark Roast** ‚≠ê **Never**

- All unique characteristics destroyed
- Might as well use commodity coffee

### Roasting Technique

**SL-28 Roasting Characteristics**:
- **Density**: Very high (slow growth at altitude)
- **Heat Absorption**: Requires good heat application
- **Drying Phase**: Longer than average (dense beans)
- **Development**: Short development preserves brightness
- **Challenge**: Balancing development with preserving acidity

**Recommended Profile**:
1. **Charge**: 390-410¬∞F (199-210¬∞C)
2. **Drying**: 5-6 minutes to yellow (dense beans need time)
3. **Browning**: 4-5 minutes to first crack
4. **First Crack**: 10-12 minutes total
5. **Development**: **30-90 seconds** (shorter = brighter)
6. **Drop Temp**: 
   - Light: 405-410¬∞F (207-210¬∞C)
   - Light-Medium: 410-418¬∞F (210-214¬∞C)
   - Medium: 418-425¬∞F (214-218¬∞C)

**Tips**:
- **Don't rush**: Dense beans need time to develop evenly
- **Watch for tipping**: High heat can scorch dense beans
- **Preserve acids**: Short development time critical
- **Cool quickly**: Rapid cooling locks in brightness
- **Rest**: 3-10 days rest optimal for SL-28

## üçµ Brewing Kenya SL-28

### Best Brewing Methods

**Pour Over (V60, Kalita)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **PERFECT**
- **Ideal**: Showcases SL-28's brightness and complexity
- **Ratio**: 1:16 to 1:17 (15-20g coffee : 240-340g water)
- **Grind**: Medium-fine
- **Water**: 200-205¬∞F (93-96¬∞C) - hot water for bright extraction
- **Time**: 2:30-3:30 total
- **Why Perfect**: Clean extraction, full flavor expression, clarity
- **Result**: Explosive blackcurrant, berry, complexity

**Chemex** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Excellent**: Thick filter creates extra clarity
- Same parameters as V60
- Even cleaner cup
- **Result**: Pristine, bright, detailed

**Espresso** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê (Challenging but Rewarding)
- **Dose**: 18-20g
- **Yield**: 40-50g out (1:2.2 to 1:2.5 ratio - longer for acidity)
- **Time**: 25-32 seconds
- **Temp**: 200-203¬∞F (93-95¬∞C)
- **Grind**: Fine
- **Result**: Intense berry bomb, syrupy body, complex
- **Challenge**: High acidity can be overwhelming in concentrated form
- **Best**: Light-medium roast, longer ratios
- **Milk Drinks**: Incredible in cappuccino (berry + milk = magic)

**Aeropress** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Ratio**: 1:15 (17g : 255g)
- **Method**: Inverted or standard
- **Water**: 200¬∞F
- **Time**: 1:30-2:00
- **Result**: Clean, bright, less complexity than pour-over but very good

**French Press** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Works** but not ideal
- Body excellent, but oils can muddy brightness
- **Ratio**: 1:15
- **Steep**: 4:00
- **Result**: Full-bodied, softer acidity, less clarity

**Cold Brew** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Surprising**: Loses acidity (good or bad depending on preference)
- Berry flavors persist
- Smooth, sweet
- Not traditional but pleasant

### Brewing Tips

**Water Quality**:
- Kenya shows water impurities
- Use filtered or remineralized water
- **TDS**: 80-150 ppm
- Avoid hard water (dulls acidity)

**Extraction**:
- **Higher extraction** works well (Kenya is forgiving)
- Don't under-extract (sour, thin)
- Aim for 20-22% extraction yield
- **TDS Target**: 1.30-1.50% for filter coffee

**Temperature**:
- **Hot water** essential (200-205¬∞F)
- Kenyan acidity needs heat to balance with sweetness
- Too cool = sour and unbalanced

**Tasting**:
- **Taste as it cools**: SL-28 transforms from hot to warm to cool
- Best flavors often at 150-170¬∞F (65-77¬∞C)
- Still delicious at room temperature
