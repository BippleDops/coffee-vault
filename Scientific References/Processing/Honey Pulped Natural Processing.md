# Honey and Pulped Natural Processing

## Overview

Honey processing (also called pulped natural) is a hybrid method combining aspects of washed and natural processing. It creates unique flavor profiles between the two extremes and offers processing flexibility for producers.

## What is Honey Processing?

### Definition

**Honey Processing**:
- Cherry skin/pulp removed (like washed)
- Mucilage left on bean during drying (unlike washed)
- Dried with sticky mucilage layer intact
- Name from sticky, honey-like mucilage (not actual honey)

**Mucilage**:
- Sweet, sticky layer surrounding parchment
- Contains sugars, pectins, proteins
- Creates unique flavors during drying
- Variable removal levels create processing variations

## Processing Categories

### Honey Color Classifications

**White Honey**:
- 80-90% mucilage removed
- Closest to washed
- Light, clean profile
- Shortest drying time

**Yellow Honey**:
- 50-75% mucilage removed
- Moderate body and sweetness
- Balanced characteristics
- Most common honey type

**Red Honey**:
- 25-50% mucilage removed
- Fuller body
- More complex
- Sweeter than yellow

**Black Honey**:
- 0-25% mucilage removed
- Almost like natural
- Very fruity, heavy body
- Longest drying time
- Most complex

**Gold Honey** (less common):
- Between yellow and red
- Regional variation
- Not standardized

### Regional Naming Variations

**Central America**:
- Honey processing terminology standard
- Color-based classification
- Well-defined categories

**Costa Rica**:
- Pioneered modern honey processing
- Most refined categorization
- Quality focus

**Other Regions**:
- "Pulped natural" (Brazil)
- "Semi-washed" (some regions)
- Terminology less standardized

## Process Steps

### Step 1: Harvesting

**Requirement**:
- Ripe cherry selection
- Same as washed processing
- Selective picking preferred
- Ripeness critical

### Step 2: Depulping

**Mechanical Removal**:
- Machine removes skin and pulp
- Leaves parchment and mucilage
- Pressure-controlled for mucilage retention
- Different settings for honey levels

**Mucilage Retention**:
- Adjustable by depulper settings
- Water amount used
- Pressure level
- Equipment type

### Step 3: Drying with Mucilage

**Drying Process**:
- Spread on raised beds or patios
- Sticky mucilage makes handling difficult
- Requires careful monitoring
- Must turn regularly

**Duration by Type**:
- White honey: 8-10 days
- Yellow honey: 10-15 days
- Red honey: 15-20 days
- Black honey: 20-25 days

**Challenges**:
- Stickiness makes turning hard
- Fermentation risk (sugar in mucilage)
- Mold risk if not monitored
- Requires more attention than washed

### Step 4: Hulling

**Removing Parchment**:
- Once dried to 10-12% moisture
- Mechanical hulling
- Similar to other processes
- Dried mucilage comes off with parchment

## Flavor Characteristics

### Honey vs Other Methods

**vs Washed**:
- More body (from mucilage sugars)
- More sweetness
- Less bright acidity
- More complex

**vs Natural**:
- Cleaner than natural
- Less fruity/funky
- More consistent
- Lighter body than natural

**Unique Profile**:
- Balance between methods
- Sweet and complex
- Creamy mouthfeel
- Moderate acidity

### Flavor Development

**Mucilage Contribution**:
- Sugars absorbed during drying
- Creates sweetness in cup
- Adds body and viscosity
- Develops caramel notes

**Fermentation**:
- Controlled, moderate fermentation
- Develops complexity
- Creates unique flavor compounds
- Less wild than natural

## Advantages and Disadvantages

### Advantages

**For Producers**:
- Less water use than washed (80-90% reduction)
- Environmental benefits
- Can create premium product
- Price premium over washed

**Quality Benefits**:
- Unique flavor profile
- Marketable as specialty
- Consistent with skill
- Sweetness enhancement

### Disadvantages

**Challenges**:
- Labor intensive (turning sticky beans)
- Fermentation risk (mucilage sugars)
- Weather dependent (like natural)
- Requires skill and monitoring

**Infrastructure**:
- Raised beds preferred (airflow)
- Depulping equipment needed
- Drying space required
- Moisture measurement tools

## Quality Control

### Critical Control Points

**Mucilage Retention**:
- Consistent depulper settings
- Target mucilage percentage
- Quality check after depulping

**Drying Monitoring**:
- Regular moisture checks
- Visual inspection
- Smell testing (detect fermentation)
- Temperature monitoring

**Turning Schedule**:
- First 3 days: Multiple times daily
- Days 4-7: 2-3 times daily
- Week 2+: Once daily minimum
- Adjust based on weather

### Defects to Avoid

**Over-Fermentation**:
- Alcoholic, vinegary notes
- Excessive sourness
- Unpleasant flavors

**Under-Drying**:
- Mold growth
- Storage issues
- Quality degradation

**Inconsistent Drying**:
- Variable moisture
- Mixed flavors
- Quality inconsistency

## Roasting Honey Processed Coffee

### Characteristics

**Bean Density**:
- Between washed and natural
- Medium density
- Roast at moderate rate

**Roast Approach**:
- Preserve sweetness
- Don't over-develop
- Gentle heat application
- Medium roast ideal

**Target**:
- Bring out caramel notes
- Preserve fruit character
- Balance acidity and sweetness
- Avoid excessive bitterness

## Brewing Recommendations

### Best Methods

**Pour-Over**:
- V60, Kalita Wave
- Highlights sweetness
- Good balance
- Clean cup

**Espresso**:
- Excellent for honey
- Caramel sweetness
- Creamy body
- Complex shots

**Chemex**:
- Filters some heaviness
- Clean, sweet cup
- Preserves character

### Brewing Parameters

**Temperature**:
- 92-94Â°C typical
- Slightly lower than washed
- Preserves sweetness

**Ratio**:
- 1:15 to 1:17
- Honey tolerates variation
- Adjust to taste

**Time**:
- Standard for method
- Don't over-extract
- Sweetness can turn bitter

## Economic Considerations

### Cost Comparison

**vs Washed**:
- Lower water costs (major savings)
- Higher labor (sticky beans, more turning)
- Similar infrastructure
- Net: Slightly lower cost

**vs Natural**:
- Higher labor (sticky handling)
- Faster drying (less weather risk)
- More consistent quality
- Net: Slightly higher cost

**Premium Pricing**:
- Specialty market values honey
- Can command 10-30% premium
- Depends on quality and marketing
- Growing demand

## Environmental Impact

### Water Conservation

**Comparison**:
- Washed: 40-100 liters per kg green coffee
- Honey: 5-15 liters per kg
- Natural: 0-2 liters per kg

**Sustainability**:
- Significant water savings vs washed
- Lower wastewater generation
- Reduced environmental impact
- Attractive in water-scarce regions

### Waste Management

**By-products**:
- Pulp can be composted
- Less wastewater than washed
- Mucilage dries with bean (no separate waste)
