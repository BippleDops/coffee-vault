# Carbonic Maceration in Coffee Processing

## Overview

Carbonic maceration, borrowed from wine-making, is an ultra-premium coffee processing technique creating exceptionally unique flavor profiles through intracellular fermentation in CO2-rich environments.

## Wine-Making Origins

### Beaujolais Nouveau Method

**Original Application**:
- French wine technique
- Whole-berry fermentation
- CO2 atmosphere
- Creates fruity, vibrant wines

**Adaptation to Coffee**:
- 2015+: Experimental coffee processing
- Colombian pioneers
- Specialty micro-lots
- Ultra-premium positioning

## The Process

### Step 1: Cherry Selection

**Critical**:
- Only perfect, ripe cherries
- Zero defects
- Uniform ripeness
- Hand-sorted meticulously

### Step 2: Sealed Container

**Setup**:
- Food-grade sealed tank
- Cherries loaded whole
- Minimal crushing/damage
- One-way valve for CO2 release

### Step 3: CO2 Introduction

**Methods**:

**Passive** (Most Common):
- Cherries naturally produce CO2
- Anaerobic environment develops
- Takes hours to establish
- Natural process

**Active** (Advanced):
- Inject CO2 gas
- Immediate anaerobic environment
- More control
- Faster process

### Step 4: Intracellular Fermentation

**Unique Process**:
- Fermentation inside intact cherry
- No microbial fermentation initially
- Enzymatic breakdown
- Cell metabolism

**Duration**: 48-200+ hours
**Temperature**: 18-22°C ideal
**Monitoring**: pH, CO2, temperature

### Step 5: Post-Processing

**After CM Period**:
- Remove cherries
- Depulp or continue as natural
- Wash or dry as intended
- Final drying to 10-12%

## Chemistry and Flavor Development

### Intracellular Fermentation

**What Happens**:
- Enzymatic activity inside cherry
- Without oxygen
- Creates unique esters
- Develops specific aromatics

**Compounds Created**:
- Fruity esters (intense)
- Floral compounds
- Wine-like characteristics
- Complex aromatics

### Flavor Characteristics

**Typical Profile**:
- Intense fruit (strawberry, raspberry common)
- Floral (rose, jasmine)
- Wine-like complexity
- Candy/confection notes
- Exceptional sweetness
- Clean acidity

**Compared to Anaerobic**:
- More refined
- Less funky
- Fruit-forward
- Cleaner profile

## Variations

### CM + Washed

**Process**:
- Carbonic maceration period
- Then depulp and wash
- Clean flavor development

**Result**:
- Clean, bright
- Fruity from CM
- Transparent from washing
- Balanced

### CM + Natural

**Process**:
- Carbonic maceration
- Then dry intact
- Layered fermentation

**Result**:
- Extremely fruity
- Complex, layered
- Heavy body
- Intense character

### CM + Honey

**Process**:
- Carbonic maceration
- Depulp, leave mucilage
- Dry with mucilage

**Result**:
- Sweet and fruity
- Balanced body
- Complexity from CM
- Sweetness from honey

## Quality Control

### Critical Parameters

**Temperature**:
- 18-22°C optimal
- Higher = faster, riskier
- Lower = slower, more control
- Consistency essential

**Duration**:
- Recipe-specific
- 72-120 hours common
- Longer = more intense
- Risk vs reward

**CO2 Management**:
- Must release pressure
- One-way valves essential
- Pressure buildup dangerous
- Monitoring critical

### Risks

**Over-Processing**:
- Alcoholic notes
- Excessive fermentation
- Unpleasant funk
- Vinegar character

**Contamination**:
- Wild yeasts/bacteria
- Moldy fruit
- Off-flavors
- Batch loss

## Roasting Carbonic Maceration Coffee

### Approach

**Roast Level**: Light preferred
- Preserve complexity
- Don't mask CM character
- Highlight fruit and floral
- Minimal development

**Development**:
- Gentle development
- Don't overdo
- Preserve delicacy
- Clean roast

## Brewing Recommendations

**Best Methods**:
- Pour-over (V60, Kalita)
- Chemex
- Aeropress

**Parameters**:
- 90-93°C
- 1:16-1:17 ratio
- Careful extraction
