## üìã Overview

Mexico represents a unique position in coffee geography - North America's only significant coffee producer, with rich indigenous heritage, extensive organic production, and proximity to the massive US coffee market. Despite challenges from climate change and economic pressures, Mexico continues producing distinctive, high-quality coffees with chocolate-forward, balanced profiles and exceptional organic/shade-grown credentials.

**Why Mexican Coffee Matters**:
- **Organic Leader**: Largest organic coffee producer globally (>25% of production organic)
- **Shade-Grown Champion**: Extensive agroforestry systems, biodiversity protection
- **Indigenous Heritage**: Many indigenous communities depend on coffee (Maya, Mixtec, Zapotec)
- **Fair Trade**: Strong cooperative structure, fair trade certifications common
- **Proximity**: Close to US market, fresh coffee readily available
- **Diversity**: Three distinct primary growing regions (Chiapas, Oaxaca, Veracruz)
- **Tradition**: Coffee cultivation since 1700s, rich cultural integration
- **Quality Potential**: High-altitude zones produce exceptional specialty coffee

**Historical Significance**:
- **1790s**: Coffee introduced to Veracruz from Caribbean
- **1800s**: Expansion to southern states (Chiapas, Oaxaca)
- **Early 1900s**: Became major producer
- **1973**: INMECAFE (Mexican Coffee Institute) created, supported smallholders
- **1989**: INMECAFE dissolved, price crisis followed
- **1990s-2000s**: Organic and fair trade movement emergence
- **2012-2014**: Coffee Rust (La Roya) epidemic, 40% production loss
- **Present**: Recovery, specialty focus, climate adaptation

**Current Challenges**:
- Coffee rust ongoing threat
- Climate change affecting growing zones
- Migration to US reducing labor force
- Price volatility, low farmgate prices
- Competition from Central/South American origins
- Aging farmer population

**Positive Trends**:
- Growing specialty recognition
- Organic and sustainability premiums
- Direct trade relationships increasing
- Younger generation returning to coffee (some)
- Innovation in processing (honey, natural, experimental)
- Quality-focused cooperatives thriving

## üìç Geography & Climate

### Major Coffee-Growing States

**Chiapas** (45-50% of Mexican production):
- **Location**: Far southern Mexico, bordering Guatemala
- **Altitude**: 900-1,700 MASL
- **Production**: Largest coffee-producing state
- **Regions**: Soconusco (coastal mountains), Sierra Madre, Jaltenango
- **Character**: Balanced, chocolate-forward, nutty, caramel

**Oaxaca** (25-30% of production):
- **Location**: Southern Mexico, Pacific Coast mountains
- **Altitude**: 900-1,600 MASL
- **Production**: Second-largest, quality-focused
- **Regions**: Pluma Hidalgo, Sierra Norte, Mixteca
- **Character**: Nutty, chocolatey, balanced, mild fruit

**Veracruz** (20-25% of production):
- **Location**: Gulf Coast mountains, Eastern Mexico
- **Altitude**: 1,000-1,600 MASL
- **Production**: Historic coffee region
- **Regions**: Coatepec, Xico, Huatusco
- **Character**: Mild, chocolatey, low acidity, smooth

**Other Regions** (<10%):
- **Puebla**: Central highlands, limited production
- **Guerrero**: Pacific Coast, small-scale

## üå± Coffee Varieties

### Traditional Varieties (Quality, Declining)

**Typica** (20-25%, declining):
- **Heritage**: Original variety in Mexico (1700s-1800s)
- **Quality**: Excellent, clean, balanced, nutty-chocolate
- **Challenge**: Low yield, rust-susceptible
- **Status**: Maintained by organic/quality farmers, decreasing
- **Flavor**: Clean, sweet, chocolate, nuts, balanced acidity
- **Best**: Higher altitudes, shade-grown

**Bourbon** (15-20%, declining):
- **Introduction**: Early 1900s
- **Quality**: Excellent, sweet, complex
- **Challenge**: Rust-susceptible, low yield
- **Status**: Declining but valued for quality
- **Flavor**: Caramel, chocolate, balanced, sweet
- **Best**: Chiapas highlands, quality-focused farms

**Caturra** (20-25%):
- **Introduction**: Mid-1900s
- **Quality**: Good to excellent at altitude
- **Yield**: Better than Typica/Bourbon
- **Challenge**: Rust-susceptible but manageable
- **Flavor**: Bright, clean, balanced, nutty
- **Status**: Still common, reliable choice

### Modern Productive Varieties

**Mundo Novo** (15-20%):
- **Origin**: Brazilian hybrid (Typica x Bourbon)
- **Quality**: Good, reliable
- **Yield**: High, productive
- **Challenge**: Large plants, requires spacing
- **Flavor**: Balanced, chocolate, nuts, caramel
- **Status**: Popular for productivity

**Catimor** (5-10%):
- **Introduction**: 1990s-2000s for rust resistance
- **Quality**: Lower than traditional varieties
- **Yield**: Very high
- **Challenge**: Cup quality compromised
- **Flavor**: Nutty, woody, less complexity
- **Status**: Common in commodity production

### New Rust-Resistant Varieties

**Marsellesa** (Growing, 5-10%):
- **Origin**: World Coffee Research hybrid (Sarchimor derivative)
- **Introduction**: 2010s in Mexico
- **Quality**: Good cup quality + rust resistance (rare combination)
- **Advantage**: Productive, resistant, specialty-grade
- **Flavor**: Balanced, chocolate, caramel, good acidity
- **Status**: Rapidly increasing adoption post-rust crisis
- **Future**: May become dominant variety

**Castillo** (Limited, experimental):
- Colombian variety, trial plantings in Mexico
- Good resistance, moderate quality

**Costa Rica 95 (CR95)**:
- Sarchimor variety, some adoption
- Rust-resistant, moderate quality

## üîÑ Processing Methods

### Fully Washed (Traditional) - 75-85%

**Standard Mexican Processing**:

1. **Harvest**: Selective hand-picking (October-March)
2. **Depulping**: Same day, mechanical pulpers
3. **Fermentation**: 12-24 hours (varies by altitude and temperature)
4. **Washing**: Clean water channels
5. **Drying**: 
   - **Patios**: Concrete/brick, traditional
   - **Raised Beds**: Increasing in specialty
   - **Guardiolas**: Mechanical dryers (wet regions)
6. **Duration**: 8-15 days to 10-12% moisture
7. **Dry Milling**: Hulling, sizing, sorting

**Flavor Impact**:
- **Clean**: Bright, balanced, clear
- **Chocolate**: Milk chocolate, cocoa
- **Nuts**: Almond, hazelnut
- **Caramel**: Brown sugar, toffee
- **Acidity**: Medium, balanced, citric
- **Body**: Medium, smooth
- **Classic**: Traditional Mexican profile

### Honey Process (Growing, 10-15%)

**Recent Innovation**:
- Adopted from Costa Rica and El Salvador
- Increasing in specialty sector
- Multiple honey levels (yellow, red, black)

**Flavor Impact**:
- **Sweeter**: More pronounced sweetness
- **Fruit**: Berry, tropical fruit notes emerge
- **Body**: Fuller, more syrupy
- **Complexity**: Higher than washed

**Specialty Focus**: Competition lots often honey process

### Natural (Dry) Process (5-10%, Emerging)

**Challenges**:
- Mexico's humidity makes natural processing difficult
- Requires ideal dry season conditions
- High risk of defects (mold, fermentation issues)

**When Successful**:
- Very fruity, sweet
- Full body
- Berry and chocolate
- Unique for Mexico

**Limited**: Mostly experimental specialty lots
