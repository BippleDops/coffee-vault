**Region**: East-Central Africa  
**Global Rank**: Small producer (~50th globally), exceptional quality  
**Annual Production**: 300,000-400,000 bags = ~18,000-24,000 metric tons

## üìã Overview

Burundi produces exceptional specialty coffee that rivals Rwanda and Kenya in brightness and complexity, yet remains largely unknown outside specialty circles. Despite political instability, poverty, and infrastructure challenges, Burundian coffee delivers bright berry-citrus-floral profiles at remarkable value, representing one of East African coffee's best-kept secrets.

**Why Burundi Deserves Recognition**:
- **East African Quality**: Kenya/Rwanda-like brightness, berry, complexity
- **Bourbon Dominant**: 95%+ Red Bourbon (traditional variety, exceptional quality)
- **High Altitude**: 1,250-2,000 MASL ideal growing conditions
- **Washed Processing**: Clean, bright, well-processed
- **Undervalued**: 40-50% cheaper than comparable Kenyan coffee
- **Cooperative Model**: Washing stations serve smallholders
- **Improving**: Quality focus increasing, specialty recognition growing
- **Cup of Excellence**: Showcased exceptional lots (87-92+ points)

**Historical & Political Context**:
- **Coffee Introduction**: 1920s-1930s (Belgian colonial era)
- **Bourbon Establishment**: Belgian colonists planted Red Bourbon extensively
- **Independence** (1962): Coffee becomes primary export
- **Civil War** (1993-2005): Devastating conflict, coffee industry damaged
- **Post-Conflict** (2005-Present): Slow recovery, quality focus emerges
- **Challenges**: Political instability ongoing, infrastructure limited, poverty widespread
- **Coffee's Role**: 80% of export revenue, 800,000 farming families (60% of population)

**Current Situation**:
- Coffee is lifeline for nation
- Smallholder-dominated (95%+ small family farms)
- Cooperative washing stations central
- Quality increasing despite challenges
- Specialty market discovering Burundi
- Direct trade relationships growing

## üåç Geography & Terroir

**Location**: Small landlocked nation, bordered by Rwanda, Tanzania, DRC

**Geography**:
- **"Land of 1,000 Hills"**: Like Rwanda, extremely mountainous
- **Altitude**: 1,250-2,000 MASL in coffee zones
- **Lake Tanganyika**: Western border, microclimate influence
- **Volcanic Soil**: Similar to Rwanda/Tanzania, mineral-rich, fertile
- **Slopes**: Steep volcanic mountain slopes throughout

**Climate**:
- **Temperature**: 16-24¬∞C (61-75¬∞F) in highlands
- **Rainfall**: 1,200-1,600mm annually, bimodal pattern
- **Rainy Seasons**: February-May (long rains), September-November (short rains)
- **Dry Season**: June-August (harvest period)
- **Equatorial**: Consistent daylight, year-round growing

**Terroir**:
- Similar to Rwanda (immediately north)
- Volcanic soils, high altitude, steep slopes
- Ideal for bright, complex Arabica

## üå± Varieties

**Red Bourbon** (95%+ of production):
- **Dominant**: Overwhelming majority
- **Heritage**: Belgian colonial plantings (1920s-1930s)
- **Never Replaced**: Unlike other origins, Burundi kept Bourbon
- **Quality**: Exceptional cup quality
- **Challenges**: Low yield, rust-susceptible
- **Why Maintained**: Quality focus, specialty positioning

**Jackson** (2-3%):
- Bourbon mutation
- Similar to Bourbon, slightly different morphology
- Good quality

**Mibirizi** (1-2%):
- Local selection
- Good quality, limited production

**Modern Hybrids** (<1%):
- Minimal adoption
- Government resistant to replacing Bourbon (quality concern)

## üîÑ Processing

**Fully Washed** (99% of production):

**Process** (Similar to Rwanda):
1. Selective hand-picking
2. Depulping at washing station (same day)
3. Fermentation: 12-24 hours
4. Washing: Clean water channels
5. Soaking: Some stations (like Kenya/Rwanda)
6. Drying: Raised African beds, 10-21 days
7. Dry milling: Size and density sorting

**Result**: Clean, bright, berry-citrus-floral, complex

- **Sweet**: High sweetness balances acidity
- **Undervalued**: Quality matches neighbors but price 40-50% lower
