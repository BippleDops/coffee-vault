## üîÑ Processing Methods

### Natural (Dry) - Traditional Brazilian Method

**Process**:
1. Cherries picked (mix of ripe and unripe - strip picking common)
2. Dried whole on patios or raised beds
3. 20-30 days drying in sun
4. Turned regularly
5. Husked after drying

**Result**: Sweet, heavy body, low acidity, nutty, chocolate

**Why Dominant**: 
- Brazil's dry harvest season ideal for natural processing
- Less water needed (important in some regions)
- Traditional method
- Matches market expectations ("Brazilian taste")

### Pulped Natural (Semi-Washed)

**Process**:
1. Cherries depulped (fruit removed)
2. Dried with mucilage still attached
3. No fermentation or washing stage
4. Dried on patios (faster than full natural)

**Result**: Between washed and natural - some body, some brightness, sweet

**Advantages**:
- Faster drying than full natural
- Less water than fully washed
- Unique Brazilian specialty

### Fully Washed (Less Common)

**Status**: Growing in specialty segment  
**Process**: Standard washed (ferment, wash, dry parchment)  
**Result**: Brighter acidity, cleaner than natural  
**Market**: Specialty roasters seeking different Brazilian profile

## üìä Industry Structure

### Farm Scale

**Large Fazendas** (Estates):
- 100-10,000+ hectares
- Mechanized harvesting
- On-farm processing
- Export directly
- Corporate ownership increasing

**Medium Farms**:
- 10-100 hectares
- Mix of manual and mechanical
- Cooperative processing common

**Smallholders**:
- <10 hectares
- Manual harvesting
- Process via cooperatives
- Lower percentage than other origins

### Mechanization

**Brazil Leads World**:
- Flat terrain allows machines
- Mechanical harvesters common
- Strip picking (all cherries at once)
- Efficiency and scale
- Lower labor costs
- Uniformity (for better or worse)

**Impact**:
- Can harvest unripe cherries (quality issue)
- Efficient and economical
- Enables Brazil's massive production
- Specialty lots often hand-picked

## üåü Flavor Profile

### Classic Brazilian Natural

**Standard Profile**:
- **Aroma**: Nuts, chocolate, sweet, mild
- **Flavor**: Peanut, hazelnut, dark chocolate, caramel, low fruit
- **Body**: Full, heavy, creamy
- **Acidity**: Very low, mild, smooth
- **Finish**: Nutty, chocolatey, sweet

**The "Brazilian Taste"**:
- Defined by low acidity
- Nutty, chocolatey sweetness
- Full body
- Smooth, easy-drinking
- Not complex but pleasant
- "Sweet as a nut" description

### Specialty Brazilian

**Modern High-Quality Brazilian**:
- **Aroma**: Complex, fruit, chocolate
- **Flavor**: Stone fruit, chocolate, caramel, nuts, balanced
- **Body**: Medium-full (lighter than commodity)
- **Acidity**: Present but gentle (not bright)
- **Finish**: Sweet, complex

**Specialty Improvement**: 
- Selective picking
- Better processing
- Higher altitudes
- Bourbon, Yellow Bourbon varietals
- More complexity than traditional commodity

### Why Brazil is Different

**Compared to Other Origins**:
- Lowest acidity of major origins
- Heaviest body
- Most "chocolatey" profile
- Less fruit-forward
- Extremely consistent
- Available year-round
- Affordable

## üí° Buying Tips

**What to Look For**:
- **Region**: Cerrado Mineiro (DO quality), Sul de Minas, Mogiana
- **Grade**: Screen 17/18 (larger beans), NY 2-4 (defect count)
- **Process**: Natural for classic, pulped natural for specialty
- **Varietal**: Yellow Bourbon (distinctive), Bourbon (quality)
- **Altitude**: Higher is better (1,000+ MASL)

**Roast Level**:
- Medium roast showcases sweetness
- Medium-dark traditional
- Light roast can be flat (low acidity)
- Dark roast works (chocolate profile survives)

**Brewing Methods**:
- **Espresso**: Excellent base for blends (sweetness, body, crema)
- **French Press**: Natural fit (body and chocolate)
- **Drip/Auto-drip**: Traditional application
- **Cold Brew**: Perfect (low acid, sweet, smooth)
- **Pour-over**: Works but may lack brightness some prefer

**Price Points**:
- **Commodity Brazilian**: $8-12/lb
- **Specialty Brazilian**: $12-16/lb  
- **Premium (Cerrado, Yellow Bourbon)**: $16-20/lb
- **Competition micro-lots**: $20-40/lb

**Best Value**: Brazilian coffee offers excellent quality-to-price ratio
