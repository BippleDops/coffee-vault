## üìã Overview

Honduras has quietly emerged as Central America's largest coffee producer and a growing force in specialty coffee. Despite political challenges, natural disasters, and historical underinvestment, Honduran coffee has evolved from commodity-grade bulk exports to winning competitions and capturing specialty market attention with exceptional quality, diverse flavor profiles, and remarkable value.

**Why Honduras Matters**:
- **Volume Leader**: Surpassed Guatemala and Nicaragua to become Central America's #1 producer
- **Quality Evolution**: Transformation from commodity to specialty over past 15 years
- **Value Proposition**: Exceptional quality-to-price ratio
- **Diversity**: Six distinct coffee-growing regions with unique profiles
- **Resilience**: Overcame coffee rust epidemics, hurricanes, political instability
- **Innovation**: Rapid adoption of specialty processing (honey, natural, experimental)
- **Altitude**: High-elevation growing (1,200-1,700+ MASL) supports quality
- **Recognition**: Cup of Excellence since 2004, consistent competition winners

**Historical Context**:
- Coffee cultivation since 1800s
- Major expansion 1970s-1980s (replaced banana economy)
- Commodity focus until 2000s
- **2012**: Surpassed Guatemala as largest Central American producer
- **Coffee Rust Crisis** (2012-2014): Lost 30-40% production, forced modernization
- **Post-Crisis**: Quality-focused recovery, specialty emergence
- **Present**: Balancing volume production with specialty development

## üå± Coffee Varieties

### Traditional Varieties (High Quality, Declining)

**Bourbon** (10-15% and declining):
- **Quality**: Excellent cup quality, sweet, complex
- **Challenge**: Susceptible to coffee leaf rust (CLR)
- **Status**: Being replaced by rust-resistant varieties
- **Flavor**: Caramel, chocolate, balanced, sweet
- **Where**: Higher-altitude farms, quality-focused producers

**Typica** (5-10% and declining):
- **Quality**: Outstanding complexity and clarity
- **Challenge**: Very low yield, highly rust-susceptible
- **Status**: Rare, heritage farms only
- **Flavor**: Clean, bright, complex, delicate
- **Where**: Small specialty lots, traditional farms

**Caturra** (15-20%):
- **Quality**: Good, especially at altitude
- **Yield**: Better than Bourbon/Typica
- **Challenge**: Rust-susceptible
- **Flavor**: Bright, clean, citrus, balanced
- **Status**: Still common, gradual replacement

### Modern Rust-Resistant Varieties (Dominant Post-2014)

**Catuai** (25-30%):
- **Origin**: Caturra x Mundo Novo (Brazil)
- **Quality**: Good cup quality, reliable
- **Advantage**: Wind-resistant, good yield, compact
- **Flavor**: Balanced, chocolate, nuts, caramel
- **Status**: Widely adopted, popular choice

**Lempira** (20-25% and growing):
- **Origin**: Honduran-bred variety (IHCAFE)
- **Development**: Sarchimor derivative, rust-resistant
- **Quality**: Good cup quality (better than Catimor)
- **Advantage**: High yield, strong rust resistance
- **Flavor**: Balanced, sweet, chocolate, nuts
- **Status**: Government-promoted, increasing adoption
- **Recognition**: Shows quality can coexist with resistance

**IHCAFE 90** (15-20%):
- **Origin**: Honduran research (IHCAFE - Instituto Hondure√±o del Caf√©)
- **Development**: Catimor-type, rust-resistant
- **Quality**: Moderate cup quality (better with altitude)
- **Advantage**: Very high yield, strong resistance
- **Flavor**: Nutty, chocolate, balanced, less complex
- **Status**: Common in commodity production, less in specialty

**Parainema** (5-10%, growing in specialty):
- **Origin**: Honduran-bred from Sarchimor
- **Quality**: Good to excellent, specialty-grade
- **Advantage**: Rust resistance, decent yield, good quality
- **Flavor**: Balanced, sweet, chocolate, fruit
- **Status**: Emerging favorite for specialty

**Obata** (<5%, experimental):
- **Origin**: Brazilian hybrid
- **Quality**: Good potential
- **Status**: Trial plantings

### The Coffee Rust Impact

**La Roya Crisis** (2012-2014):
- **Impact**: 30-40% production loss
- **Cause**: Coffee leaf rust epidemic (Hemileia vastatrix)
- **Result**: Massive replanting with rust-resistant varieties
- **Transformation**: Forced modernization, shift to resistant genetics
- **Quality Concern**: Traditional Bourbon/Typica largely replaced
- **Silver Lining**: New varieties (Lempira, Parainema) show quality potential

## üîÑ Processing Methods

### Washed (Fully Washed) - 70-80% of Production

**Traditional Honduran Processing**:

1. **Selective Harvest**: Hand-picked ripe cherries
2. **Depulping**: Within 24 hours of harvest
3. **Fermentation**: 12-24 hours (altitude and temperature dependent)
4. **Washing**: Clean water channels, mucilage removed
5. **Drying**: 
   - **Patios**: Concrete or brick, 10-15 days
   - **Raised Beds**: Increasing adoption, 12-18 days (better quality)
   - **Guardiolas** (mechanical dryers): Some regions for wet season
6. **Storage**: In parchment until dry milling

**Flavor Impact**:
- Clean, bright, balanced
- Citric acidity, fruit notes
- Medium body
- Reliable, consistent quality
- Classic Central American profile

### Honey Process (Semi-Washed) - 15-20% and Growing

**Honduras' Specialty Focus**:
- Rapid adoption in specialty sector (post-2010)
- Various levels: White, Yellow, Red, Black honey
- Labor-intensive but quality-focused

**Process**:
- Depulp, leave mucilage on bean
- Dry with sticky mucilage intact
- 10-20 days drying (labor-intensive, requires raking)

**Honey Level Varieties**:
- **White Honey**: 10-25% mucilage, lighter profile
- **Yellow Honey**: 25-50% mucilage, balanced
- **Red Honey**: 50-75% mucilage, fruity
- **Black Honey**: 75-100% mucilage, very fruity, wine-like

**Flavor Impact**:
- Sweeter than washed
- Fruit-forward (berry, tropical fruit)
- Fuller body than washed
- Complexity between washed and natural
- Increasingly popular in competitions

### Natural (Dry) Process - 5-10%, Growing in Specialty

**Recent Innovation**:
- Not traditional in Honduras (humid climate challenges)
- Increasing experimentation in specialty sector
- Requires careful management (mold risk in humid climate)

**Process**:
- Whole cherries dried on patios or raised beds
- 20-30 days drying time
- High labor, weather-dependent

**Flavor Impact**:
- Very fruity, berry-forward
- Full body, syrupy
- Lower acidity
- Funky, wine-like (when done well)
- Can have defects if rushed or uncontrolled

### Experimental Processing (Emerging)

**Innovation in Specialty**:
- **Anaerobic Fermentation**: Sealed tanks, controlled fermentation
- **Carbonic Maceration**: Inspired by wine-making
- **Extended Fermentation**: 48-72+ hours
- **Hybrid Methods**: Combinations of above

**Results**:
- Extreme fruit flavors
- Complex, unexpected profiles
- Fetches premium prices
- Growing presence in competitions
