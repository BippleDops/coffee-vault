## üìã Recipe Overview

**Description**: A precision V60 recipe meticulously designed to showcase the delicate floral and fruited notes of light roast coffees, particularly Ethiopian naturals and washed Geishas. This method emphasizes clarity, complexity, and tea-like body while maintaining bright acidity and clean sweetness.

**Recipe Philosophy**: Light roasts require gentle extraction to preserve delicate aromatics while achieving adequate extraction. This recipe uses controlled water temperature, precise pouring technique, and optimal brew time to extract floral compounds without introducing astringency or bitterness.

**Recipe Source**: Developed from Nordic roasting traditions and Japanese precision brewing
**Derived From**: Scott Rao V60 method, Tetsu Kasuya 4:6 method principles
**Based On**: Third-wave specialty coffee extraction science

**Best For**:
- Coffee enthusiasts seeking maximum clarity and complexity
- Showcasing competition-grade light roasts (85+ cupping score)
- Floral-forward Ethiopian coffees (Yirgacheffe, Guji, Sidamo)
- Washed Geisha varieties from Panama, Colombia, Ethiopia
- Natural processed Ethiopians with fruit-forward profiles
- Anyone wanting to taste coffee's most delicate characteristics

**Not Ideal For**:
- Dark roasts (will over-extract and taste bitter)
- Those preferring bold, heavy-bodied coffee
- Quick morning routines (requires attention and time)
- Beginners to pour over (requires technique refinement)
- Low-quality commodity coffee (won't showcase complexity)

## üìù Step-by-Step Instructions

### Pre-Brewing Preparation (2-3 minutes before brewing)

**Step 1: Water Preparation**
- Fill gooseneck kettle with filtered water
- Set temperature to 96¬∞C (will drop to 94¬∞C)
- Turn on kettle and allow to reach temperature
- Kettle will hold temperature until ready

**Step 2: Bean Weighing**
- Place small container on scale
- Tare scale to zero
- Weigh exactly 15.0g coffee beans
- Use whole beans (never pre-ground)

**Step 3: Grinding**
- Ensure grinder is clean and dry
- Set grind size to medium-fine (20-22 clicks Comandante)
- Pour beans into grinder
- Grind immediately before brewing (oxidation starts instantly)
- Grind should be uniform, consistent particle size
- Check for grinder retention (tap out all coffee)

**Step 4: Filter Preparation**
- Place V60 dripper on vessel or cup
- Insert V60 filter (fold seam if applicable)
- Ensure filter sits flush against V60 walls

**Step 5: Preheat and Rinse**
- Pour hot water through filter (50-100g)
- This rinses paper taste and preheats V60
- Swirl water in vessel to preheat it
- Discard rinse water completely
- Ensure V60 and vessel are hot to touch

**Step 6: Final Setup**
- Place preheated V60 and vessel on scale
- Tare scale to zero
- Add ground coffee to filter
- Gently shake V60 to level coffee bed
- Create small well in center with finger (optional)
- Have timer ready to start
- Confirm kettle at 94¬∞C (¬±1¬∞C)

### Brewing Process (3:00 total time)

**Phase 1: Bloom (0:00 - 0:45)**

**0:00 - Start Timer**
- Start timer simultaneously with first water contact
- Begin pouring water in center of coffee bed
- Use slow, controlled circular motion
- Pour spiraling outward from center
- Avoid pouring on filter (stay 1cm from edge)

**0:00 - 0:15 - Bloom Pour**
- Pour to 40g total (2.7x coffee dose)
- Complete pour by 0:15
- All coffee grounds should be saturated
- Bloom should be even, no dry spots

**0:15 - 0:20 - Gentle Agitation**
- Gentle swirl of V60 (pick up and rotate)
- OR use spoon to gently stir edges to center (5-10 stirs)
- Goal: Ensure complete saturation, no dry pockets
- Coffee will bubble and degas (especially fresh coffee)

**0:20 - 0:45 - Bloom Rest**
- Allow coffee to bloom undisturbed
- CO2 releases, creating foam
- Fresh coffee (7-14 days) will bloom vigorously
- Older coffee blooms less dramatically
- Watch for even bubbling across surface
- Bed may rise slightly, this is normal

**Bloom Science**: CO2 trapped in beans from roasting creates barrier to water. Bloom releases CO2, allowing water to access soluble compounds in subsequent pours. Adequate blooming critical for even extraction.

**Phase 2: First Main Pour (0:45 - 1:15)**

**0:45 - Begin First Pour**
- Kettle at 94¬∞C
- Begin slow, controlled pour
- Start in center, spiral outward
- Maintain steady pour rate (3-4g per second)

**Pour Technique**:
- Gooseneck spout 5-8cm above coffee bed
- Slow, controlled spiral motion
- Pour from center ‚Üí outer edge ‚Üí center
- Steady concentric circles
- Avoid disturbing coffee on filter walls
- Maintain steady stream (pencil-thin width)

**0:45 - 1:05 - Active Pouring**
- Pour to 130g total (+90g from bloom)
- Maintain steady pour rate
- Coffee bed should rise evenly
- Aim for consistent turbulence (gentle agitation)
- No fast channels or uneven flow

**1:05 - 1:15 - Complete Pour**
- Finish at 130g by 1:15
- Allow water to draw down slightly
- Coffee bed should be relatively flat
- Water level dropping toward coffee surface

**Phase 3: Second Main Pour (1:15 - 1:45)**

**1:15 - Begin Second Pour**
- Start second pour at 1:15
- Same technique as first pour
- Center spiral pattern
- Maintain 3-4g per second rate

**1:15 - 1:35 - Active Pouring**
- Pour to 200g total (+70g from previous)
- Steady, controlled spiral
- Coffee bed rises again
- Even turbulence across surface

**1:35 - 1:45 - Complete Pour**
- Finish at 200g by 1:45
- Allow brief drawdown
- Prepare for final pour

**Phase 4: Final Pour (1:45 - 2:15)**

**1:45 - Begin Final Pour**
- Start third pour at 1:45
- Slightly slower, more gentle
- Focus on center to maintain flat bed

**1:45 - 2:05 - Active Pouring**
- Pour to 250g total (+50g from previous)
- Slower, more delicate pour
- Avoid disturbing coffee bed edges

**2:05 - 2:15 - Complete Pour**
- Finish at 250g by 2:15
- Final water added
- Coffee bed should be flat
- No high-and-dry coffee on walls

**Phase 5: Drawdown (2:15 - 3:00)**

**2:15 - 3:00 - Final Drawdown**
- No more water added
- Water drains through coffee bed
- Do not disturb or agitate
- Drawdown should be steady, not stalling

**Visual Cues During Drawdown**:
- Water level gradually lowers
- Coffee bed settles flat
- Minimal residue on filter walls
- Even, level final bed (indicates even extraction)

**2:45 - 3:00 - Final Moments**
- Last water draining through
- Target completion: 3:00 (¬±15 seconds acceptable)
- Coffee bed should be flat with minimal high-and-dry
- All water drained

**3:00 - Completion**
- Remove V60 from vessel
- Inspect coffee bed:
  - Flat bed = good extraction
  - Domed or uneven = channeling occurred
  - High-and-dry on walls = pouring too aggressively
- Discard filter and spent coffee

**Phase 6: Serving**

**Immediately After Brewing**:
- Gentle swirl of brewed coffee in vessel
- Swirling integrates any settling
- Smell the aroma (floral, fruit, complex)
- Pour into preheated cup

**Drinking Window**:
- **Too Hot** (0-2 min): Aromatics strong, flavors muted
- **Optimal** (3-10 min): Full flavor complexity emerges
- **Cooling** (10-20 min): Different flavors appear as temperature drops
- **Cold** (20+ min): New characteristics, but losing vitality

**Tasting Notes**:
- First sip: Aromatics, acidity, initial impressions
- Second sip: Body, sweetness, texture
- Third sip: Complexity, finish, aftertaste
- Allow coffee to cool, taste evolution

## üéØ Pour Technique Mastery

### The Perfect Pour

**Gooseneck Control**:
- **Height**: 5-8cm above coffee surface (too high = splashing, too low = stirring)
- **Angle**: Kettle at 45¬∞ angle to vertical
- **Stream Width**: Pencil-thin, consistent diameter
- **Flow Rate**: 3-4g per second (steady, controlled)
- **Movement**: Smooth, continuous spiral motion

**Spiral Pattern**:
- Start in center of coffee bed
- Spiral outward in concentric circles
- Outer limit: 1cm from filter wall
- Return to center
- Repeat throughout pour
- Goal: Even saturation across entire bed

**Common Pour Mistakes**:
- **Pouring Too Fast**: Creates channeling, uneven extraction
- **Pouring on Walls**: Water bypasses coffee (underextraction)
- **Pouring Too High**: Disturbs coffee bed, creates turbulence
- **Inconsistent Rate**: Some areas over-extracted, others under
- **Center-Only Pour**: Edges underextracted, channeling risk

**Pour Practice Drill**:
- Practice pouring into empty V60
- Aim for consistent pour rate
- Master spiral pattern
- Build muscle memory
- Track 30-second pours (should be ~90-100g at 3g/sec)

### Agitation Philosophy

**Why Agitation Matters**:
- Ensures even saturation during bloom
- Prevents channeling
- Promotes uniform extraction
- Breaks up clumps

**Minimal Agitation Approach** (This Recipe):
- Gentle swirl after bloom only
- Pours provide adequate agitation
- Goal: Clarity and clean cup

**Alternative: Aggressive Agitation**:
- Stir after each pour
- Creates more turbulence
- Increases extraction and body
- Risk: Over-extraction, muddiness
- Best for: Under-extracting coffees

**When to Adjust Agitation**:
- **Increase**: Coffee tastes weak, sour, underextracted
- **Decrease**: Coffee tastes muddy, harsh, over-extracted
- **Standard**: Gentle bloom swirl (this recipe)

### Failure Indicators and Solutions

**Problem: Brew Too Fast (Finishing Before 2:30)**

**Symptoms**:
- Total time under 2:30
- Water racing through coffee bed
- Weak, watery flavor
- Sour, grassy, underdeveloped notes
- Thin body
- Low extraction (<18%)

**Causes**:
- Grind too coarse
- Pouring too aggressively (creating channels)
- Insufficient coffee bed depth
- Incorrect filter placement

**Solutions**:
- Grind finer (2-3 clicks finer on Comandante)
- Pour more gently and slowly
- Increase coffee dose (try 17g)
- Ensure filter sits flush against V60
- Check for grinder issues (worn burrs, uneven grind)

**Problem: Brew Too Slow (Finishing After 3:30)**

**Symptoms**:
- Total time over 3:30
- Water stalling on coffee bed
- Bitter, harsh, astringent flavor
- Over-extracted taste
- Muddy, cloudy cup
- High extraction (>23%)

**Causes**:
- Grind too fine
- Too many fines clogging filter
- Coffee too fresh (excess CO2)
- Water temperature too high
- Excessive agitation

**Solutions**:
- Grind coarser (2-3 clicks on Comandante)
- Reduce agitation (skip bloom swirl)
- Let coffee degas longer (wait 2-3 more days)
- Lower temperature to 92-93¬∞C
- Check grinder for excess fines production
- Tap grinder to reduce retention/static

**Problem: Channeling Visible**

**Symptoms**:
- Fast channels visible during brewing
- Water bypassing coffee, running down sides
- Uneven coffee bed at finish (dome shape, craters)
- Inconsistent flavor (simultaneously weak and bitter)
- Light patches on coffee bed

**Causes**:
- Uneven grind distribution
- Pouring on filter walls
- Not leveling coffee bed before brewing
- Excessive bloom agitation creating cracks

**Solutions**:
- Shake/tap V60 gently before brewing to level bed
- Improve pour technique (stay off walls)
- Reduce bloom agitation
- Check grinder for clumping
- Use WDT tool to distribute grounds (if very bad)

**Problem: Bitter, Astringent, Harsh**

**Symptoms**:
- Bitter, unpleasant aftertaste
- Dry, puckering sensation
- Harsh, aggressive flavors
- No sweetness
- Dark, over-extracted taste

**Causes**:
- Temperature too high
- Grind too fine
- Over-extraction (>22%)
- Coffee too dark for recipe
- Poor quality coffee

**Solutions**:
- Lower temperature to 92-93¬∞C
- Grind coarser
- Reduce brew time (faster pours)
- Confirm coffee is actually light roast
- Less agitation
- Fresh, quality coffee essential

**Problem: Sour, Grassy, Underdeveloped**

**Symptoms**:
- Sour, unpleasant acidity (not bright, just sour)
- Grassy, vegetal notes
- Weak flavor
- Underdeveloped taste
- Thin body
- Short finish

**Causes**:
- Temperature too low
- Grind too coarse
- Under-extraction (<18%)
- Coffee too light even for this recipe
- Insufficient brew time

**Solutions**:
- Increase temperature to 95-96¬∞C
- Grind finer
- Slower, longer pours
- Increase agitation (stir after bloom)
- Ensure coffee isn't under-roasted
- Increase dose (try 17g for 250g water)

**Problem: Muddy, Cloudy, Lacks Clarity**

**Symptoms**:
- Murky, cloudy appearance
- Muddy, indistinct flavors
- Can't distinguish individual notes
- Heavy, thick body (not desirable here)
- Fines in cup

**Causes**:
- Grind too fine (creating fines)
- Excessive agitation
- Pouring too aggressively
- Low-quality grinder (uneven grind)
- Coffee too fresh (fines from CO2 explosion)

**Solutions**:
- Grind slightly coarser
- Minimize agitation
- Gentle, controlled pours
- Upgrade grinder if possible
- Let coffee rest 2-3 more days
- Consider rinsing filter twice

**Problem: Flat, Boring, Lacks Complexity**

**Symptoms**:
- One-dimensional flavor
- No distinct notes
- Boring, plain coffee taste
- Missing floral/fruit notes expected
- Dull aroma

**Causes**:
- Stale coffee (aromatics faded)
- Poor quality coffee
- Wrong origin/roast for recipe
- Incorrect water quality (too hard/soft)
- Brew temperature issues

**Solutions**:
- Use fresh coffee (7-21 days off roast)
- Source higher quality beans (competition-grade)
- Ensure coffee is floral-forward origin
- Check water TDS (50-150 ppm)
- Verify temperature accuracy
- Try different coffee entirely

## üìä Extraction Science and Theory

### Understanding Extraction

**What is Extraction?**
- Extraction: Percentage of coffee's mass dissolved into water
- Target: 18-22% for balanced cup
- Under 18%: Sour, weak, underdeveloped
- Over 22%: Bitter, harsh, astringent
- Optimal zone: 19-21% for most coffees

**Extraction Timeline**:
- **0-30 seconds**: Acids, fruity esters (bright, sour)
- **30-90 seconds**: Sugars, caramels (sweet, balanced)
- **90-180 seconds**: Bitter compounds, heavy oils (body, bitterness)
- Bloom critical for even extraction of all compounds

**Factors Affecting Extraction**:
1. **Grind Size**: Smaller = more surface area = faster extraction
2. **Temperature**: Hotter = faster extraction, more compounds
3. **Time**: Longer contact = more extraction
4. **Agitation**: More turbulence = faster, more even extraction
5. **Water Quality**: Minerals act as solvents, affect extraction

### Light Roast Extraction Challenges

**Why Light Roasts Are Harder**:
- Denser bean structure (less roast development)
- Cell structure more intact (harder for water to penetrate)
- Acids more prominent (can taste sour if underextracted)
- Require higher extraction (20-22%) to taste balanced
- Narrower optimal extraction window

**Solutions in This Recipe**:
- Medium-fine grind (more surface area)
- Higher temperature (94¬∞C for better penetration)
- Adequate contact time (3:00 total)
- Gentle agitation (improves saturation without over-extraction)
- Proper bloom (releases CO2, allows water access)

### Floral Compound Chemistry

**What Creates Floral Notes**:
- **Linalool**: Lavender, floral sweetness
- **Geraniol**: Rose-like, floral
- **Beta-damascenone**: Fruity-floral, honey
- **Jasmine lactone**: Jasmine, peach
- These are volatile aromatics, extremely delicate

**Preservation Strategies**:
- **Lower temperature**: High heat degrades aromatics (94¬∞C optimal)
- **Fresh coffee**: Aromatics fade rapidly (7-21 days peak)
- **Minimal oxidation**: Grind immediately before brewing
- **Clean extraction**: Avoid over-extraction (bitter masks floral)
- **Proper storage**: Airtight, cool, dark

### Water Chemistry Impact

**Ideal Water Profile for Floral Coffees**:
- **Total Hardness**: 50-100 ppm (as CaCO3)
- **Calcium**: 30-50 ppm (extracts sweet, fruity notes)
- **Magnesium**: 15-25 ppm (extracts floral, bright notes)
- **Alkalinity**: 40-70 ppm (buffers acids)
- **TDS**: 100 ppm ideal

**Water Too Soft** (<50 ppm):
- Under-extraction
- Flat, lifeless cup
- Weak body
- Solution: Add minerals (Third Wave Water)

**Water Too Hard** (>200 ppm):
- Over-extraction
- Chalky, mineral taste
- Dulled flavors
- Solution: Use filtered or distilled water with remineralization

### Temperature and Solubility

**Temperature's Role**:
- Higher temperature = higher solubility of compounds
- Light roasts need 92-96¬∞C (higher than dark roasts at 88-92¬∞C)
- Each 1¬∞C change affects extraction significantly

**Heat Loss During Brewing**:
- Kettle to slurry: ~2-3¬∞C drop
- During bloom: ~3-5¬∞C drop
- During brewing: Additional 2-3¬∞C drop
- Final slurry temperature: ~85-88¬∞C at finish

**Temperature Stability Strategies**:
- Preheat V60 and vessel thoroughly
- Start with slightly higher kettle temp (96¬∞C ‚Üí becomes 94¬∞C)
- Brew in warm environment
- Avoid cold drafts
- Use ceramic V60 (best heat retention)

### Particle Size Distribution

**Grind Consistency Matters**:
- **Uniform grind**: Even extraction, balanced cup
- **Uneven grind**: Fines over-extract (bitter), boulders under-extract (sour)
- Quality grinder essential for light roasts

**Ideal Particle Distribution**:
- Narrow bell curve centered on 600-800 microns
- Minimal fines (<10% under 400 microns)
- Minimal boulders (<10% over 1000 microns)
- Achieved by quality burr grinders

**Grinder Type Impact**:
- **Conical burrs**: Wider distribution, more fines
- **Flat burrs**: Narrower distribution, more uniformity
- **Hand grinders**: Often excellent uniformity (Comandante, 1Zpresso)
- **Blade grinders**: Terrible uniformity, unusable for this recipe

## üí° Expert Tips and Advanced Techniques

### Pro Tips from Competition Baristas

**Tip 1: The Perfect Bloom**
- Bloom weight should be 2.5-3x coffee dose
- 45-second bloom ideal for most light roasts
- Fresh coffee (7-14 days): May need 60-second bloom
- Older coffee (21-30 days): 30-40 second bloom sufficient
- Gentle swirl, don't stir aggressively

**Tip 2: Pour from the Wrist**
- Control comes from wrist rotation, not arm movement
- Stabilize elbow, move only wrist and hand
- Creates smooth, consistent spiral
- Reduces fatigue in longer brews

**Tip 3: The "Tetsu Tap"**
- After bloom, gently tap V60 on counter
- Settles grounds, removes air pockets
- Creates flatter bed for even extraction
- Named after Tetsu Kasuya (2016 World Brewers Cup Champion)

**Tip 4: Temperature Consistency**
- Use same kettle position throughout brew
- Don't set down kettle (loses heat)
- Keep kettle on base between pours
- Monitor temperature if kettle has display

**Tip 5: The Flat Bed Finish**
- Flat bed at finish = even extraction
- Domed bed = channeling occurred
- High-and-dry on walls = pouring too hard on edges
- Adjust technique based on bed shape

**Tip 6: Taste, Measure, Adjust**
- Taste is ultimate judge
- TDS meter validates but doesn't replace tasting
- Take notes every brew
- Make one change at a time
- Track results over 3-5 brews before changing again

### Advanced Troubleshooting

**Dialing In New Coffee**:
1. **First Brew**: Standard recipe (15g, 250g, 94¬∞C, medium-fine)
2. **Taste and Assess**:
   - Too sour ‚Üí Temperature up OR grind finer
   - Too bitter ‚Üí Temperature down OR grind coarser
   - Good but weak ‚Üí Finer grind, same temperature
   - Good but strong ‚Üí Coarser grind or higher ratio
3. **Second Brew**: Adjust ONE variable
4. **Compare**: Better, worse, or different?
5. **Iterate**: Continue until optimal

**The Triangle Method** (Comparing Grind Sizes):
- Brew three cups: coarse, medium, fine (all other variables same)
- Taste side-by-side
- Identify optimal grind zone
- Refine from there

**Time Laddering** (Competition Technique):
- Brew multiple cups with different total times (2:30, 2:45, 3:00, 3:15)
- Adjust grind to hit each time
- Taste to find optimal time for this coffee
- Reveals how coffee responds to extraction

### Seasonal and Environmental Adjustments

**Summer Brewing** (Hot, Humid):
- Grind may need to be slightly coarser (humidity affects grind)
- Coffee may degas faster (fresher = more volatile)
- Consider iced method
- Keep coffee in coolest storage

**Winter Brewing** (Cold, Dry):
- Preheat equipment more thoroughly
- May need higher temperature (heat loss to environment)
- Static in grinder (use RDT - Ross Droplet Technique: spritz beans with water)
- Coffee stays fresh longer (cooler storage)

**High Altitude** (>1500m elevation):
- Water boils at lower temperature
- May need pressure brewing methods instead
- Adjust temperature expectations
- Coffee may brew faster (lower air pressure)

**Hard Water Regions**:
- Invest in water filtration or remineralization
- Use Third Wave Water, Perfect Coffee Water, or DIY water
- Hard water ruins floral notes
- Consider bypass water for dilution

### Storage and Freshness Optimization

**Optimal Coffee Storage**:
- **Container**: Airtight, opaque, one-way valve
- **Location**: Cool, dark, consistent temperature
- **Not Refrigerator**: Condensation and odor absorption
- **Not Freezer**: Only for long-term (>1 month) storage

**Freshness Timeline**:
- **Days 1-4**: Too fresh, excess CO2, hard to extract
- **Days 5-14**: Peak freshness, optimal brewing
- **Days 15-21**: Excellent, slight aromatics fade
- **Days 22-30**: Good, noticeable degradation
- **Days 31+**: Declining, losing complexity

**Batch Size Strategy**:
- Buy in quantities consumable in 2-3 weeks
- 250-350g bags ideal for home brewing
- Track roast date, buy fresh

### Cleaning and Maintenance

**V60 Cleaning**:
- Rinse after each use
- Weekly: Soap and hot water
- Monthly: Cafiza or Urnex soak (removes coffee oils)
- Check for cracks (ceramic can crack from thermal shock)

**Grinder Maintenance**:
- Daily: Brush out retention
- Weekly: Vacuum chamber and burrs
- Monthly: Full disassembly and cleaning
- Every 2-3 months: Check burr alignment
- Replace burrs every 500-1000 lbs of coffee (or 5-10 years home use)

**Kettle Descaling**:
- Monthly: Citric acid or vinegar descale
- Prevents mineral buildup
- Maintains temperature accuracy
- Extends kettle life

**Scale Calibration**:
- Monthly: Calibrate with calibration weight
- Prevents drift in accuracy
- Critical for consistent brewing
- Replace batteries regularly
