## üìù Step-by-Step Instructions

### Detailed Steps

1. **Preparation** (0:00)
   - Boil water, cool to 93¬∞C
   - Weigh 20g coffee beans
   - Grind to medium (23-24 clicks Comandante)
   - Rinse V60 filter with hot water
   - Preheat V60 and server
   - Tare scale with V60 and coffee

2. **Bloom** (0:00 - 0:45)
   - Start timer
   - Add 60g water (3x dose) in circular motion
   - Ensure complete saturation
   - Gentle swirl to ensure even saturation
   - Guatemalan coffees bloom beautifully
   - Wait until 0:45

3. **First Pour** (0:45 - 1:20)
   - Pour to 160g total (100g water added)
   - Steady spiral pour from center outward
   - Maintain consistent pour rate
   - Complete by 1:20

4. **Second Pour** (1:20 - 1:55)
   - Pour to 260g total (100g water added)
   - Same spiral technique
   - Guatemalan coffee handles steady extraction well
   - Complete by 1:55

5. **Final Pour** (1:55 - 2:25)
   - Pour to 320g total (60g water added)
   - Center pour to maintain flat bed
   - Complete by 2:25

6. **Drawdown** (2:25 - 3:00)
   - Allow complete drawdown
   - Target completion: 2:50-3:10
   - Bed should be flat
   - Guatemalan coffees typically drain steadily

7. **Finish** (3:00)
   - Remove V60
   - Gentle swirl of brewed coffee
   - Serve immediately
   - Enjoy chocolate-citrus complexity

### Technique Notes

**Pour Technique**: Steady, controlled spiral
**Agitation Method**: Gentle swirl after bloom, minimal during pours
**Pour Velocity**: Moderate and steady (aim for ~9g/second)

## üí° Tips & Troubleshooting

**Tips**:
- **Balance is key** - Guatemala combines chocolate and citrus
- Temperature controls brightness - 91-92¬∞C for chocolate, 93-94¬∞C for citrus
- Guatemalan highland coffees are high quality - let them shine
- SHB (Strictly Hard Bean) indicates high altitude, high quality
- Antigua is most famous region - balanced, accessible
- Huehuetenango is most complex - bright, intricate
- Allow to cool slightly - complexity develops at 65¬∞C
- Works excellently as daily coffee - balanced and crowd-pleasing

**Understanding Guatemalan Coffee**:
Guatemala has 8 distinct coffee-growing regions, each with unique characteristics:
1. **Antigua**: Balanced, chocolate, citrus - most famous
2. **Huehuetenango**: Complex, bright, wine-like
3. **Atitl√°n**: Full-bodied, chocolate, volcanic soil
4. **Cob√°n**: Floral, delicate, unique terroir
5. **Fraijanes**: Bright acidity, full body
6. **Oriente**: Lower altitude, simpler profile
7. **San Marcos**: Early harvest, bright
8. **Nuevo Oriente**: Emerging region

**Common Mistakes**:
- Temperature too high (>94¬∞C) - extracts harsh acidity
- Temperature too low (<91¬∞C) - mutes complexity
- Grinding too coarse - underextracts chocolate sweetness
- Over-agitation - can muddy the clean finish
- Not adjusting for region - Huehuetenango needs different approach than Antigua

**Adjustment Guide**:
- **Too Acidic/Bright**: Lower temp to 91-92¬∞C, grind slightly coarser
- **Too Heavy/Dull**: Increase temp to 94¬∞C, grind slightly finer
- **Lacks Sweetness**: Ensure adequate bloom (3x dose), maintain 93¬∞C
- **Lacks Complexity**: Increase extraction time slightly
- **Bitter**: Lower temperature or coarser grind

**Troubleshooting**:
- **Harsh Acidity**: Temperature too high or over-extracted
- **Flat/Simple**: Under-extracted - finer grind or hotter water
- **Muddy**: Over-agitation or grind too fine
- **Lacks Balance**: Adjust temperature to find chocolate-citrus sweet spot

## üìù Notes

Guatemalan highland coffees represent Central American coffee at its finest - balanced complexity that combines chocolate sweetness with bright citrus acidity. This recipe is designed to extract both characteristics in harmony.

**Guatemala Coffee History**:
Coffee was introduced to Guatemala in the 1750s by Jesuit priests. The volcanic soil, high altitudes (1,300-2,000m), and distinct microclimates create ideal conditions. Guatemala is one of few origins where terroir dramatically affects flavor - each region tastes distinctly different.

**Why Guatemalan Coffee Is Special**:
1. **Volcanic soil**: Rich in minerals, imparts unique character
2. **High altitude**: Slower bean development, more complexity
3. **Microclimate diversity**: 8 distinct regions, unique profiles
4. **Traditional processing**: Washed process emphasizes clean, bright flavors
5. **Bourbon/Typica heritage**: Traditional varieties, complex flavors

**Antigua - The Icon**:
Antigua is Guatemala's most famous region and produces quintessential Guatemalan profile:
- Surrounded by 3 volcanoes (Agua, Fuego, Acatenango)
- Volcanic soil rich in minerals
- 1,500-1,700m altitude
- Balanced chocolate and citrus
- Full body, clean finish
- Consistent quality

**Huehuetenango - The Complex**:
Guatemala's highest-altitude region (1,500-2,000m):
- Most complex flavor profile
- Bright, wine-like acidity
- Intricate fruit notes
- Lighter body than Antigua
- Competition favorite

**SHB Designation**:
"Strictly Hard Bean" (SHB) or "Hard Bean" (HB) indicates altitude:
- **SHB**: Above 1,370m (4,500 ft) - highest quality
- **HB**: 1,200-1,370m (4,000-4,500 ft) - high quality
- Higher altitude = denser bean = more complex flavor

**Best Use Cases**:
- Daily coffee (balanced, crowd-pleasing)
- Introduction to specialty coffee (accessible complexity)
- Milk drinks (chocolate notes work well)
- Versatile brewing (works hot or iced)
- Office brewing (reliable, consistent)

**Market Position**:
Guatemalan coffee occupies sweet spot:
- More complex than Brazilian
- More balanced than Ethiopian/Kenyan
- More accessible than geisha/rarities
- Reasonable price ($15-22/12oz typical)
- Reliable quality

**Perfect for**: Anyone wanting balanced, complex coffee without extreme characteristics. Guatemala is specialty coffee's "goldilocks" origin - not too bright, not too heavy, just right.
