## ‚òï Varieties & Cultivation

**Varieties Cultivated by Members**:

1. **SL28** (55% of production)
   - Kenya's flagship variety
   - Exceptional cup quality
   - Blackcurrant, citrus, berry complexity
   - Lower productivity, disease susceptible
   - Premium pricing
   - Planted by most traditional farmers

2. **SL34** (30%)
   - Sister variety to SL28
   - Similar quality profile
   - Slightly different terroir expression
   - Also disease susceptible
   - Often intercropped with SL28

3. **Ruiru 11** (10%)
   - Disease-resistant hybrid
   - Higher productivity
   - Coffee Berry Disease (CBD) resistance
   - Lower cup quality than SL varieties
   - Some farmers transitioning for economic reasons

4. **Batian** (5%)
   - Modern disease-resistant variety
   - Better quality than Ruiru 11
   - CBD and Coffee Leaf Rust resistance
   - Increasing adoption
   - Quality approaching SL varieties

**SL28 & SL34 Legacy**:
These varieties are Kenya's crown jewels:
- Developed by Scott Laboratories (1930s-1940s)
- Selected for quality, not yield
- Drought tolerance
- Distinctive cup profile (blackcurrant signature)
- Kenya's specialty coffee reputation built on SL varieties

**Variety Challenges**:
- SL28/34: Disease susceptible (especially Coffee Berry Disease)
- Lower productivity than modern varieties
- Climate change vulnerability
- Economic pressure to replace with high-yield varieties
- Gichataini encourages SL preservation through quality premiums

## üî¨ Processing - Double Fermentation Excellence

Gichataini is renowned for meticulous double fermentation washed processing, producing exceptionally clean, complex Kenyan coffee.

**Kenyan Washed Process** (Traditional Double Fermentation):

**Full Processing Protocol**:

**Day 1 - Cherry Reception & Pulping**:
1. **Reception** (Same-day delivery required):
   - Farmers deliver cherries same day as picking
   - Reception hours: 2pm-7pm
   - Cherry quality inspection
   - Underripe/overripe cherries rejected
   - Weight recording (farmer payment tracking)

2. **Flotation**:
   - Water flotation tank
   - Floaters removed (underripe, defects)
   - Density sorting
   - First quality checkpoint

3. **Pulping** (Evening, within hours of harvest):
   - Mechanical disc pulper
   - Skin removal, mucilage remains
   - Parchment separation by density (gravity channels)
   - P1 (highest density), P2, P3 grades separated

**Day 2 - First Fermentation**:
4. **Dry Fermentation** (12-24 hours):
   - Parchment in tiled tanks
   - No water added (dry fermentation)
   - Mucilage breaks down naturally
   - Fermentation time: Temperature-dependent (16-24 hours)
   - Cool nights (common at 1,800m) = longer fermentation
   - Monitoring by touch, smell, visual inspection

**Day 3 - First Washing & Second Fermentation**:
5. **First Washing**:
   - Clean water washing
   - Mucilage removal
   - Multiple rinses
   - Washing channels with agitation

6. **Soaking** (Second Fermentation):
   - 12-24 hours soaking in clean water
   - Parchment fully submerged
   - Further flavor development
   - Cleanliness enhancement
   - This second fermentation is Kenya's signature

**Day 4 - Final Washing & Drying Begins**:
7. **Final Washing**:
   - Multiple rinses in clean water
   - Complete cleanliness
   - Final quality inspection
   - Removal of any remaining mucilage

8. **Drying Initiation**:
   - Transfer to raised drying tables
   - African raised beds (mesh)
   - Initial moisture reduction

**Days 4-18 - Drying**:
9. **Raised Bed Drying** (12-18 days):
   - African raised beds (mesh, airflow from below)
   - Thin layers (2-3cm depth)
   - Hand-sorting during drying (defect removal)
   - Turned every 1-2 hours during peak sun
   - Covered during hottest hours (prevent cracking)
   - Covered at night (dew protection)
   - Slow, controlled drying

10. **Moisture Monitoring**:
    - Multiple daily moisture checks
    - Target: 10-11% moisture
    - Even drying crucial for quality

11. **Hand-Sorting**:
    - During drying: Defect removal
    - Multiple passes
    - Visual inspection
    - Quality obsession

**Post-Drying**:
12. **Resting**:
    - Parchment rested 30-60 days
    - Moisture stabilization
    - Flavor maturation
    - Storage in parchment protects green coffee

13. **Delivery to Cooperative**:
    - Parchment delivered to Barichu Cooperative
    - Cooperative arranges dry-milling
    - Final grading (AA, AB, C, etc.)
    - Auction or direct sale

**Why Double Fermentation Works**:
- First fermentation: Mucilage breakdown
- Second fermentation (soaking): Flavor complexity, cleanliness
- Result: Exceptionally clean, bright, complex Kenyan coffee
- This method is labor and time-intensive but produces Kenya's signature quality

**Processing Infrastructure**:

- **Pulper**: Mechanical disc pulper (high-quality)
- **Fermentation Tanks**: Tiled tanks (temperature stable, clean)
- **Washing Channels**: Multiple-stage washing system
- **Soaking Tanks**: Second fermentation tanks
- **Raised Beds**: 100+ African raised drying tables
- **Drying Area**: Large, well-ventilated drying area
- **Storage**: Parchment storage warehouse
- **Water Source**: Clean river water (Mount Kenya runoff)

**Quality Control**:
- Cherry reception quality checks
- Flotation density sorting
- Fermentation monitoring (smell, touch, timing)
- Drying moisture checks (multiple daily)
- Hand-sorting during drying
- Pre-delivery cupping (sample lots)
- Traceability (lots tracked by date, grade)

**Water Management**:
- Clean water essential (Kenya's signature brightness)
- Water sourced from Mount Kenya runoff
- Water recycling where possible
- Pulp composting (not water discharge)
- Environmental consciousness

**Lot Separation**:
- By day (daily lots)
- By grade (P1, P2, P3 from pulping)
- By quality during sorting
- Micro-lot selection (best days, best grades)

## üìù Producer Story

Gichataini Coffee Factory was established in 1999 by smallholder coffee farmers in Mathira East, Nyeri County, as part of the Barichu Farmers' Cooperative Society. The factory represents the collective ambition of 1,500+ smallholder families to produce premium Kenyan coffee.

**Cooperative Formation**:
In the 1990s, Kenyan coffee smallholders faced challenges:
- Low prices
- Limited processing infrastructure
- Poor market access
- Exploitation by intermediaries

Gichataini's formation addressed these challenges:
- Collective processing infrastructure
- Quality-based payment systems
- Direct market access (auction)
- Democratic governance

**Processing Excellence Evolution**:
Gichataini built reputation through:
- Meticulous double fermentation
- Quality obsession (hand-sorting, moisture monitoring)
- Lot separation and traceability
- Factory manager expertise (Samuel Muthee's leadership)
- Member farmer training (cherry selection)

**Auction Success**:
Throughout 2000s-2020s, Gichataini lots commanded premium prices at Nairobi auction:
- AA lots regularly among top Nyeri prices
- Buyer recognition of quality
- Factory reputation building
- Member farmer payment increases

**Direct Trade Emergence**:
Around 2015+, specialty roasters began direct purchasing:
- Relationship building with cooperative
- Pre-harvest contracts
- Premium over auction prices
- Quality feedback loops
- Storytelling and traceability

**Current Operations**:
Today, Gichataini balances:
- Traditional auction sales (60%)
- Growing direct trade (30%)
- Export agents (10%)
- Quality focus (meticulous processing)
- Member farmer support (training, payments)

**Member Farmer Stories** (Composite):
