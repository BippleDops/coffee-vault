   - Well-adapted to highland conditions
   - Good cup quality, reliable production
   - Members' primary variety
   - Cup: Balanced, sweet, fruit notes, medium body

2. **Bourbon** (30%)
   - Traditional PNG variety
   - Excellent quality potential
   - Lower productivity than Arusha
   - Some older farms maintain
   - Cup: Complex, sweet, floral, full body

3. **Typica** (15%)
   - Heirloom variety (PNG's original coffee)
   - Very low productivity
   - Exceptional quality
   - Genetic preservation
   - Older trees, some farms
   - Cup: Complex, elegant, refined, tea-like

4. **Blue Mountain** (5%)
   - Jamaican Blue Mountain lineage
   - Limited plantings (experimental)
   - Quality focus
   - Small amounts
   - Cup: Mild, balanced, sweet

**Variety History**:
Coffee came to PNG in 1930s-1950s:
- Typica first (from Jamaica)
- Arusha from Tanzania (1950s)
- Bourbon introduction
- Blue Mountain (limited)

Smallholders maintain varieties through generations, creating mixed-age plantations.

## üî¨ Processing - Washing Station Excellence

Koakaka operates a central washing station serving 450+ member farms. Processing quality dramatically improved farmer incomes.

**Before Cooperative** (Pre-2008):
- Farmers sold wet cherry to middlemen
- Poor prices (exploitative)
- No quality incentive
- No value addition

**After Cooperative** (2008+):
- Central washing station built
- Members deliver cherries
- Cooperative processes to parchment
- Direct sales to exporters
- Fair pricing, quality premiums

**Processing Methods**:

**Primary Method - Washed Process** (70%):

**Washed Process Protocol**:

1. **Cherry Reception** (Daily during harvest):
   - Farmers deliver cherries (walked from villages, sometimes hours)
   - Reception hours: 8am-6pm
   - Quality inspection (ripe selection)
   - Underripe/overripe rejected or paid lower price
   - Weight recording (farmer payment tracking)

2. **Flotation**:
   - Water flotation tank
   - Floaters removed (defects, underripe)
   - Density sorting
   - Quality control

3. **Pulping** (Same day as delivery):
   - Mechanical disc pulper (hand-crank or small motor)
   - Skin removal, mucilage remains
   - Parchment flows to fermentation tanks

4. **Fermentation** (18-36 hours):
   - Concrete or plastic fermentation tanks
   - Dry fermentation (no water added initially)
   - Cool mountain temperatures = longer fermentation (24-36 hours)
   - Mucilage breakdown
   - Monitoring by smell, touch

5. **Washing**:
   - Clean mountain spring water
   - Multiple rinses
   - Washing channels (gravity-fed)
   - Complete mucilage removal

6. **Drying**:
   - Raised bed drying (African beds)
   - Greenhouse drying tables (rain protection, wet season)
   - 12-18 days (weather-dependent)
   - Turned multiple times daily
   - Moisture monitoring
   - Target: 11-12% moisture

**Semi-Washed Process** (20%):
- Partial fermentation
- Less water use (conservation)
- Faster processing
- Still good quality
- Used when water scarce or efficiency needed

**Natural Process** (10% - Experimental):
- Limited (wet season makes natural processing challenging)
- Dry season only (Oct-Feb)
- Whole cherry drying
- Raised beds only
- 25-35 days
- Experimental lots

**Processing Challenges - Wet Season**:
Harvest coincides with wet season (April-Sept):
- Rain during drying = major challenge
- Greenhouse drying tables essential
- Extended drying times
- Moisture management critical
- Mold risk

**Processing Infrastructure**:

- **Wet Mill**: Central processing station
- **Pulper**: Mechanical pulper (small-scale but effective)
- **Fermentation Tanks**: Concrete tanks (6-8 tanks)
- **Washing Channels**: Gravity-fed system (mountain springs)
- **Raised Beds**: 50+ African raised drying beds
- **Greenhouse Tables**: Rain protection (essential for wet season)
- **Storage**: Parchment warehouse (secure, dry)
- **Water Source**: Mountain spring (clean, abundant)

**Quality Control**:
- Cherry reception quality checks
- Flotation density sorting
- Fermentation monitoring
- Daily drying moisture checks
- Hand-sorting during drying (defect removal)
- Pre-export cupping (quality grading)
- Lot traceability

**Water Management**:
- Mountain spring water (pristine quality)
- Gravity-fed system (no pumps needed)
- Minimal water use (environmental consciousness)
- Pulp composting (returned to member farms)
- Zero pollution (streams remain clean)

**Logistics Challenges**:
Moving processed coffee from remote mountains to export:
- Parchment carried by porters (walking, sometimes days)
- During dry season: Vehicles access some areas
- Wet season: Manual carrying only option
- Expensive, time-consuming
- Adds cost but preserves quality

Koakaka Cooperative's story exemplifies remote highland smallholder resilience and collective action.

- No roads to many villages (walking only)
- Middlemen controlled coffee trade
- Exploitative prices ($0.50-1.00/kg cherry)
- No quality incentive
- No market information
- Poverty despite growing high-quality coffee

Farmers knew they were being exploited but had no alternative.

**Catalyst for Change** (2006-2007):
International coffee prices rose, but farmer payments didn't increase. Farmers realized:
"Middlemen are getting rich, we stay poor. We must organize."

**Cooperative Formation** (2008):
Eight villages sent representatives to meetings:
- Shared grievances
- Discussed collective action
- Decided to form cooperative
- Pooled resources for washing station
- Established democratic governance

Initial challenges:
- Limited capital (built simple processing infrastructure)
- Lack of technical knowledge (learned processing)
- Skepticism (would cooperative leaders exploit too?)
- Logistics (extreme remoteness)

**Early Success** (2008-2010):
First harvests processed collectively:
- Higher quality coffee (proper processing)
- Direct sales to exporters (bypass middlemen)
- 2-3x higher prices than middlemen
- Members saw immediate benefits
- Trust built through transparent accounting

**Organic Certification** (2012):

- Already farming organically (formalized it)

- 2012: Certification achieved
- Premium market access

**Fair Trade Certification** (2015):
Built on organic success:
- Fair Trade certification pursued
