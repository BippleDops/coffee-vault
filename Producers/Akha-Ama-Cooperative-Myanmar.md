# Akha Ama Coffee Cooperative - Myanmar

## Overview

Akha Ama Coffee Cooperative represents one of Southeast Asia's most inspiring coffee stories—a social enterprise cooperative in Myanmar's conflict-affected Eastern Shan State that has transformed impoverished Akha ethnic minority villages from opium poppy cultivation and subsistence farming to producing exceptional specialty coffee scoring 85-91 points while creating unprecedented economic opportunities and cultural pride for one of Myanmar's most marginalized indigenous groups. Founded in 2010 by Lee Ayu Chuepa, a visionary Akha woman who returned to her ancestral village after working in Chiang Mai, Thailand, Akha Ama unites approximately 180 Akha families across remote mountain villages at 1,200-1,800 meters, cultivating Catimor, SL34, Caturra, and Typica varieties using organic methods and producing 45-85 tons annually of meticulously processed washed, natural, honey, and experimental coffees that compete internationally.

The name "Akha Ama" means "Akha Mother" in the Akha language—reflecting the cooperative's mission to nurture Akha communities, preserve indigenous culture, and honor the central role of Akha women in agriculture and community life. The cooperative operates far beyond a typical coffee producer organization: it runs Myanmar's most celebrated specialty coffee cafe in Chiang Mai (northern Thailand, where founder Lee established the first cafe before expanding to Yangon), providing direct farm-to-cup integration; manages a community learning center teaching Akha youth coffee skills, entrepreneurship, and cultural preservation; implements women's empowerment programs challenging patriarchal traditions; and serves as advocacy platform for Akha indigenous rights in Myanmar where ethnic minorities face systematic discrimination and armed conflict.

Myanmar (formerly Burma) is one of Asia's newest coffee origins—coffee cultivation began only in the 1980s-1990s, introduced by United Nations programs as opium alternative in Shan State where ethnic minority groups (Akha, Lahu, Lisu, Wa, Palaung, and others) cultivated opium poppies for heroin trade. Despite decades of civil war, military dictatorship, extreme poverty, and international isolation, Myanmar's high-altitude coffee from Shan State has emerged since 2010 as specialty origin with exceptional potential—clean, bright, complex profiles reflecting volcanic soils, monsoon climate, and extreme altitude cultivation. Akha Ama stands at forefront of this emergence, demonstrating that Myanmar coffee can achieve world-class quality (87-91 points) while serving as vehicle for indigenous empowerment, women's leadership, conflict transformation, and cultural preservation.

## Location and Geography

### Eastern Shan State, Golden Triangle Region

Shan State occupies Myanmar's eastern highlands—vast mountainous plateau bordering China (north), Laos (east), and Thailand (southeast), forming part of the notorious "Golden Triangle" historically dominant in opium production. The state is Myanmar's largest (155,000 km²) and most ethnically diverse, home to Shan majority plus dozens of ethnic minority groups including Akha, Wa, Palaung, Lahu, Lisu, Kachin, and others who have inhabited these mountains for centuries, practicing swidden agriculture, maintaining distinct languages and cultures, and experiencing decades of armed conflict between ethnic armies, Myanmar military, and drug militias.

**Akha Ama Cooperative Territory**:
The cooperative operates primarily in Pang Kham and Mong Hsu townships, remote mountainous areas in southern Shan State approximately 100 kilometers southeast of Taunggyi (Shan State capital):

- **Elevation**: Member villages 1,200-1,800 MASL (3,900-5,900 feet), coffee cultivation primarily 1,300-1,700m
- **Geographic position**: 15-25 km from Thai border; remote valleys accessible only by dirt tracks
- **Topography**: Steep mountain slopes (40-70% gradients), narrow valleys, rushing streams
- **Accessibility**: 6-8 hour drive from Taunggyi on poor dirt roads frequently damaged by monsoon rains and landslides; member villages often 1-3 hour walk from nearest vehicle-accessible point
- **Security context**: Region affected by sporadic armed conflict between Myanmar military and ethnic armed organizations (Shan State Army, other groups); 2021 military coup increased instability

**Climate**:
Eastern Shan State experiences tropical monsoon highland climate:

- **Temperature**: 15-28°C (59-82°F) average, cooler at highest elevations and during winter
- **Monsoon season**: May-October (southwest monsoon), 1,200-2,000mm annual rainfall
  - Peak June-August, heavy rains, frequent mist
  - Harvest season overlaps late monsoon (challenging for natural/honey processing)
- **Dry season**: November-April, mild temperatures, occasional cold snaps
  - Coffee harvest November-March (peak December-February)
  - Excellent conditions for drying
- **Humidity**: High during monsoon (80-95%), moderate dry season (50-70%)
- **Mist and fog**: Frequent morning fog year-round, creating cloud forest microclimate

**Topography**:
- **Steep slopes**: 40-70% gradients typical, coffee planted on near-vertical mountainsides
- **Forest remnants**: Mixed evergreen-deciduous mountain forest (heavily cleared but some areas remain)
- **Watersheds**: Streams feeding Salween River system draining to Andaman Sea
- **Landslides**: Common during monsoon on deforested slopes
- **Roads**: Minimal—dirt tracks, often impassable during rains

**Soil**:
- **Type**: Mountain soils derived from limestone and granite parent material, red-brown clay-loam
- **Fertility**: Moderate natural fertility, declining from decades of swidden agriculture, opium cultivation without soil conservation
- **pH**: Slightly acidic (5.5-6.5), suitable for coffee
- **Drainage**: Excellent on steep slopes (critical for quality)
- **Organic matter**: High in forested areas (4-8%), low in degraded areas (1-3%)
- **Erosion**: Severe problem on deforested slopes without conservation measures

**Ecosystem and Conservation Context**:
- **Biodiversity hotspot**: Indo-Burma biodiversity hotspot, high endemism
- **Deforestation**: Extensive clearing for opium, subsistence agriculture, logging
- **Wildlife**: Tigers (extremely rare), elephants (occasional), gibbons, diverse birds
- **Opium legacy**: Historical slash-and-burn for opium poppies devastated forests
- **Coffee as conservation**: Shade-grown coffee provides perennial crop alternative, reducing pressure for further clearing

### Akha People and Cultural Context

**Akha Indigenous Group**:
The Akha (self-designation: "Akha" or "Aka") are Tibeto-Burman ethnic minority originally from Yunnan Province, southern China, who migrated to Myanmar, Thailand, Laos, and northern Vietnam over past 200-300 years:

- **Population in Myanmar**: ~300,000 Akha in Shan State (estimates vary; census data unreliable)
- **Language**: Akha language (Tibeto-Burman family), tonal, distinct from Burmese and Shan; traditionally unwritten but now using Roman alphabet for literacy
- **Religion**: Traditional animist beliefs (ancestor worship, spirit propitiation, agricultural ceremonies) with some Christian conversion (missionaries active among hill tribes)
- **Social structure**: Patrilineal clans, village leadership by elders and headmen, strong communal identity
- **Traditional dress**: Distinctive elaborate headdresses adorned with silver coins, beads, and seeds; black clothing with colorful embroidery (women's traditional dress)
- **Marginalization**: Akha face severe discrimination in Myanmar—denied citizenship rights, excluded from education in Burmese language, politically marginalized, culturally suppressed

**Traditional Livelihoods**:
- **Swidden agriculture**: Slash-and-burn rotating cultivation (clear forest, farm 2-3 years, fallow 10-15 years), growing upland rice, corn, vegetables
- **Opium cultivation**: Historically primary cash crop—Akha villages deep in Golden Triangle opium production from 1950s-1990s
- **Subsistence farming**: Rice, corn, vegetables, small livestock (pigs, chickens) for survival
- **Forest products**: Hunting, gathering bamboo shoots, medicinal plants, mushrooms

**Opium Crisis and Akha Villages**:
- **1950s-1990s**: Akha villages throughout Shan State cultivated opium poppies as sole cash crop
  - Opium extremely profitable (10-20x value of other crops)
  - Opium traders provided credit, tools, supplies
  - Entire economy centered on opium
- **1990s-2000s**: Myanmar government (under international pressure) began opium eradication campaigns
  - Military crackdowns, forced eradication
  - Violence, arrests, crop destruction
  - Akha villages left without income source, facing extreme poverty
  - UN and NGO programs introduced coffee, tea as opium alternatives
- **Akha Ama context**: Cooperative formed specifically as response to opium eradication—providing viable alternative cash crop enabling Akha families to survive without opium

## Historical Context

### Myanmar Coffee History and Opium Alternative Programs

**Coffee Introduction (1980s-1990s)**:
Coffee is relatively new to Myanmar:

- **1980s**: United Nations International Drug Control Programme (UNDCP, later UNODC) introduced coffee to Shan State as opium alternative
- **Initial introduction**: Arabica Catimor varieties (high-yield, rust-resistant) provided to Akha, Lahu, Palaung villages participating in alternative development programs
- **1990s**: Coffee cultivation expanded slowly; poor quality, no market access, minimal training
- **Early challenges**: Farmers unfamiliar with coffee cultivation; processing knowledge absent; Myanmar isolated internationally (sanctions, dictatorship), limiting export markets
- **2000s**: Coffee production remained small-scale, low-quality, primarily sold to local intermediaries at minimal prices

**Myanmar Political Context**:
- **1962-2011**: Military dictatorship under various juntas, Burma/Myanmar internationally isolated, sanctioned
- **Ethnic conflicts**: Armed conflicts between Myanmar military and ethnic armies (Shan, Kachin, Karen, Wa, others) ongoing since 1948 independence
- **2011-2015**: Political liberalization, partial civilian government (Thein Sein), some sanctions lifted
- **2016-2021**: Democratic government under Aung San Suu Kyi, continued liberalization, economic growth
- **February 2021**: Military coup, return to military dictatorship, brutal crackdowns, civil disobedience, armed resistance
- **Coffee sector impacts**: Political instability, conflict, sanctions limit international investment, market access; security situation restricts movement, threatens farmers

**Specialty Coffee Emergence (2010s)**:
Despite political challenges, Myanmar specialty coffee emerged 2010-present:

- **2010-2015**: Small-scale specialty coffee production begins; international buyers discover Myanmar's quality potential
- **Cup of Excellence style competitions**: Some Myanmar coffees score 85-88 points, attracting attention
- **Market access**: Improved political situation (2011-2020) enabled direct trade relationships, specialty exports
- **Shan State focus**: Almost all Myanmar Arabica from Shan State (Palaung, Akha, Lahu, Danu producers)
- **Akha Ama leadership**: Akha Ama becomes Myanmar's most internationally recognized specialty coffee brand

### Akha Ama Foundation and Growth

**Lee Ayu Chuepa—Founder**:
Akha Ama's story is inseparable from its founder:

- **Background**: Lee Ayu Chuepa born 1980s in Akha village in Shan State, Myanmar; grew up in extreme poverty, opium-dependent village economy
- **Migration to Thailand**: As teenager/young woman, migrated to Thailand (like many Akha youth) seeking work; arrived in Chiang Mai, northern Thailand
- **Exposure to coffee culture**: Worked in Chiang Mai cafe, learned about specialty coffee, barista skills, coffee business
- **Vision**: Inspired to bring specialty coffee knowledge back to Akha communities—seeing coffee as vehicle for economic development, cultural preservation, Akha empowerment
- **2008-2009**: Began planning return to Myanmar, developing cooperative concept

**Cooperative Establishment (2010)**:
- **2010**: Lee returned to Shan State, began organizing Akha families in her home village and surrounding communities
- **Founding members**: 35 families committed to coffee cultivation, organic farming, quality focus
- **Model**: Social enterprise cooperative—member coffee producers + direct retail through cafes
- **First cafe**: Lee established first Akha Ama Coffee cafe in Chiang Mai, Thailand (2010)—serving coffee from Myanmar, creating direct market, raising awareness
- **Investment**: Minimal external funding; bootstrap model using cafe revenues to invest in Myanmar farmer support, processing infrastructure

**Growth and Development (2010-present)**:

- **2010-2013**: Membership grows to 80 families; processing facility built in main village; quality improvements through training
- **2014**: Second cafe opens (Yangon, Myanmar); increased visibility domestically and internationally
- **2015-2016**: Experimental processing begins (natural, honey, anaerobic); SL34 variety trials initiated
- **2017-2018**: International recognition—87-89 point coffees, specialty roaster partnerships worldwide
- **2019**: Membership reaches 150 families; community learning center established (Akha youth training in coffee, literacy, entrepreneurship)
- **2020**: COVID-19 challenges (cafes closed temporarily, tourism collapse, export disruptions)
- **2021-2023**: Military coup crisis—Chiang Mai cafe continues; Yangon cafe threatened by political instability; cooperative continues operating despite extreme difficulties
- **Present**: 180 families, 45-85 tons annually, 85-91 point coffees, internationally celebrated

**Unique Social Enterprise Model**:
Akha Ama differs from typical cooperatives:

1. **Vertical integration**: Farm → processing → roasting → retail cafes—cooperative controls entire value chain
2. **Producer ownership of retail**: Farmers are shareholders in cafes (unprecedented for Myanmar, rare globally)
3. **Cultural mission**: Explicit focus on Akha culture preservation, language, traditional knowledge
4. **Women's leadership**: Founded and led by woman; prioritizes women's empowerment, challenges patriarchal traditions
5. **Advocacy**: Platform for indigenous rights, ethnic minority visibility, peace-building in conflict-affected region

## Organization and Structure

### Cooperative Governance and Akha Community Leadership

**Membership**:
- **Total members**: 180 families (grew from 35 founding families in 2010)
- **Ethnicity**: 100% Akha indigenous
- **Villages**: Members from 12 villages across 25 km radius in Pang Kham and Mong Hsu areas
- **Women members**: 40% women members or women-headed households (high for Myanmar where land typically controlled by men)
- **Farm sizes**: 1-3 hectares coffee (2.5-7.5 acres); many members have additional subsistence crops
- **Requirements**: Organic farming practices, participate in training, deliver coffee to cooperative processing facility, commit to quality standards

**Governance Structure**:

1. **Founder-Director: Lee Ayu Chuepa**:
   - Lee serves as director, visionary leader, spokesperson
   - Provides strategic direction, market development, international relationships
   - Based primarily in Chiang Mai (cafe operations, international engagement) with regular visits to Myanmar villages

2. **Akha Community Council**:
   - 15-member council elected by members from different villages
   - Includes village headmen, elders, younger farmers, women representatives
   - Makes decisions on cooperative operations, farmer support, community investments
   - Conducted in Akha language with Burmese translation when needed
   - Consensus-oriented decision-making reflecting Akha community traditions

3. **Processing Manager**:
   - Oversees wet mill, dry mill, quality control
   - Manages seasonal workers during harvest
   - Coordinates collection from villages

4. **Field Coordinators** (4 staff):
   - Visit member farms providing technical assistance
   - Distribute seedlings, inputs
   - Monitor organic compliance
   - Collect harvest information

5. **Cafe Operations**:
   - Separate management for Chiang Mai cafe (Thailand) and Yangon cafe (Myanmar)
   - Some Akha youth from member families trained as baristas, employed in cafes
   - Cafes promote Akha culture, serve as advocacy platforms

**Unique Aspects**:
- **Women's leadership**: Lee's leadership and women-member emphasis unusual in patriarchal Akha society
- **Youth engagement**: Cooperative deliberately engages youth through training, barista employment, entrepreneurship programs
- **Cultural preservation**: Council meetings include Akha ceremonies, traditional decision-making, respect for elders
- **Bilingual operations**: All materials, training, meetings available in Akha language (not common in Myanmar where Burmese dominates)

## Coffee Cultivation

### Farm Characteristics and Organic Farming

**Typical Member Farm**:
- **Size**: 1-3 hectares coffee (2.5-7.5 acres)
- **Altitude**: Most farms 1,300-1,700m, some up to 1,800m
- **Slope**: 40-70% gradients, steep mountainsides
- **Tree density**: 2,000-3,500 trees per hectare depending on shade level, spacing
- **Tree age**: Mixed—older Catimor from 1990s UNDCP programs (20-30 years), younger replanted trees (5-15 years)
- **Shade**: 30-60% shade from retained forest trees, planted shade trees (Albizia, Erythrina), fruit trees (banana, avocado, lychee)
- **Intercropping**: Coffee often intercropped with vegetables, corn, upland rice (subsistence food security)

**Organic Farming Practices** (100% of members):
Akha Ama emphasizes organic farming despite not pursuing formal certification:

- **No synthetic inputs**: No chemical fertilizers, pesticides, herbicides (prohibited by cooperative; members too poor to afford anyway)
- **Soil fertility**: Composting (coffee pulp, crop residues, animal manure), green manure cover crops, forest leaf litter mulching
- **Pest management**: Manual removal of pests, encouraging beneficial insects (birds, wasps, ladybugs), botanical sprays (garlic-chili-neem preparations)
- **Disease management**: Pruning for airflow, removing diseased material, Catimor rust-resistance helps
- **Weed control**: Manual weeding with machetes, hand hoes; mulching suppresses weeds
- **Erosion control**: Contour planting, stone barriers, shade trees stabilizing soil on steep slopes

**Why No Organic Certification?**:
- **Cost prohibitive**: Certification fees $3,000-10,000 annually, beyond cooperative capacity
- **Administrative burden**: Paperwork, documentation, audits difficult in remote conflict-affected area
- **Market positioning**: Direct trade relationships and cafe sales don't require certification; buyers trust Akha Ama's organic claims
- **De facto organic**: Members farming organically by economic necessity and cooperative philosophy regardless of certification

**Traditional Akha Agricultural Knowledge**:
- **Forest spirits**: Akha animist beliefs revere forest spirits; encourages maintaining trees, sustainable forest use
- **Crop diversity**: Traditional Akha swidden agriculture emphasized diversity; reflected in intercropped coffee systems
- **Community labor**: Akha tradition of reciprocal labor (neighbors helping neighbors) applies during coffee harvest
- **Seasonal ceremonies**: Planting, harvest ceremonies honoring spirits, asking for good crops

**Cultivation Methods**:
- **Pruning**: Annual selective pruning; stumping rotation every 7-10 years to rejuvenate aging trees
- **Fertilization**: 2-3 compost applications per year (coffee pulp compost, animal manure)
- **Mulching**: Heavy mulching with organic matter (coffee pulp, rice husks, forest leaf litter) for moisture retention, weed suppression
- **Replanting**: Gradually replacing old Catimor with new seedlings (Catimor, SL34, Caturra) from cooperative nursery

**Labor**:
- **Family-based**: Husband, wife, children, extended family primary workforce
- **Gender roles**: Men manage pruning, heavy work; women central to selective picking, processing; children help during school breaks
- **Community labor**: During peak harvest, neighbors exchange labor following Akha reciprocal traditions
- **Youth**: Cooperative engages youth, positioning coffee farming as viable career (not just parents' hard labor)

**Challenges**:
- **Steep terrain**: All farm operations difficult on 40-70% slopes
- **Remote access**: Carrying inputs up mountains, carrying coffee down extremely labor-intensive; some farms 2-3 hour walk from road
- **Climate variability**: Irregular monsoons, occasional droughts affecting yields
- **Coffee leaf rust**: Increasing pressure, especially at lower elevations
- **Conflict insecurity**: Military-ethnic armed group clashes occasionally restrict movement, threaten farmers
- **Land tenure insecurity**: Akha lack official land titles (Myanmar denies citizenship, land rights); vulnerable to land grabs

### Varieties

**Variety Distribution**:

1. **Catimor** (60-65% of production):
   - Introduced 1980s-1990s by UNDCP opium alternative programs
   - Rust-resistant hybrid (Caturra x Timor), vigorous growth
   - High yields (1,200-2,000 kg/ha) critical for farmers transitioning from opium
   - Good quality when carefully processed (84-87 points, occasionally 88)
   - Dominates production for economic security, rust resistance in humid monsoon climate
   - Farmers familiar with Catimor (30 years cultivation experience)

2. **SL34** (20-25% and growing):
   - Kenyan variety experimentally introduced by Akha Ama 2015-2016
   - Sister to SL28, similar exceptional quality potential (86-90 points)
   - Complex fruit (blackcurrant, berry), floral, winey, bright acidity
   - Moderate yields (900-1,500 kg/ha), rust-susceptible but manageable at high altitude
   - Cooperative aggressively expanding SL34—distinctive quality, market differentiation
   - "Myanmar SL34" creates unique market positioning (rare variety in unusual origin)

3. **Caturra** (10-12%):
   - Dwarf Bourbon mutation, compact trees
   - Excellent quality (85-88 points), easier harvest (shorter trees)
   - Moderate yields (900-1,400 kg/ha)
   - Introduced 2010s by cooperative for quality-productivity balance

4. **Typica** (3-5%):
   - Traditional variety, limited introduction 2010s
   - Exceptional quality potential (86-90 points) at highest altitudes
   - Low yields (700-1,200 kg/ha), very rust-susceptible
   - Maintained in small quantities for ultra-premium micro-lots

**Variety Strategy**:
- **Catimor foundation**: Majority Catimor for reliable yields, rust resistance, economic stability
- **SL34 expansion**: Aggressively promoting SL34 for quality differentiation, premium market positioning
- **Quality focus**: Expanding Caturra, limited Typica at highest altitudes for specialty micro-lots
- **Risk management**: Maintaining variety diversity reduces disease vulnerability, climate risk

**SL34 as Signature**:
SL34 is iconic Kenyan variety rarely grown outside East Africa. Akha Ama's focus on Myanmar SL34 creates remarkable market opportunity:

- **Novelty**: "Myanmar SL34" extremely unusual—buyers intrigued by Kenyan variety in Southeast Asian terroir
- **Quality**: 87-90 points achieved, matching Kenyan SL34 quality standards
- **Flavor uniqueness**: Myanmar terroir (monsoon climate, limestone-granite soils, altitude) creates distinctive expression—Kenyan brightness with tropical fruit notes
- **Market positioning**: Specialty buyers seeking rare varieties, origin diversity excited by Myanmar SL34
- **Premium pricing**: SL34 micro-lots commanding $5-9/lb green vs $2.50-3.50 standard Catimor

## Coffee Processing

### Washed Processing (Primary Method)

Approximately 70-75% of Akha Ama coffee is fully washed, producing clean, bright profiles showcasing Myanmar's terroir.

**Harvest**:
- **Season**: November-March (peak December-February)
- **Selective picking**: Only ripe red cherries, 3-5 passes through farms
- **Collection**: Farmers bring cherry to cooperative processing facility within 24 hours of picking (many carry 20-40 kg loads 1-3 hours from farms to facility)
- **Quality training**: Cooperative extensively trains pickers on cherry selection—underripe, overripe, defects rejected at reception

**Wet Processing at Cooperative Facility**:

1. **Cherry Reception and Sorting**:
   - Visual inspection by cooperative staff, defect removal
   - Flotation in water tanks: floaters (underripe, dried, defects) removed, sinkers (quality cherries) continue
   - Cherries weighed, farmers credited

2. **Pulping**:
   - Mix of hand pulpers (traditional, labor-intensive) and small mechanical pulpers (limited electricity availability)
   - Careful calibration to avoid damaging beans
   - Pulp collected for composting, returned to members as fertilizer

3. **Fermentation**:
   - Mucilage-covered beans fermented in concrete tanks 14-24 hours
   - Fermentation time varies by temperature (shorter during warmer periods, longer during cool winter)
   - Natural fermentation (ambient microbes) breaks down sticky mucilage
   - Staff monitor fermentation progress—rubbing beans, checking mucilage breakdown, preventing over-fermentation

4. **Washing**:
   - Fermented beans washed in channels with clean stream water (mountain spring-fed)
   - Manual agitation removes loosened mucilage
   - Multiple rinses until water clear
   - Density sorting during washing (floaters removed)

5. **Drying**:
   - Washed parchment spread on raised beds (African-style mesh screen tables)
   - Thin layers (2-3 cm) for even airflow, consistent drying
   - 12-18 days to 10.5-11.5% moisture content (favorable dry season conditions)
   - Turned every 2-3 hours when sun-drying
   - Covered at night and if unexpected rain (rare during dry season harvest but occasionally happens)
   - Moisture content carefully monitored with meters

6. **Dry Parchment Rest**:
   - Rested 30-60 days in parchment to stabilize moisture, mellow flavors

**Quality Control**:
- **Hulling**: Parchment mechanically removed at cooperative dry mill
- **Grading**: Screen sorted by size, density sorted (manual flotation, gravity separation)
- **Hand-sorting**: Multiple rounds by trained women workers (predominantly from member families) removing defects, discolored beans
- **Cupping**: Lee and trained staff cup all export lots, identify exceptional micro-lots, verify no defects

### Natural and Honey Processing

Since 2015, Akha Ama has aggressively developed natural and honey processing (now 25-30% of production):

**Natural Processing**:
- Whole cherries dried intact on raised beds
- 22-32 day drying with frequent turning
- Requires perfect weather—Myanmar's dry season (December-March) ideal, but late harvest cherries (February-March) risk monsoon onset
- Produces fruit-forward, fuller body, berry-winey profile (86-90 points when successful)
- Higher risk but higher market premiums justify ($4-7/lb vs $2.50-3.50 washed)

**Honey Processing**:
- Pulped cherries dried with mucilage partially (yellow honey) or fully (red/black honey) intact
- 14-20 day drying with frequent turning
- Intermediate profile: more body/fruit than washed, cleaner than natural (85-89 points)
- Less risky than full natural, increasingly popular with specialty buyers

**Anaerobic and Experimental Fermentation** (2016-present):

Akha Ama has pioneered experimental fermentation in Myanmar:

1. **Anaerobic Fermentation**:
   - Whole cherries or pulped beans fermented in sealed plastic barrels (limiting oxygen)
   - 36-72 hour fermentation, temperature monitoring
   - Creates intense fruit, floral, winey, funky complexity
   - 88-91 points when successful, significant defect risk
   - Limited quantities (100-500 kg annually) for ultra-premium market

2. **Extended Fermentation**:
   - Washed processing with extended fermentation time (48-72 hours vs standard 14-24)
   - Develops deeper fruit, winey notes
   - 87-89 points, more reliable than full anaerobic

3. **Carbonic Maceration** (experimental):
   - Whole cherries in CO2 environment (inspired by wine production)
   - 4-7 days in sealed containers
   - Creates unique tropical fruit, bubble gum, floral notes
   - Very limited experimental batches (87-90 points)

**Processing Innovation as Strategy**:
- Position Akha Ama as Myanmar's processing innovation leader
- Differentiate from commodity Robusta (Myanmar primarily Robusta producer—Arabica <2% of national production)
- Attract specialty buyers seeking unique profiles, experimental coffees
- Create premium micro-lots commanding $6-12/lb (vs $2.50-3.50 standard washed)
- Generate excitement, international recognition for Myanmar coffee

## Quality and Market Position

### Quality Profile

**Cupping Scores**: Akha Ama coffees score 85-91 points on SCA scale:
- **89-91 points**: Exceptional experimental processing, SL34 micro-lots, perfect execution
- **87-88 points**: Premium SL34 and high-altitude Caturra/Typica, careful processing
- **85-86 points**: Standard export quality Catimor, consistent washed processing

**Flavor Profile** (washed Catimor/Caturra, classic Akha Ama):
- **Aroma**: Tropical fruit (lychee, longan, rambutan), floral (jasmine, magnolia), citrus, honey
- **Acidity**: Bright, citric (lime, yuzu), malic (green apple), phosphoric (crisp)
- **Body**: Medium to medium-full, silky, juicy

## Social Impact and Community Transformation

### Economic Transformation—Opium to Coffee

**Before Akha Ama** (pre-2010):
- Akha villages dependent on opium cultivation
- Opium eradication (1990s-2000s) left villages in extreme poverty
- Coffee introduced as alternative but low quality, minimal market access, insufficient income
- Annual household income: $150-300 from low-quality coffee sold to intermediaries at $0.15-0.25/lb cherry equivalent
- Malnutrition, lack of education, health crises common

**After Akha Ama** (2010-present):
- **Coffee income**: $800-2,000 per family annually (quality-dependent)
- **3-6x income increase** vs pre-cooperative
- **Stable market**: Direct trade and cafe sales provide reliable buyer, consistent pricing
- **Quality premiums**: SL34 and experimental processing enable some families to earn $2,000-3,500 annually
- **Food security**: Higher income enables purchasing rice, food during lean seasons
- **Asset accumulation**: Families investing in home improvements (iron roofs, solar panels), motorbikes, children's education

**Opium Elimination**:
- 100% of member families eliminated opium cultivation
- Coffee provides viable alternative income source
- Demonstrates sustainable crop substitution success
- Model for other ethnic minority villages in Shan State

**Household-Level Impacts**:
- Improved housing (replacing bamboo/thatch with wooden homes, iron roofs)
- Better nutrition (purchasing meat, fish, vegetables)
- School enrollment (children no longer required to work full-time)
- Health care access (ability to pay for clinic visits, medicine)
- Dignity and pride (moving from stigmatized opium farmers to respected coffee producers)

## Environmental Sustainability

### Organic Shade-Grown Coffee and Forest Conservation

**Environmental Legacy of Opium**:
Opium cultivation devastated forests:
- Slash-and-burn clearing for opium fields
- Short cultivation cycles (2-3 years) requiring continuous new clearing
- Steep slope cultivation causing severe erosion
- No soil conservation, rapid land degradation
- Chemical contamination (opium processing chemicals, herbicides)

**Coffee as Environmental Improvement**:
Coffee cultivation represents environmental improvement vs opium:

- **Perennial crop**: Coffee trees produce 20-40 years, eliminating constant forest clearing
- **Shade systems**: 30-60% shade maintaining tree cover, forest structure
- **Organic farming**: No chemical fertilizers, pesticides eliminating contamination
- **Soil building**: Composting, mulching, organic matter building soil health
- **Erosion control**: Perennial trees, contour planting, shade trees stabilizing steep slopes

**Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services**:
Akha Ama's shade-grown organic coffee systems provide:

- **Wildlife habitat**: Shade coffee provides corridor habitat for birds, mammals, insects between forest fragments
- **Forest connectivity**: Coffee farms connect remaining forest patches in heavily cleared landscape
- **Carbon sequestration**: Trees and organic soil sequester carbon (estimated 60-100 tons CO2/hectare)
- **Water protection**: No chemical runoff contaminating streams
- **Soil conservation**: Preventing erosion that sediments streams, degrades watersheds

**Species Observed in Coffee Farms**:
- Birds: 80-120 species including resident and migratory species
- Mammals: Civets, porcupines, occasional wild boar
- Insects: High diversity including pollinators (bees, butterflies)
- Amphibians: Frogs, salamanders in shaded, moist coffee zones

**Agroforestry and Shade Trees**:
Cooperative promotes diverse shade species:
- **Nitrogen-fixing trees**: Albizia, Erythrina (improve soil fertility)
- **Native forest trees**: Retained during land clearing or replanted (conservation value)
- **Fruit trees**: Banana, avocado, lychee (food, supplementary income)
- **Timber trees**: Teak, other hardwoods (long-term investment, building materials)

**Limitations and Challenges**:
- **Already cleared land**: Coffee grows on land already deforested for opium, swidden agriculture (not causing new clearing but not restoring full forest)
- **Biodiversity vs original forest**: Coffee agroforestry lower biodiversity than intact forest (but far better than annual crops, pasture)
- **Limited conservation resources**: Cooperative focused on livelihoods, lacks resources for intensive conservation programs
- **Hunting pressure**: Some wildlife hunting continues (subsistence, crop protection)

**Conservation Potential**:
- Coffee income reduces pressure for expanding agriculture into remaining forests
- If coffee provides sufficient livelihood, families less likely to clear forest for additional swidden fields
- Shade coffee creates economic value for maintaining trees vs cutting all trees for sun-intensive crops
- Model for conservation-integrated agriculture in conflict-affected biodiversity hotspots

### Climate Change Adaptation

**Climate Threats in Shan State**:
- **Temperature increase**: ~1.0°C warming over 30 years, accelerating
- **Monsoon variability**: Irregular onset, mid-season dry spells, excessive rainfall events
- **Extreme weather**: More intense storms, flooding, landslides during monsoon
- **Drought**: Occasional severe droughts (2015, 2019) affecting coffee flowering, yields
- **Pest/disease**: Coffee leaf rust expanding to higher elevations, coffee berry borer pressure increasing

**Adaptation Strategies**:

1. **Altitude and Microclimate**:
   - Shan State's 1,200-1,800m elevation provides altitude buffer as temperatures rise
   - Can shift cultivation higher if needed (land available up to 2,000m)
   - Shade trees buffer temperature extremes, reduce heat stress

2. **Variety Selection**:
   - Maintaining Catimor for rust resistance
   - SL34, Caturra, Typica at highest altitudes where rust pressure lower
   - Variety diversity reduces risk (if one variety fails, others may survive)

3. **Shade Intensification**:
   - Increasing shade cover to 40-60% to buffer temperature, reduce water stress
   - Diverse shade species providing resilience

4. **Water Management**:
   - Mulching heavily to retain soil moisture during dry periods
   - Some members experimenting with water harvesting, storage (limited infrastructure)
   - Cooperative considering irrigation system for future (currently no irrigation)

5. **Soil Health**:
   - Organic matter building improves soil water retention, drought resilience
   - Composting, mulching increase soil organic matter from 1-3% to 4-8%

6. **Diversification**:
   - Intercropped fruit trees, vegetables provide income insurance if coffee affected by climate extremes
   - Traditional Akha crop diversity (corn, rice, beans) as climate buffer

7. **Processing Flexibility**:
   - Natural, honey processing less water-dependent than washed (adaptation if water scarce)
   - Covered drying infrastructure protects from irregular rains

**Carbon Sequestration and Climate Finance Potential**:
- Shade coffee systems sequester 60-100 tons CO2/ha (trees + soil)
- Cooperative exploring carbon credit programs (voluntary carbon markets) to fund climate adaptation investments
- Potential revenue: $5-15/ton CO2 = $300-1,500/ha over 10 years
- Could finance irrigation, shade tree expansion, variety trials, climate-resilient infrastructure

## Challenges and Future Outlook

### Critical Challenges

**1. Myanmar Political Instability and Military Coup**:
- **February 2021 coup**: Military seized power, ending democratic government
- **Civil disobedience, armed resistance**: Protests, strikes, armed conflict erupted nationwide
- **Shan State conflict**: Intensified fighting between military and ethnic armed organizations
- **Impacts on Akha Ama**:
  - Yangon cafe threatened by instability, crackdowns
  - Export logistics disrupted (transportation, banking, customs complications)
  - International buyers concerned about Myanmar sourcing (ethical concerns, sanctions, logistics)
  - Farmer security threatened by increased military operations
  - Future highly uncertain

**2. Conflict and Security in Shan State**:
- Decades of armed conflict between Myanmar military and ethnic armies (Shan State Army, other groups)
- Sporadic fighting restricts movement, threatens farmers
- Land mines in some areas
- Drug militias still active in parts of Golden Triangle
- Farmers vulnerable to forced labor, extortion by armed groups
- Coup exacerbated insecurity

**3. Ethnic Discrimination and Lack of Citizenship**:
- Akha denied Myanmar citizenship (classified as "foreigners" despite centuries in region)
- Without citizenship: no legal land titles, no access to schools, no freedom of movement, no political rights
- Vulnerable to land grabs, forced relocation by government or military
- Children cannot attend Burmese-language schools (language barrier, discrimination)
- Extremely difficult to travel internationally, access government services

**4. Small Scale and Market Access**:
- Production small (45-85 tons annually), insufficient for large roasters
- Cooperative lacks capital for major infrastructure expansion
- Myanmar's unknown origin status requires constant buyer education
- Landlocked, poor infrastructure—export logistics expensive, slow
- Sanctions, banking restrictions complicate international transactions

**5. Climate Change and Disease Pressure**:
- Climate variability threatens yields, quality
- Coffee leaf rust increasing, especially at lower elevations
- Limited resources for adaptation (irrigation, resistant varieties)
- Farmers vulnerable to weather-related crop failures

**6. Competition from Thai Specialty Coffee**:
- Thailand's specialty coffee sector growing rapidly (Doi Chaang, other producers)
- Thai coffee better known, easier logistics, no conflict concerns
- Akha Ama competes with Thai Akha cooperatives (some buyers may choose Thai origin for simplicity)

**7. Sustainability of Vertical Integration Model**:
- Operating cafes in Thailand (Chiang Mai) and Myanmar (Yangon) complex, capital-intensive
- Cafe profitability varies (tourism-dependent, competitive markets)
- Coup threatens Yangon cafe viability
- Question: Can cooperative sustain retail operations or should focus on green bean production?
