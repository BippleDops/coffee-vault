**Vision**:
"To empower indigenous Bunong communities through specialty coffee, preserving culture and environment while creating economic opportunity in post-conflict Cambodia's emerging coffee region."

**Historical Context - Cambodia & Coffee**:

**Colonial Era**: French introduced coffee to Cambodia in late 1800s, primarily robusta in lowlands.

**Khmer Rouge Era** (1975-1979):
Cambodia's coffee industry, like all agriculture, was devastated by Khmer Rouge genocide:
- Coffee farms destroyed or abandoned
- Agricultural knowledge lost
- Infrastructure destroyed
- Population displacement and death
- Coffee industry essentially ceased

**Post-Conflict Recovery** (1990s-2000s):
- Gradual agricultural recovery
- Coffee primarily robusta, commodity focus
- Limited specialty coffee knowledge
- Mondulkiri Province (highlands) emerged as potential arabica region

**Mondulkiri Project Foundation** (2012):

Luke and Pauline McKenna (Australian/New Zealand) moved to Mondulkiri Province:
- Observed indigenous Bunong people growing coffee (wild arabica, low knowledge)
- Recognized potential for specialty arabica (altitude, climate)
- Established social enterprise to support Bunong farmers
- Combined coffee quality with community development
- Pioneered Cambodia's specialty arabica emergence

**Organizational Structure**:

**Social Enterprise Team**:
- **Founders/Directors**: Luke and Pauline McKenna

## üåø Regional & Farm Characteristics

**Mondulkiri Province**:
- Northeastern Cambodia (borders Vietnam)
- Highlands (800-1,200m altitude)
- Sparsely populated (indigenous Bunong majority in rural areas)
- Forested, mountainous terrain
- Cooler climate than lowland Cambodia
- Cambodia's only viable arabica region

**Altitude Range**: 800-1,200 meters
**Climate**: Tropical monsoon (wet season May-Oct, dry season Nov-April)
**Soil Type**: Basalt volcanic soils (ancient volcanic activity), red laterite
**Terrain**: Rolling hills, forested areas
**Temperature**: Cooler than lowlands (altitude), suitable for arabica

**Why Mondulkiri for Coffee**:
1. **Altitude**: 800-1,200m (marginal but viable for arabica)
2. **Climate**: Distinct dry season (harvest timing)
3. **Soil**: Volcanic basalt, fertile
4. **Temperature**: Cool enough for arabica (vs lowland Cambodia)
5. **Forest**: Shade and biodiversity
6. **Indigenous Knowledge**: Bunong traditional forest coffee

**Production Scale**:
- **Regional Production**: 500-1,000+ tons annually (growing, mostly robusta)
- **Mondulkiri Project**: 15-30 tons specialty arabica annually (small but growing)
- **Farmer Network**: 150-200+ families
- **Farm Size**: 1-3 hectares coffee per family
- **Harvest Season**: November - February (dry season)

**Typical Bunong Smallholder Farm**:
- **Size**: 1-3 hectares coffee (often forest garden style)
- **Production**: 200-800 kg green coffee annually
- **Other Crops**: Rice, fruit trees, vegetables (subsistence and income)
- **Labor**: Family labor (occasional hired help at harvest)
- **Processing**: Historically on-farm (now centralized through Mondulkiri Project)
- **Income**: Coffee increasingly important cash crop

**Forest Garden Coffee**:
Many Bunong farms practice traditional forest garden agriculture:
- Coffee under native forest canopy (shade-grown)
- Intercropped with fruit trees (banana, papaya, others)
- Minimal clearing (integrating coffee into forest)
- Low-input, organic practices
- Biodiversity-rich
- Traditional Bunong agricultural practice

**Mondulkiri Project Support**:
- Technical assistance (pruning, fertilization, pest management)
- Quality training (selective picking, processing)
- Input subsidies (organic fertilizer, equipment)
- Processing infrastructure (centralized wet mill)
- Market access (direct trade, export)
- Fair pricing (above market rates)

## üî¨ Processing (Centralized Quality Control)

**Processing Evolution**:

**Pre-Project** (Before 2012):
- Farmers processed on-farm (limited knowledge)
- Variable quality (inconsistent drying, fermentation)
- Defects common
- Low prices due to quality issues

**Post-Project** (2012-Present):
- Centralized wet mill (Mondulkiri Project facility)
- Quality control systems
- Farmer training
- Consistent processing protocols
- Specialty quality achievement

**Processing Infrastructure**:

**Mondulkiri Project Wet Mill**:
- **Depulping**: Mechanical cherry skin removal
- **Fermentation**: Controlled fermentation (12-18 hours, water management)
- **Washing**: Clean water rinse, density sorting
- **Drying**: African raised beds, parabolic dryers
- **Storage**: Parchment storage (climate controlled as possible)
- **Hulling**: Dry milling before export (contract basis or export partners)

**Standard Processing Methods**:

1. **Washed** (70%):
   - Depulping, fermentation, washing
   - Clean, bright profile
   - Cambodian specialty standard
   - Quality control and consistency

2. **Natural** (20%):
   - Whole cherry drying
   - Fruit-forward profiles
   - Weather-dependent (dry season only)
   - Experimental and specialty lots

3. **Experimental** (10%):
   - Honey processing trials
   - Extended fermentation
   - Processing innovation
   - Market differentiation

**Quality Control Systems**:

**Cherry Collection**:
- Farmers bring cherry to collection points (multiple villages)
- Visual inspection (ripeness, defects)
- Cherry floatation (density sorting)
- Payment based on quality (incentive)

**Processing Monitoring**:
- Fermentation timing and temperature control
- Washing quality (clean water, complete mucilage removal)
- Drying moisture monitoring (target 10-12%)
- Turning schedules (raised beds every 2-3 hours)
- Weather adaptation (covering when needed)

**Post-Processing**:
- Sample roasting and cupping (every lot)
- Defect counting and removal
- Grading and classification
- Traceability (village, farmer group, variety, processing)
- Pre-export cupping

**Processing Challenges**:

1. **Climate**: Humid, rain during potential drying (timing critical)
2. **Infrastructure**: Limited, developing (electricity, roads)
3. **Water**: Clean water access (wet processing requirement)
4. **Knowledge**: Farmer training ongoing (quality understanding)
5. **Scale**: Small volume (efficiency challenges)

**Processing Innovation**:
- Experimenting with alternative methods (honey, anaerobic)
- Adapting to Cambodian climate (dry season timing critical)
- Quality improvement (training, infrastructure)
- Differentiation for specialty market

## üìù Producer Story

**Cambodia's Coffee History**:

Coffee arrived in Cambodia during French colonial era (late 1800s), primarily robusta in lowlands. By mid-1900s, Cambodia had modest coffee production.

**Khmer Rouge Devastation** (1975-1979):
During Khmer Rouge genocide, Cambodia's agricultural sector was decimated:
- 1.5-2 million deaths (25% of population)
- Forced relocation, collectivization
- Coffee farms destroyed or abandoned
- Agricultural knowledge lost
- Infrastructure destroyed
- Economy collapsed

**Post-Conflict Recovery** (1980s-2000s):
Cambodia slowly recovered:
- Agriculture rebuilt (rice focus)
- Coffee production resumed (mostly robusta, commodity)
- Specialty coffee minimal to non-existent
- Rural poverty deep, especially indigenous communities

**Indigenous Bunong & Mondulkiri**:

Mondulkiri Province (northeastern Cambodia) is homeland of indigenous Bunong people:
- Highland region (800-1,200m)
- Forested, remote
- Bunong traditional livelihoods: forest products, agriculture
- Marginalized, impoverished
- Some traditional coffee cultivation (small-scale, low knowledge)

**Mondulkiri Project Foundation** (2012):

Luke and Pauline McKenna (Australian/New Zealand backgrounds) moved to Mondulkiri Province:
- Observed Bunong farmers growing coffee (low quality, low prices)
- Recognized arabica potential (altitude, climate, soil)
- Saw opportunity for social impact (health, education, poverty)
- Established Mondulkiri Project as social enterprise

**Vision**: Support Bunong farmers to produce specialty coffee, creating sustainable income while funding community development programs.

**Initial Challenges** (2012-2015):
- Low coffee quality (processing knowledge limited)
- Infrastructure absent (wet mill, drying, storage)
- Farmer knowledge gaps (agronomy, quality)
- Market access zero (no specialty buyers)
- Community needs vast (health, education, infrastructure)

**Building Foundation** (2012-2015):
- Wet mill construction (centralized processing)
- Farmer training (selective picking, post-harvest)
- Quality systems (cupping, grading)
- Market development (roaster relationships, direct trade)
- Community programs launched (health, education)

**Quality Improvement Era** (2015-2020):
- Processing refinement (washed, natural experiments)
- Agronomy support (pruning, fertilization, disease management)
- Variety trials (Typica emphasis, Bourbon introduction)
- Quality scores improving (80-84+ points)
- Market expansion (more roaster partners)

**Current Era** (2020-Present):
- Established specialty supplier (consistent quality, 83-86+ points)
- Growing farmer network (150-200+ families)
- Community impact deepening (health, education, infrastructure)
- Market recognition (emerging origin interest)
- Sustainability focus (social enterprise model refinement)

**Impact Achieved**:
- **Economic**: 150-200+ families with improved coffee income
- **Health**: Mobile clinics, malaria reduction, maternal care
- **Education**: Schools built, literacy improved, scholarships
- **Infrastructure**: Roads, water systems, electricity access
- **Cultural**: Bunong language and traditions supported
- **Environmental**: Forest coffee preservation

**Philosophy**:
Mondulkiri Project's approach:
- "Coffee as a tool for community transformation"
- Indigenous empowerment and cultural respect
- Quality and impact both matter
- Long-term relationships (farmers, roasters, communities)
- Sustainable development (environmental, economic, social)
- Transparency and accountability

**Future Vision**:
- Sustainable social enterprise (coffee funding community programs indefinitely)
- Bunong community thriving (health, education, income, culture)
- Cambodian specialty coffee recognized globally
- Model for post-conflict agricultural development
- Indigenous people empowered with dignity and agency

**Emerging Origin Challenges & Potential**:
Cambodia faces challenges:
- Lower altitude (800-1,200m vs 1,400-2,000m+ for premier regions)
- Infrastructure limitations (processing, logistics)
- Market unfamiliarity (buyer education needed)

But demonstrates potential:
- Unique terroir (distinct from neighbors)
- Organic practices (traditional farming)
- Quality improvement trajectory (82-86+ points achieved)
- Origin diversity (beyond saturated regions)

**Post-Conflict Context**:
Cambodia's Khmer Rouge genocide (1975-1979) was one of 20th century's worst atrocities. Coffee's destruction during that era and slow recovery reflects national trauma. Mondulkiri Project represents post-conflict agricultural development and hope.

**Indigenous Rights & Coffee**:
Bunong people, like indigenous communities globally, face marginalization, land threats, and poverty. Coffee provides:
- Economic alternative (forest-friendly income)
- Land value (preservation incentive, rights strengthening)
- Cultural pride (traditional knowledge respected)
- Agency and empowerment (market participation)
