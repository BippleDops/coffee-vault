# Thanh Ha Cooperative

## Overview

Thanh Ha Cooperative represents Vietnam's ambitious transformation from the world's largest Robusta producer to a credible specialty Arabica origin. Established in 2005 in the Central Highlands' Da Lat region, the cooperative unites 3,200 smallholder families—many from ethnic minority groups—producing specialty Arabica coffees scoring 82-87 points that challenge perceptions of Vietnamese coffee and showcase the country's high-altitude terroir potential.

Vietnam is synonymous with Robusta coffee, producing 30-40% of global Robusta supply (nearly 2 million tons annually). However, the Central Highlands' mountainous regions, particularly around Da Lat (1,400-1,650 MASL), possess ideal conditions for high-quality Arabica cultivation: volcanic soils, cool temperatures, and abundant rainfall. Thanh Ha Cooperative has been instrumental in developing Vietnam's specialty Arabica sector, achieving Fair Trade (2007) and organic certification (2009), pioneering quality processing methods, and building direct trade relationships with specialty roasters seeking the "next" Asian origin story.

Beyond coffee quality, Thanh Ha exemplifies inclusive community development in Vietnam's ethnic minority highlands: 65% of members are from ethnic minority groups (K'Ho, Ma, Cham), women comprise 48% of membership, Fair Trade premiums have built schools and health clinics serving 15,000+ people, and organic agriculture protects the region's forests and watersheds. The cooperative's name "Thanh Ha"—meaning "green river" in Vietnamese—reflects the Central Highlands' pristine environment and the cooperative's commitment to sustainable agriculture.

## Location and Geography

### Regional Context

**Lam Dong Province**: Central Highlands of Vietnam, mountainous region centered around Da Lat city

**Da Lat City**: "City of Eternal Spring," mountain resort town (population ~250,000) at 1,500m elevation, renowned for cool climate, flowers, and increasingly, specialty coffee

**Altitude**: 1,400-1,650 meters above sea level
- Higher than most Vietnamese coffee (Robusta typically 400-800m)
- Cool highland climate ideal for Arabica
- Significant diurnal temperature variation (12-15°C swings)
- Elevation gradient creates diverse microclimates within region

**Microclimate**: Tropical highland monsoon climate
- November-March: Dry season, primary harvest period (18-25°C days, 12-16°C nights)
- April-May: Transition, some late harvest
- June-October: Monsoon season, heavy rains (22-28°C, humid)
- Annual rainfall: 1,800-2,500mm (concentrated in monsoon)
- Morning mist common year-round
- "Eternal spring" climate (cool year-round compared to lowland Vietnam)

### Terroir Characteristics

**Soil Composition**:
- Volcanic basalt soils (from ancient volcanic activity)
- Red laterite (iron-rich, characteristic of Central Highlands)
- Well-draining clay-loam
- High mineral content (iron, phosphorus, potassium)
- Moderate organic matter (4-7%, improving with organic practices)
- pH slightly acidic (5.0-6.0)

**Topography**:
- Rolling hills and mountain slopes (10-30 degree slopes typical)
- Plateau and valley systems
- Terracing employed on steeper slopes
- Pine forests on surrounding peaks

**Water Sources**:
- Mountain streams and rivers
- Spring water from forest watersheds
- Abundant water during monsoon, limited during dry season
- Irrigation infrastructure developed for dry season

**Biodiversity**:
- Central Highlands biodiversity hotspot (flora and fauna endemic species)
- Pine forests (planted colonial era for timber, now landscape feature)
- Native broadleaf forests in protected areas
- Wildlife: Primates, wild boar, civets, diverse birds
- Agricultural mosaic: Coffee, vegetables, flowers, forests

## Farm Characteristics

### Land Distribution

**Total Cultivated Area**: 4,800 hectares of coffee across member farms

**Average Farm Size**: 1.5 hectares per family
- Smaller plots: 0.5-1 hectare (ethnic minority farmers often have less land)
- Larger plots: 2-4 hectares (Kinh Vietnamese farmers typically)
- Intercropped with vegetables, fruit trees, upland rice

**Land Tenure**:
- Land allocation system (Vietnamese socialist land policy)
- "Red book" land certificates provide 50-year use rights
- Land can be inherited but not sold (technically state-owned)
- Ethnic minorities historically received less favorable land allocations
- Cooperative advocates for member land rights

### Cultivation Practices

**Transitioning Agriculture**:
- Historically intensive, chemical-dependent (Vietnam's agricultural model)
- Cooperative promoting organic and sustainable practices
- Organic certification (2009) required 3-year transition for all members
- Ongoing training to maintain and improve organic practices

**Agroforestry System** (40% shade coverage):
- Shade trees: Macadamia (nut income), avocado, lychee, longan (fruit income)
- Pine trees (existing landscape feature, some coffee under pine)
- Nitrogen-fixing trees introduced (Leucaena, Gliricidia)
- Intercropping: Vegetables (cabbage, lettuce), flowers (Da Lat specialty)
- Diversification for food security and income

**Organic Agriculture** (certified 2009):
- No synthetic pesticides, herbicides, or fertilizers
- Compost from coffee pulp and farm waste
- Limited manure availability (livestock not common in highlands)
- Botanical pest controls (neem, chili pepper sprays)
- Disease management through cultural practices
- Challenges: Transitioning from chemical-dependent model, soil fertility restoration

**Soil Management**:
- Compost application (2-5kg per tree annually, limited availability)
- Coffee pulp mulch returned to farms
- Cover crops during monsoon season
- Terracing on slopes for erosion control
- Organic matter building (goal: increase from 4-7% to 8-10%)

### Variety Selection

**Catimor** (60% of production):
- Vietnam's dominant Arabica variety (Timor hybrid with Robusta genetics)
- High yielding, rust-resistant, vigorous
- Moderate cup quality (82-84 points typical)
- Widely planted 1990s-2000s for productivity
- Foundation of commercial production

**Typica** (20% of production):
- Traditional heirloom variety
- Lower yielding but superior cup quality (84-87 points)
- Planted for quality differentiation
- Thrives at higher elevations

**Bourbon** (15% of production):
- Excellent cup quality and sweetness
- Moderate yield
- Cooperative promoting Bourbon expansion for quality improvement
- Member training on Bourbon cultivation

**Caturra** (5% of production):
- Compact variety, good quality
- Recently introduced for quality diversification
- Trial plantings expanding

**Variety Challenges**:
- Catimor dominance reflects Vietnam's historical focus on yield over quality
- Replanting with quality varieties slow (cost, lost production during establishment)
- Cooperative nursery program providing Bourbon and Typica seedlings (established 2012)
- Goal: Shift to 50% quality varieties (Typica, Bourbon) by 2030

### Agronomic Practices

**Planting Density**: 2,000-3,000 trees per hectare
- Moderate density for Vietnam (Robusta regions plant 1,000-1,500/ha)
- Allows intercropping and shade integration
- Spacing: 2-3 meters between rows, 1.2-1.8 meters between trees

**Pruning System**:
- Selective pruning after harvest
- Height management (2-2.5 meters)
- Single-stem or multi-stem systems (farmer preference)
- Stumping old trees (rehabilitation program)
- Training provided by cooperative field officers

**Fertilization** (organic):
- Compost: 2-5kg per tree annually (limited by availability)
- Coffee pulp mulch (100% returned to members)
- Foliar sprays with compost tea
- Challenges: Organic fertilizer production insufficient for optimal nutrition
- Yields lower than conventional (trade-off for organic premium and soil health)

**Integrated Pest Management**:
- Coffee leaf rust moderate pressure (monsoon humidity, but cool temperatures reduce severity)
- Coffee berry borer present
- Organic copper-based fungicides (limited use, expensive)
- Botanical insecticides (neem, chili)
- Cultural practices primary defense (pruning, sanitation, harvest timing)

### Harvest Management

**Harvest Season**: November-March (peak December-January)
- Vietnam's dry season
- Extended harvest due to elevation gradient and variety timing

**Selective Picking**:
- Training emphasizes ripe cherry selection (cultural shift from mass harvesting)
- Multiple passes through farms (2-4 passes typical, goal increase to 4-6)
- Quality incentive payments improving selectivity
- Family labor primary workforce
- Some hired labor during peak

**Labor Organization**:
- Family and kinship labor (traditional in ethnic minority communities)
- Community labor exchange
- Women primary harvesters (cultural norm)
- Payment incentives for quality cherries (tiered pricing introduced 2015)

**Cherry Collection**:
- Village collection points (28 sites)
- Daily delivery during harvest
- Initial quality sorting (floatation)
- Transport to washing stations
- Payment based on weight and quality

## Processing Methods

### Wet Mill Infrastructure

**Central Washing Station**: Da Lat facility (established 2006, upgraded 2015 with Fair Trade premium)

**Regional Washing Stations**: 3 smaller facilities across member villages
- Reduces transport distance
- Initial processing closer to farms
- Quality consolidated at central facility

**Total Capacity**: 40-50 tons cherry per day across facilities (peak harvest)

**Equipment**:
- Mechanical disc pulpers (water-efficient models)
- Fermentation tanks (concrete and tiled, 80,000 liters total)
- Washing channels (concrete, multi-stage)
- Drying infrastructure: Raised beds (limited), patio drying (primary), mechanical dryers (backup)
- Quality laboratory at central facility (cupping room, established 2016)

**Infrastructure Challenges**:
- Patio drying challenging during monsoon (mold risk if late harvest)
- Limited raised bed capacity (investment needed)
- Equipment maintenance (parts availability)
- Electricity reliable in Da Lat (better than rural Vietnam)

### Processing Protocols

**Washed Process** (70% of production):

Traditional Vietnamese washed processing with quality improvements:

1. **Reception and Sorting**:
   - Cherry delivered to collection points
   - Floatation tanks remove defects
   - Visual sorting
   - Processing within 12-24 hours

2. **Pulping**:
   - Mechanical disc pulpers
   - Pulp collected for composting and member distribution
   - Mucilage-covered parchment to fermentation

3. **Fermentation**:
   - Wet or dry fermentation (facility-dependent)
   - 18-30 hours depending on temperature
   - Cool highland temperatures require longer fermentation
   - pH and sensory monitoring at central facility

4. **Washing**:
   - Multiple washing stages
   - Manual or mechanical agitation removes mucilage
   - Density separation in channels
   - Clean water final rinse

5. **Drying**:
   - Patio drying primary method (concrete slabs)
   - Raised beds for premium lots (limited capacity)
   - 12-18 days to target moisture (11-12%, slightly higher than optimal due to climate challenges)
   - Frequent turning
   - Covered at night and during sudden rain
   - Mechanical dryers for finishing if needed

**Natural Process** (15% of production):
- Introduced 2014 for market differentiation
- Whole cherry drying on raised beds (limited capacity) or patios
- 18-25 days to target moisture
- Monitoring for over-fermentation (monsoon humidity risk)
- Successful naturals command premium pricing
- Quality variable (infrastructure and climate challenges)

**Honey Process** (10% of production):
- Introduced 2017, still developing expertise
- Red and yellow honey styles
- Mechanical demucilager removes portion of mucilage
- Extended drying time
- Premium pricing potential
- Learning curve ongoing

**Anaerobic Fermentation** (5% of production, experimental):
- Introduced 2020, cutting-edge for Vietnam
- Small-scale trials at central facility
- Sealed tanks, 48-72 hours
- Temperature control challenging (warm climate)
- Early results promising (84-86 points, unique profile)
- Knowledge shared with members and other cooperatives

### Quality Control

**Processing Standards**:
- SCA protocols adopted 2016
- 1 Q-Grader trained staff (2018, cooperative investment in training)
- Daily cupping during harvest at central facility
- Moisture content testing (every lot)
- Defect sorting at multiple stages

**Quality Challenges**:
- Inconsistent cherry quality from 3,200 members (education ongoing)
- Processing variability across 4 facilities (standardization improving)
- Infrastructure limitations (drying capacity, equipment)
- Monsoon season adds complexity (humidity, drying challenges)

**Parchment Storage**:
- Warehouse at central facility (climate-controlled room)
- GrainPro bags for moisture protection
- Pest control (insects, rodents)
- Regular moisture checks
- Rested 30-45 days before hulling

**Traceability**:
- Village-level lot tracking
- Washing station identification
- Processing method documentation
- Cupping scores recorded
- Microlot potential for highest-quality villages

### Defect Management

**Common Defects**:
- Fermentation defects from processing inconsistency (education improving)
- Quakers from Catimor variety and nutrition issues
- Moisture-related defects from climate challenges (storage, drying)
- Under-developed beans from some plots (nutrition, aging trees)

**Quality Improvement Efforts**:
- Cherry quality training (selective picking, timing)
- Processing standardization across facilities
- Storage improvements (GrainPro bags, moisture monitoring)
- Variety improvement (replanting with Bourbon, Typica)
- Sorting at multiple stages

**Green Coffee Standards**:
- Category 1 defects: Zero tolerance for export specialty lots
- Category 2 defects: Maximum 10 per 350g sample (working toward <5)
- Screen size: 15+ (70% of specialty production)
- Moisture content: 11-12% (slightly high due to climate, working to reduce to 10.5-11%)
- Water activity: <0.65
- Density sorting removes low-density beans

## Certifications and Standards

### Fair Trade Certification (2007)

**Fair Trade Premium**:
- $0.20 per pound above market price
- Additional $0.30 per pound for organic
- Annual premium total: $160,000-220,000 (varies with sales volume)
- Democratic allocation voted in General Assembly

**Premium Allocation** (recent years):
- Education: 40% (school construction, fees, ethnic minority education support)
  - 8 primary schools renovated/built
  - 280+ children receiving school fee support
  - University scholarship program (18 students, priority for ethnic minorities)
- Healthcare: 30% (health clinic construction, equipment, maternal health)
  - 2 community health clinics built and staffed
  - Mobile health clinics for remote villages
  - Maternal and child health programs
- Infrastructure: 20% (roads, washing stations, clean water)
  - 15 boreholes for clean water
  - Road improvements to remote villages
- Emergency fund: 10% (crop failure, family crisis)

**Fair Trade Impact**:
- Price floor protection during coffee market crashes
- Community infrastructure transformative for ethnic minority villages
- Empowered democratic governance
- International market access

### Organic Certification (2009)

**Certifying Body**: Control Union (international certifier)

**Organic Practices**:
- 3-year transition (2006-2009), Fair Trade premium supported transition
- No synthetic inputs (major cultural shift from chemical-dependent model)
- Compost and organic fertilizers only
- Biological pest management
- Internal control system (ICS) manages 3,200 members' compliance (challenging)
- Annual inspections by Control Union certifiers

**Organic Premium**: $0.40-0.60 per pound above conventional

**Organic Challenges**:
- Transitioning from chemical-dependent model (requires education, behavior change)
- Organic fertilizer production insufficient (compost, manure limited)
- Yields lower than conventional (improving as soil health builds)
- ICS system complex for large membership (requires resources and training)
- Pest and disease pressure without chemicals (learning organic management)

### UTZ Certification (2015)

**Sustainability Standards**:
- Good agricultural practices (GAP) training
- Traceability and record-keeping
- Environmental management (water, waste, biodiversity)
- Workers' rights and safe working conditions
- Continuous improvement protocols

## Producer Story and Cultural Context

### Historical Context

**French Colonial Coffee Introduction**:
- Coffee introduced to Vietnam by French colonists (1850s)
- Arabica initially, later Robusta (better suited to lowlands)
- Central Highlands developed for coffee 1920s-1940s
- Da Lat established as French resort town and agricultural center

**War and Reunification**:
- Vietnam War (1955-1975) disrupted agriculture
- Reunification under socialist government (1975)
- Collectivization period (1975-1986, limited coffee development)
- Doi Moi reforms (1986): Economic liberalization, private land use rights

**Coffee Boom**:
- 1990s-2000s: Explosive coffee expansion (primarily Robusta)
- Vietnam became world's #2 coffee producer (after Brazil)
- Arabica remained minor (5-7% of national production)
- Chemical-intensive agriculture typical
- Little focus on quality (commodity mindset)

**Specialty Emergence**:
- 2000s: Da Lat region began focusing on specialty Arabica
- Cooperatives formed to improve quality and market access
- Thanh Ha Cooperative founded 2005 during this wave
- Fair Trade and organic certification enabled premium market access
- Present: Vietnam's specialty Arabica sector still small but growing

### Ethnic Minority Context

**K'Ho, Ma, Cham Peoples**:
- Indigenous Central Highlands minorities
- Distinct languages, cultures, traditions
- Historically practiced swidden agriculture, hunting, gathering
- Marginalized under successive Vietnamese governments
- Land rights, education access, economic opportunity limited

**Cooperative as Empowerment**:
- Provides market access and fair prices (reduces exploitation)
- Democratic governance gives voice (representation in leadership)
- Education and healthcare from Fair Trade premium (improves welfare)
- Cultural respect and preservation (multi-language, traditions honored)
- Economic development without forced cultural assimilation

**Coffee as Livelihood**:
- Coffee provides cash income for minority farmers
- Integrated with traditional food cultivation
- Cooperative model respects communal values
- Challenges: Balancing cash crop with food security, avoiding chemical dependency

### Vision and Philosophy

**Cooperative Mission**:
"To improve the livelihoods of smallholder coffee farmers, particularly ethnic minorities, through sustainable specialty coffee production, fair prices, democratic governance, and community development, while preserving our cultural heritage and protecting our environment."

**Core Values**:
1. **Inclusion**: Ethnic minorities and women empowered and represented
2. **Quality**: Continuous improvement toward specialty excellence
3. **Sustainability**: Organic agriculture protects environment and health
4. **Equity**: Fair prices, democratic voice, shared benefits
5. **Community**: Education, healthcare, infrastructure for all
6. **Culture**: Respect and preserve ethnic minority languages and traditions

**10-Year Vision** (2025-2035):

- Climate-resilient agroforestry systems across all farms
- Women's leadership parity (50% board representation)

- Domestic market expansion (value-added products)
- Model cooperative for ethnic minority empowerment in Vietnam

## Sustainability and Environmental Stewardship

### Environmental Context

**Central Highlands Ecology**:
- Biodiversity hotspot (endemic species)
- Forest coverage declined significantly (coffee expansion, timber)
- Watershed importance (Dong Nai River system, water for millions)
- Chemical agriculture legacy (pollution concerns)

**Thanh Ha Conservation Approach**:
- Organic agriculture prevents agrochemical pollution
- Agroforestry preserves/restores forest structure (40% shade)
- Watershed protection (headwaters of rivers)
- Biodiversity enhancement through shade and organic practices

### Biodiversity Protection

**Species Diversity**:
- 4,800 hectares shade-grown coffee provides habitat
- Birds: 80+ species documented in member farms
- Mammals: Primates, civets, wild boar in forest-adjacent farms
- Insects: Diverse pollinators and beneficial insects (organic system supports)

**Forest Connectivity**:
- Shade trees connect farm patches to forest reserves
- Wildlife corridors maintained
- Native tree species reintroduction program (cooperative initiative, 10,000 trees planted since 2015)

### Water and Watershed

**Watershed Protection**:
- Organic practices prevent agrochemical runoff
- Terracing and permanent vegetation prevent erosion and siltation
- Shade trees protect water quality (root systems, soil structure)
- Springs and streams protected

**Processing Water Management**:
- Water recycling at central facility (50% reuse, target 70%)
- Wastewater settling ponds (reduce contamination)
- Constructed wetlands planned (biological treatment)
- Dry season water conservation critical (limited supply)

### Carbon and Climate

**Carbon Sequestration**:
- Agroforestry system: 2-3 tons CO2/hectare/year
- Total: 9,600-14,400 tons CO2 sequestered annually (4,800 hectares)
- Permanent tree coverage provides ongoing sequestration

**Emissions Reduction**:
- Organic practices eliminate synthetic fertilizer emissions
- Minimal mechanization (most work manual)
- Local processing reduces transport emissions

**Climate Resilience**:
- Agroforestry provides temperature moderation
- Diversified agriculture reduces economic vulnerability
- Organic soil management improves drought resilience
