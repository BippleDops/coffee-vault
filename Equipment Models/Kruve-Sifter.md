### Technique Resources

**How to Use Kruve Sifter**:

**Basic Sifting Process**:
1. **Grind coffee**: Use your grinder at chosen setting (20-40g sample)
2. **Weigh initial dose**: Record total weight (baseline)
3. **Select sieves**: Choose relevant sizes for your brewing method
4. **Stack sieves**: Largest on bottom, smallest on top, catch cup above
5. **Pour grounds**: Add coffee to top of sieve stack
6. **Shake/tap**: Agitate to separate particles (2-5 minutes, thorough)
7. **Disassemble stack**: Carefully separate sieves
8. **Weigh fractions**: Weigh coffee retained in each sieve
9. **Calculate percentages**: Determine particle distribution
10. **Record data**: Note grinder, setting, results

**Espresso Optimization Method**:
1. Grind espresso dose (18-20g)
2. Use 400¬µm sieve (separate fines from bulk)
3. Weigh fines (particles <400¬µm): Record percentage
4. Discard fines or brew separately (reduce bitterness)
5. Use remaining coffee for espresso (cleaner extraction)
6. Adjust grind setting based on fines percentage
7. Goal: Minimize fines while maintaining extraction

**Grinder Comparison Protocol**:
1. Select standard coffee and dose (e.g., 30g medium roast)
2. Grind on equivalent settings across grinders
3. Use same sieve stack (e.g., 200, 400, 600, 800, 1000¬µm)
4. Sift each grinder's output
5. Weigh each fraction
6. Calculate particle distribution percentages
7. Compare distributions (see grinder differences objectively)
8. Identify which grinder produces desired distribution

**Grind Distribution Analysis**:
- **Ideal espresso**: Narrow distribution, minimal fines, few boulders
- **Good grinder**: 60-70% in target range (e.g., 300-600¬µm for espresso)
- **Mediocre grinder**: Wide distribution, 20%+ fines, 10%+ boulders
- **Optimization**: Adjust grind setting to shift distribution into desired range

### Technical Analysis

**How Sifting Works**:
1. Coffee grounds poured into sieve stack
2. Agitation causes particles to fall through holes
3. Small particles pass through, large particles retained
4. Multiple sieves create size fractions
5. Weighing fractions reveals particle distribution

**Why Particle Distribution Matters**:

**Espresso (Most Critical)**:
- Fines (<300¬µm): Over-extract, cause bitterness, create channeling
- Target range (300-600¬µm): Optimal extraction
- Boulders (>600¬µm): Under-extract, sourness, wasted potential
- Ideal: Narrow distribution, minimal fines/boulders

**Pour-Over**:
- Fines: Slow flow, can cause over-extraction
- Target: Medium grind (600-900¬µm)
- Boulders: Fast flow, under-extraction
- Less critical than espresso (immersion more forgiving)

**French Press**:
- Fines: Pass through mesh, create sediment and muddiness
- Target: Coarse (900-1400¬µm)
- Distribution: Wider acceptable range

**What Kruve Reveals About Grinders**:
- **Particle distribution shape**: Narrow (good) vs. wide (poor)
- **Fines production**: Low (good) vs. high (poor)
- **Boulders**: Minimal (good) vs. significant (poor)
- **Consistency**: Tight distribution (good) vs. scattered (poor)
- **Grinder quality**: Expensive grinders generally produce narrower distributions
- **Burr geometry impact**: Conical vs. flat burrs have different distributions
- **Alignment issues**: Misaligned grinders produce more fines

**Sifting to Improve Coffee**:
- Remove fines: Reduces bitterness in espresso
- Remove boulders: Can re-grind for better extraction
- Narrow distribution: Use only target particle range
- Result: Cleaner, more balanced extraction
- Trade-off: Waste some coffee (removed fines/boulders)

### Scientific Methodology

**Proper Kruve Testing Protocol**:

**Variables to Control**:
- Same coffee (batch, roast date, density)
- Same dose (e.g., 30g every time)
- Same grind setting (or equivalent across grinders)
- Same sifting duration (e.g., 3 minutes)
- Same agitation method (consistent shaking)

**Data to Record**:
- Grinder model and settings
- Coffee details (origin, roast, date)
- Total dose weight
- Weight retained in each sieve
- Calculated percentages
- Date and conditions

**Analysis Metrics**:
- **Fines percentage**: <400¬µm for espresso
- **Target range percentage**: 400-600¬µm for espresso
- **Boulders percentage**: >600¬µm for espresso
- **Distribution width**: Spread of particle sizes
- **Peak particle size**: Where most coffee concentrated

**Interpreting Results**:
- **Good grinder**: 70%+ in target range, <15% fines, <10% boulders
- **Mediocre grinder**: 50-60% in range, 20-25% fines, 15-20% boulders
- **Poor grinder**: <50% in range, >25% fines, >20% boulders
- **Blade grinder**: Extremely wide distribution, high fines and boulders

### Practical Applications

**Espresso Optimization Workflow**:
1. Test current grinder with Kruve (establish baseline)

4. Find optimal setting (minimize fines while maintaining extraction)
5. Consider grinder upgrade if distribution too wide

### Limitations & Criticisms

**Kruve Doesn't Fix Bad Grinders**:
- Only reveals problems (doesn't solve them)
- Sifting is band-aid (better grinder is cure)
- Time-consuming for daily use (not practical long-term)

**Diminishing Returns**:
- Most improvement from good grinder, not sifting
- Optimization has limits (can't make bad grinder great)
- Effort vs. benefit calculation varies

**Practical Challenges**:
- Time-consuming: 5-10 minutes per analysis
- Tedious: Shaking, disassembling, weighing, calculating
- Coffee waste: Removed fines can't be used optimally
- Cleanliness: Particles everywhere during sifting
- Repetition: Needs multiple tests for statistical validity

**Not for Everyone**:
- Niche tool (specific use cases)
- Expensive for occasional use
- Requires interest in data and optimization
- May enable obsessive tweaking (endless optimization)

**Alternative Approaches**:
- Invest in better grinder (more impactful)
- Dial in by taste (simpler, equally effective for some)
- Trust good grinder (expensive grinders often don't need sifting)

### Best Practices Summary

**For Meaningful Results**:
1. Control variables (same coffee, dose, conditions)

**For Espresso Optimization**:
1. Use 400¬µm sieve (separate fines)

3. Remove fines for cleaner espresso

**For Grinder Testing**:
