## üìã Overview

Rwanda Nyamasheke Red Bourbon Washed represents one of modern specialty coffee's most remarkable success stories‚Äîa nation that rebuilt itself after genocide through a deliberate, government-supported strategy to produce world-class specialty coffee. This coffee embodies Rwanda's transformation from tragedy to triumph, demonstrating how quality-focused agricultural development can lift entire communities while producing extraordinary coffee.

**Key Characteristics**:
- Post-genocide recovery coffee (Rwanda Cup of Excellence program launched 2008)
- Nyamasheke District terroir (western Rwanda, Lake Kivu region, volcanic soils)
- Red Bourbon variety (traditional African Bourbon, exceptional cup quality)
- Fully washed processing (clean, bright, complex African profile)
- Cherry and raspberry notes (signature Rwandan fruit-forward character)
- High altitude (1,700-2,100 MASL creates exceptional density and complexity)
- Smallholder production (organized through washing stations/cooperatives)
- Premium specialty pricing ($5-12/lb green, $16-25/12oz roasted)
- Bright acidity, medium body, complex fruit and floral notes
- Exceptional value (African quality at accessible pricing)

**Historical & Cultural Context**:

**Rwanda's Coffee Transformation** (1994-Present):
- **1994**: Rwandan genocide (800,000-1,000,000 killed, infrastructure devastated)
- **Late 1990s**: Reconstruction begins, coffee identified as economic development opportunity
- **2001**: Government partnership with PEARL/SPREAD (USAID-funded NGO) begins
- **2002-2004**: Investment in washing stations (wet mills), farmer training, quality focus
- **2008**: First Rwanda Cup of Excellence (international quality recognition)
- **2010s**: Rwandan specialty coffee gains global reputation
- **Present**: Rwanda known for exceptional fruit-forward, complex coffees

Rwanda's deliberate strategy: Replace low-quality, fully-washed commodity coffee (grown since Belgian colonial era, 1920s-1962) with high-quality specialty coffee. The government invested in washing station infrastructure, trained farmers in quality cultivation, connected producers with international buyers, and promoted Cup of Excellence to showcase quality. The result: Rwanda went from anonymous commodity producer to specialty darling in 15 years.

**Nyamasheke District** (Western Rwanda):
- **Location**: Western Rwanda, on eastern shore of Lake Kivu (border with DRC)
- **Topography**: Mountainous, volcanic soils, Lake Kivu proximity moderates climate
- **Altitude**: 1,600-2,200 MASL (among Rwanda's highest coffee-growing areas)
- **Terroir**: Volcanic soils from Virunga volcanic chain, cool temperatures, defined seasons
- **Reputation**: Nyamasheke and neighboring Karongi districts produce Rwanda's most sought-after coffees

**Red Bourbon in Rwanda**:
- **Introduction**: Belgian colonizers introduced Bourbon variety (1930s-1940s)
- **Genetics**: Rwandan Bourbon descended from Bourbon Island (R√©union), via East Africa
- **Dominance**: 95%+ of Rwandan coffee is Red Bourbon (monoculture, quality consistency)
- **Challenge**: Susceptible to coffee leaf rust and coffee berry disease (requires management)
- **Benefit**: Exceptional cup quality when well-cultivated and processed

**Washed Processing Tradition**:
- Rwanda historically produced washed coffee (colonial era legacy)
- Post-genocide, strategy focused on perfecting washed processing (quality over novelty)
- Investment in modern washing stations (centralized wet mills, quality control)
- Result: Clean, bright, complex cups showcasing terroir and variety

**Market Position**:
Rwanda offers exceptional African coffee at more accessible pricing than Kenyan or Ethiopian Cup of Excellence lots. At $16-22/12oz roasted, Rwandan coffee delivers bright, complex, fruit-forward profiles that demonstrate why African coffee is celebrated, without the premium prices of more famous origins.

**Misconception Alert**: "Rwandan coffee is sad/genocide coffee." This narrative is both reductive and inaccurate. Modern Rwandan coffee is a story of resilience, intentional quality focus, entrepreneurship, and global market integration. Rwandan producers are proud of their coffee and their achievement‚Äîreducing them to tragedy dishonors their work.

## üåç Origin & Geography: Nyamasheke District & Lake Kivu

### Location & Topography

**Geographic Position**:
- **Country**: Rwanda (East-Central Africa)
- **Province**: Western Province
- **District**: Nyamasheke District
- **Region**: Lake Kivu eastern shore (western Rwanda, DRC border)
- **Altitude**: District ranges 1,200-2,200 MASL; coffee typically 1,700-2,100 MASL
- **Coordinates**: Approximately 2.3¬∞S, 29¬∞E
- **Distance from Kigali** (capital): ~200 km west (5-6 hour drive on mountain roads)
- **Lake Kivu**: One of African Great Lakes, borders Nyamasheke to west

**Topography**:
- **Terrain**: Steep mountainsides, deep valleys, volcanic hills
- **Slopes**: 25-50¬∞ inclines common (hand-picking required, terracing essential)
- **Elevation range**: 1,000-meter vertical range (1,200-2,200 MASL within district)
- **Aspect**: Varied orientations (east, south, west-facing slopes create diverse microclimates)
- **Drainage**: Excellent (steep slopes, volcanic soils prevent waterlogging)
- **Water sources**: Mountain streams, rivers, Lake Kivu (processing water)

**Why This Geography Is Ideal for Coffee**:
1. **Extreme altitude**: 1,700-2,100 MASL creates slow cherry maturation (flavor development)
2. **Lake Kivu proximity**: Large water body moderates temperature extremes (microclimate stability)
3. **Volcanic soils**: Ancient volcanic activity created mineral-rich growing medium
4. **Steep slopes**: Natural drainage, hand-picking quality control
5. **Diverse microclimates**: Elevation and aspect variation allow site selection optimization
6. **Cool temperatures**: Altitude + lake effect = ideal temperature range for quality coffee

**Lake Kivu Influence**:
- **Size**: 2,700 km¬≤ (1,040 sq mi), one of African Great Lakes
- **Depth**: 480m maximum depth (deep lake = temperature stability)
- **Climate moderation**: Large water mass stabilizes temperature (day/night, seasonal variation reduced)
- **Humidity**: Lake increases ambient humidity (plant water stress reduction)
- **Breezes**: Lake-generated breezes moderate temperature, reduce disease pressure
- **Comparison**: Similar to Lake Atitl√°n (Guatemala) or Lake Toba (Sumatra) microclimate effects

### Climate & Weather Patterns

**Climate Classification**: Tropical highland climate (modified by altitude and lake)

**Temperature**:
- **Average annual**: 15-19¬∞C (59-66¬∞F) at coffee altitudes
- **Daytime highs**: 20-24¬∞C (68-75¬∞F)
- **Nighttime lows**: 10-14¬∞C (50-57¬∞F) at higher altitudes
- **Diurnal variation**: 8-12¬∞C (critical for quality)
- **Frost**: Never (equatorial latitude 2¬∞S, too low for frost even at altitude)
- **Lake Kivu effect**: Moderates extremes (cooler days in dry season, warmer nights year-round)

**Rainfall & Seasons**:
- **Annual precipitation**: 1,200-1,800mm
- **Bimodal pattern**: Two wet seasons, two dry seasons (equatorial Africa pattern)
  - **Long wet season**: March-May (peak flowering)
  - **Short dry season**: June-August (minor harvest)
  - **Short wet season**: September-November (secondary flowering)
  - **Long dry season**: December-February (main harvest, peak January)
- **Distribution**: Afternoon thunderstorms common during wet seasons
- **Variability**: Climate change increasing unpredictability (challenge for farmers)

**Seasonal Coffee Calendar**:
- **Main flowering**: March-April (long wet season rains trigger)
- **Secondary flowering**: September-October (short wet season)
- **Cherry development**: April-December (wet season growth)
- **Main harvest**: December-February (long dry season, 70-80% of production)
- **Secondary harvest** (fly crop): June-July (short dry season, 20-30% of production)
- **Processing**: January-February (main crop), June-July (fly crop)

**Dry Season Advantage**:
- Rwanda's long dry season (December-February) coincides with main harvest
- Dry weather enables washed processing (water availability without rain interference)
- Reduced disease pressure during harvest (lower humidity)

**Climate Challenges**:
- **Heavy rains during wet season**: Erosion risk on steep slopes (terracing essential)
- **Coffee leaf rust**: High humidity during wet season = high disease pressure
- **Climate change**: Increasing rainfall variability, temperature shifts (adaptation needed)

**Sunlight & Cloud Cover**:
- **Wet season**: Frequent cloud cover, filtered sunlight
- **Dry season**: More direct sun, clear skies
- **Shade trees**: Traditional practice (reduce sun stress, erosion control, biodiversity)

### Soil Composition: Volcanic Legacy

**Soil Type**: Volcanic ash-derived soils from ancient Virunga volcanic activity

**Volcanic Context**:
- **Virunga Mountains**: Volcanic chain northeast of Lake Kivu (includes Rwandan volcanoes)
- **Activity**: Ancient eruptions (millions of years), deposits spread across region
- **Current**: Some Virunga volcanoes still active (Nyiragongo in DRC), but coffee areas safe
- **Legacy**: Deep volcanic ash and weathered volcanic soils across western Rwanda

**Soil Characteristics**:
- **Color**: Deep red to reddish-brown (iron oxide content, volcanic minerals)
- **Texture**: Clay-loam to clay (high clay content from weathered volcanic material)
- **Structure**: Well-aggregated despite clay (volcanic ash creates good structure)
- **Drainage**: Good (volcanic porosity, steep slopes enhance drainage)
- **Depth**: Variable (1-3+ meters volcanic soils on slopes, deeper in valleys)
- **pH**: Moderately acidic (5.0-5.8), ideal for coffee
- **Organic matter**: Moderate (3-6%), tropical climate = fast decomposition

**Mineral Content**:
- **Phosphorus**: Moderate (volcanic minerals, but some fixation to iron/aluminum)
- **Potassium**: Good levels (volcanic source, essential for cherry development)
- **Calcium & Magnesium**: Present (volcanic minerals, pH buffering)
- **Iron & Aluminum**: High (gives red color, can cause nutrient availability issues)
- **Micronutrients**: Complete spectrum (zinc, boron, manganese, copper from volcanic source)
- **Nitrogen**: Low to moderate (leaching from rain, requires fertilization/organic matter)

**Terroir Expression**:
- Volcanic soils contribute to Rwandan coffee's bright acidity and fruit complexity
- Mineral complexity translates to cup complexity (layered flavors)
- Excellent drainage prevents off-flavors from waterlogged roots
- Red soils provide visual terroir marker (distinctive Rwandan landscape)

**Soil Management**:
- **Erosion control**: Critical on steep slopes (terracing, cover crops, contour planting)
- **Organic matter**: Shade trees, mulching, composting (maintain soil health)
- **Fertilization**: NPK applications (nitrogen especially, potassium for fruit quality)
- **Liming**: Occasional (maintain pH 5.5-6.0 range for optimal nutrient availability)

## üå± Variety Information: Red Bourbon in Rwanda

### Red Bourbon Origins & Introduction to Rwanda

**Bourbon Variety Background**:
- **Origin**: Bourbon Island (now R√©union), Indian Ocean, 1700s
- **Parentage**: Natural mutation of Typica (or separate introduction from Yemen)
- **Spread**: Bourbon spread to East Africa (Tanzania, Kenya, Rwanda, Burundi) in early 1900s

**Rwanda Introduction**:
- **Timing**: 1930s-1940s (Belgian colonial period)
- **Source**: Likely via Tanzania (German East Africa, then Belgian mandate)
- **Purpose**: Commercial coffee production (colonial crop)
- **Initial quality**: Commodity production (volume over quality during colonial era)
- **Post-1994**: Quality became focus (Bourbon's cup quality recognized and leveraged)

**Rwandan Bourbon Genetics**:
- **Lineage**: East African Bourbon ecotypes (adapted to local conditions over decades)
- **Purity**: Relatively pure Bourbon (limited cross-breeding, monoculture practice)
- **Selection**: Natural selection over 70+ years created locally-adapted populations
- **Characteristics**: Bourbon characteristics with Rwandan terroir adaptation

**Why Bourbon Dominated**:
- **Colonial choice**: Belgians chose Bourbon (quality variety for export)
- **Farmer familiarity**: Generations of cultivation knowledge
- **Cup quality**: Recognized as quality variety (post-genocide strategy leveraged this)
- **Genetic stability**: Bourbon breeds true (seed propagation consistent)
- **Tradition**: Cultural attachment to familiar variety

**Monoculture Concern**:
- **95%+ Bourbon**: Rwanda is essentially Bourbon monoculture
- **Risk**: Disease outbreak could devastate entire sector
- **Benefit**: Consistency (buyers know what to expect from Rwandan coffee)
- **Future**: Discussions of diversification (introduce disease-resistant varieties while maintaining Bourbon quality focus)

### Red Bourbon Characteristics

**Tree Morphology**:
- **Height**: 2.5-3.5 meters (typically managed to 2-2.5m through pruning)
- **Shape**: Conical, relatively compact
- **Branch angle**: 45-50¬∞ from trunk
- **Internodes**: Short to medium (compact growth)
- **Leaves**: Broad, wavy edges, bronze young tips (varietal marker)
- **Root system**: Deep taproot (good for volcanic soils, drought tolerance)

**Cherry & Bean Characteristics**:
- **Cherry color**: Deep red when ripe (hence "Red Bourbon")
- **Ripeness indicator**: Color change green ‚Üí yellow ‚Üí orange ‚Üí red (clear visual cue)
- **Cherry size**: Medium to medium-large
- **Beans per cherry**: Typically 2 (normal), occasional peaberries
- **Bean shape**: Oval, rounded (not elongated like Geisha)
- **Bean size**: Medium (screen 15-17 typical for Rwandan Bourbon)
- **Density**: Very high at Rwandan altitudes (1,700-2,100 MASL = extremely dense)

**Yield Characteristics**:
- **Productivity**: Moderate to low (lower than modern high-yield varieties)
- **Rwandan context**: 1-3 kg cherries/tree/year (altitude and small-holder intensive management)
- **Biennial bearing**: Moderate tendency (alternate heavy/light years)
- **Economic viability**: Only viable with specialty pricing (low yield offset by high quality premiums)

**Disease Resistance**:
- **Coffee Leaf Rust (CLR)**: Susceptible (major challenge in Rwanda)
- **Coffee Berry Disease (CBD)**: Moderately susceptible
- **Management**: Requires fungicide applications, shade management, proper nutrition
- **Rwanda context**: High humidity during wet season = high disease pressure
- **Control**: Government and cooperative-supported spray programs

**Why Rwanda Continues Bourbon Despite Challenges**:
- **Cup quality**: Exceptional when well-grown (justifies management costs)
- **Brand identity**: "Rwandan Bourbon" is market differentiator
- **Familiarity**: Farmers know Bourbon cultivation intimately
- **Quality premiums**: Specialty market pays enough to offset lower yields and disease costs
- **Success model**: Bourbon worked for transformation (don't fix what's not broken)

### Bourbon Cup Quality in Rwandan Terroir

**Why Rwandan Bourbon Tastes Special**:

**Altitude Impact**:
- 1,700-2,100 MASL = slow maturation (7-9 months cherry development)
- Extended time allows complex sugar and acid development
- Dense beans concentrate flavor compounds
- Cool temperatures create vibrant acidity

**Volcanic Terroir**:
- Mineral-rich soils provide trace elements (flavor complexity)
- Drainage prevents off-flavors
- Volcanic terroir creates distinctive cup character (fruit-forward, bright)

**Bourbon Genetics + Rwandan Terroir = Signature Profile**:
- **Cherry notes**: Fresh cherry, black cherry, cherry blossom (Rwandan signature)
- **Berry complexity**: Raspberry, blackberry, blueberry (varies by lot)
- **Bright acidity**: Citric and malic acids (apple, lemon, orange)
- **Floral notes**: Jasmine, orange blossom (especially in top lots)
- **Sweetness**: Brown sugar, honey, caramel (Bourbon characteristic)
- **Clean cup**: Washed processing creates clarity (terroir and variety shine)

**Rwandan Bourbon vs. Other Origin Bourbon**:
- **vs. Guatemalan/Central American Bourbon**: Rwandan is brighter, more fruit-forward, less chocolate/nut
- **vs. Burundian Bourbon**: Very similar (neighbors, similar terroir), Burundi slightly brighter
- **vs. Kenyan AA** (not Bourbon, but comparison): Rwandan Bourbon similar brightness, different fruit character

## üîÑ Processing: Fully Washed Method - Rwandan Standard

### Washing Station (Wet Mill) Model

**Centralized Processing** (Key to Rwanda Success):
- **System**: Smallholder farmers deliver cherries to centralized washing stations
- **Washing stations**: Privately owned or cooperative-owned wet mills
- **Geographic coverage**: Nyamasheke has 15-25 washing stations (varies)
- **Catchment area**: Each station serves 500-2,000 farmers (radius 5-10 km typical)
- **Quality control**: Centralized processing ensures consistency (farmers can't process individually)

**Benefits of Washing Station Model**:
- **Quality consistency**: Professional staff, standardized protocols
- **Investment**: Washing stations make infrastructure investment (farmers can't afford individually)
- **Training**: Washing station staff trained in quality processing
- **Market access**: Washing stations export directly or via exporters (connect farmers to market)
- **Traceability**: Lot tracking enables premiums for quality

**Farmer Responsibilities**:
- **Cultivation**: Plant, maintain, harvest coffee on their land (typically 0.25-2 hectares)
- **Selective picking**: Only ripe cherries (washing stations enforce this through pricing)
- **Transport**: Deliver cherries to washing station same day or next morning
- **Payment**: Receive payment based on cherry weight and quality (ripeness, defects)

**Washing Station Responsibilities**:
- **Reception**: Accept cherries, quality check, weigh, record
- **Processing**: Wet processing from cherry to parchment
- **Drying**: Sun-drying to target moisture
- **Storage**: Hold parchment until dry milling
- **Quality control**: Sort and grade for export
- **Sales**: Sell to exporters or direct to roasters

### Rwandan Washed Processing Step-by-Step

**Stage 1: Harvest & Cherry Delivery** (December-February main season, June-July fly crop)

**Selective Harvesting**:
- **Method**: 100% hand-picking (Rwanda's terrain requires it)
- **Ripeness**: Only fully ripe red cherries (washing stations enforce through payment tiers)
- **Passes**: 3-5 selective passes through farm during harvest season
- **Daily picking**: Farmers pick daily or every 2-3 days (deliver fresh)
- **Quality incentive**: Washing stations pay premium for fully ripe, defect-free cherries

**Cherry Delivery**:
- **Timing**: Same day or next morning (prevent fermentation)
- **Transport**: Farmers carry cherries in baskets, bags, bicycles (1-10 km typical distance)
- **Volume**: Small-scale (20-100 kg per farmer per delivery typical)

**Stage 2: Reception & Sorting** (Day 1, Within 24 Hours of Picking)

**Washing Station Reception**:
- **Weigh-in**: Cherries weighed, farmer recorded (payment tracking)
- **Visual inspection**: Quality check (ripeness, defects)
- **Sorting**: Farmers may be asked to remove underripe/overripe cherries (re-sort)

**Flotation Sorting**:
- **Method**: Cherries poured into water-filled tanks
- **Separation**:
  - **Floaters**: Unripe, dried, insect-damaged, low-density (rejected or lower price)
  - **Sinkers**: Ripe, dense, high-quality (premium lot)
- **Volume removed**: 5-15% typical (depends on farmer picking quality)
- **Water source**: River, stream, or borehole (clean water essential)

**Quality Grading**:
- **A1 cherries**: Fully ripe sinkers, no defects (premium price)
- **A2/A3**: Slightly underripe or mixed lots (standard price)
- **Rejected**: Floaters, defective cherries (no payment or minimal)

**Stage 3: Depulping** (Day 1, Same Day as Reception)

**Mechanical Depulping**:
- **Equipment**: Disc depulpers (most common in Rwanda)
- **Capacity**: 1,500-5,000 kg cherries/hour (varies by washing station size)
- **Process**:
  - Cherries fed into hopper
  - Discs remove skin and most pulp
  - Mucilage layer remains on parchment
  - Beans separated from pulp waste
- **Adjustment**: Gap settings control pulp removal (too tight = damaged beans, too wide = residual pulp)

**Quality Control**:
- **Visual check**: Inspect depulped beans (should be clean parchment with mucilage, no skin residue)
- **Damaged beans**: Minimal acceptable (proper settings reduce damage to <1%)

**Byproduct Management**:
- **Pulp**: Composted or disposed (environmental concern, some stations compost for fertilizer)
- **Water**: Used water treated before river disposal (environmental regulations)

**Stage 4: Fermentation** (12-24 hours typically, Day 1-2)

**Dry Fermentation** (Most Common in Rwanda):
- **Method**: Depulped beans in fermentation tanks without added water
- **Purpose**: Natural enzymes (pectinase) break down mucilage
- **Duration factors**:
  - **Temperature**: 18-22¬∞C at Rwandan altitude (cooler = slower fermentation)
  - **Altitude**: Higher altitude washing stations = cooler temps = 18-24 hours
  - **Lower altitude**: Warmer = 12-18 hours
  - **Mucilage thickness**: Riper cherries = thicker mucilage = longer fermentation
- **Monitoring**:
  - **Touch test**: Mucilage changes from slippery to rough
  - **Visual**: Color and texture changes
  - **Water test**: Sample beans washed, check if mucilage releases cleanly
  - **Experience**: Staff know timing for their specific washing station altitude/climate

**Fermentation Endpoint**:
- **Target**: Complete mucilage breakdown, clean parchment
- **Under-fermented**: Residual mucilage (difficult drying, potential defects)
- **Over-fermented**: Vinegar/fermented flavors (stinker beans, ruined lot)
- **Optimal**: Clean parchment, no slipperiness, ready for washing

**Wet Fermentation** (Some Washing Stations):
- **Method**: Beans submerged in water during fermentation
- **Duration**: Typically 12-36 hours (longer than dry)
- **Benefits**: More controlled temperature, potentially cleaner cup
- **Drawbacks**: More water use, longer time

**Impact on Cup**:
- **Proper fermentation**: Bright acidity, clean cup, complex fruit flavors
- **Optimal fermentation**: Enhances Rwandan cherry/berry character
- **Critical control**: Fermentation timing creates or destroys quality (hours matter)

**Stage 5: Washing** (Day 2, Post-Fermentation)

**Purpose**: Remove all fermented mucilage, leaving perfectly clean parchment

**Washing Process**:
- **Method**: Washing channels (long concrete channels with flowing water)
- **Water flow**: Fresh water continuously flows through channels
- **Agitation**: Beans stirred and moved through channels (mechanical or manual)
- **Duration**: 10-30 minutes washing, multiple rinse cycles
- **Volume**: Large quantities of water (10-20 liters per kg cherry, environmental concern)

**Washing Channel Grading**:
- **Gradient channels**: Water flow separates beans by density
- **Premium (A1)**: Heaviest beans sink immediately (highest quality)
- **Standard (A2)**: Medium density, slower sinking
- **Lights (A3)**: Lighter beans (lower quality, may be rejected)
- **Floaters**: Removed entirely (defects)

**Final Quality Check**:
- **Visual inspection**: Parchment should be clean, light yellow-green color
- **Touch test**: No residual slipperiness
- **Smell**: Clean, no off-odors (fermented smell indicates problem)

**Environmental Challenge**:
- **Water use**: Washed processing uses substantial water (sustainability concern)
- **Wastewater**: Nutrient-rich water can pollute rivers (environmental regulation)
- **Solutions**: Water recycling, constructed wetlands, settling ponds (modern washing stations)

**Stage 6: Drying** (10-21 days, Day 2-23)

**Sun Drying on Raised Beds** (Rwandan Standard):
- **Infrastructure**: Raised beds (African-style drying beds, 1-1.5m high)
- **Surface**: Mesh screens (nylon or polypropylene, allows airflow)
- **Layer thickness**: 2-4 cm (single to double layer, thinner = faster drying)
- **Coverage**: Shade cloth midday (prevent scorching), tarps at night (dew) and if rain threatens

**Drying Process**:

**Week 1 (Days 1-7)**:
- **Moisture**: 55-60% ‚Üí 35-40%
- **Turning frequency**: Every 30-60 minutes during sun (labor intensive)
- **Color**: Parchment light yellow-green ‚Üí golden yellow
- **Weather**: Rwanda's dry season is reliable (December-February main crop drying ideal)

**Week 2 (Days 8-14)**:
- **Moisture**: 35-40% ‚Üí 18-22%
- **Turning**: Every 2-3 hours (less frequent)
- **Monitoring**: Check for uneven drying (redistribute coffee)

**Week 3 (Days 15-21)** - Final Drying:
- **Moisture**: 18-22% ‚Üí 10.5-11.5% (target)
- **Turning**: Once daily
- **Testing**: Daily moisture meter readings
- **Endpoint**: Parchment brittle, breaks cleanly

**Weather Challenges**:
- **Rain during dry season**: Occasional (covered beds essential)
- **Dew**: Nighttime dew common (tarps protect)
- **Humidity**: Morning humidity (delay sun exposure until humidity drops)

**Drying Labor**:
- **Intensive**: Constant raking, sorting, covering/uncovering
- **Employment**: Drying provides jobs (15-30 staff per washing station during harvest)

**Stage 7: Resting (Descanso)** (30-60 days)

**Post-Drying Storage**:
- **Form**: Parchment coffee (protective layer maintained)
- **Duration**: 1-2 months minimum before dry milling
- **Conditions**: Cool, dry storage (moisture-controlled if possible)
- **Purpose**: Moisture equalization, flavor stabilization

**Stage 8: Dry Milling & Grading** (Pre-Export)

**Hulling**:
- **Location**: Centralized dry mills (not at washing stations)
- **Process**: Mechanical removal of parchment layer
- **Result**: Green coffee beans exposed

**Grading** (Rwandan System):

**Screen Sorting**: Size separation
- Screens 15-19 typical for Rwandan Bourbon
- Size consistency for even roasting

**Density Sorting**: Gravity tables
- **A1**: Highest density (best quality)
- **A2**: Standard density
- **A3**: Lower density
- Remove low-density defects

**Electronic Color Sorting**:
- Machines remove discolored beans (defects)
- Multiple passes for premium lots

**Hand Sorting**: Final quality control
- 3-5 hand-sorting passes typical for specialty lots
- Women sort beans on tables (skilled sorters identify visual defects)
- Remove: Quakers, broken beans, insect damage, discoloration

**Rwandan Grading System**:
- **A1**: Top quality (screen 15+, zero to minimal defects, highest density)
- **A2**: Good quality (screen 14+, minor defects acceptable)
- **A3**: Standard commercial (screen 13+, higher defect tolerance)
- **BH (Broken/Half beans)**: Separate grade (broken beans, lower value)

**Final Preparation**:
