## ðŸ“‹ Overview

Costa Rica TarrazÃº Geisha Red Honey represents the convergence of three exceptional elements in specialty coffee: the legendary Geisha variety known for its ethereal floral complexity, the innovative red honey processing method that enhances sweetness and body while maintaining clarity, and Costa Rica's renowned TarrazÃº region with its ideal high-altitude microclimate and rich volcanic soils.

**Key Characteristics**:
- Ultra-premium Geisha variety (most sought-after in specialty coffee)
- Red honey processing (60-80% mucilage retained during drying)
- TarrazÃº region prestige (Costa Rica's most famous coffee zone)
- SHB grade (high altitude, dense beans)
- Jasmine and bergamot floral notes (Geisha signature)
- Tea-like body with honey sweetness
- Exceptional complexity and clarity
- Competition-quality coffee (frequently scores 88-92+ points)

**Why This Coffee Is Special**:

The Geisha variety, originally from Ethiopia's Gesha village, found its greatest expression when planted in Central America's high-altitude regions. While Panama's Geisha gained fame first (2004 Best of Panama auction), Costa Rican Geisha has proven equally exceptional, offering similar floral complexity and delicate character at more accessible pricing.

Red honey processing adds a crucial dimension to Geisha. While washed Geisha showcases the variety's floral and tea-like character, honey processing introduces sweetness, body, and fruit complexity that makes the coffee more approachable while maintaining the variety's signature elegance. The "red honey" designation indicates 60-80% of the mucilage (fruit pectin layer) is left on the bean during drying, creating a sweet, complex cup with more body than washed but more clarity than natural processing.

TarrazÃº (Los Santos region) has been synonymous with Costa Rican coffee quality since the 1800s. The region's altitude (1,200-1,900 MASL), volcanic soils from the nearby Cordillera de Talamanca mountains, defined wet and dry seasons, and cool temperatures create ideal conditions for slow cherry maturation and complex flavor development. When Geisha is planted in this terroir and processed with precision honey methods, the result is extraordinary.

**Market Position**: This coffee commands premium pricing ($20-35/12oz roasted, $12-25/lb green) but represents accessible luxury compared to Panamanian Geisha ($30-100+/12oz). It's competition-quality coffee available to consumers willing to invest in an exceptional experience.

**Misconception Alert**: Geisha is not just "hype." The variety's unique genetic makeup produces genuinely different flavor compounds compared to other arabicas. Chemical analysis shows higher concentrations of linalool (floral aromatics) and distinct acid profiles that create the characteristic jasmine, bergamot, and tea-like notes. This is scientifically measurable difference, not marketing.

## ðŸ”„ Processing: Red Honey Method

### Understanding Honey Processing

**What Is Honey Processing?**
- **Definition**: Partial removal of cherry fruit, mucilage layer retained during drying
- **Name origin**: "Honey" refers to sticky mucilage (not actual honey added)
- **Spectrum**: White â†’ Yellow â†’ Red â†’ Black honey (increasing mucilage retention)
- **Costa Rican innovation**: Developed and perfected in Costa Rica (2000s)
- **Purpose**: Adds sweetness, body, fruit notes while maintaining more clarity than natural processing

**Honey Processing Categories**:

**White Honey** (10-25% mucilage):
- Minimal mucilage retained
- Closest to washed in clarity
- Subtle sweetness increase
- Faster drying (less fermentation risk)

**Yellow Honey** (25-50% mucilage):
- Moderate mucilage retention
- Balanced sweetness and clarity
- Honey and fruit notes emerge
- Moderate drying time

**Red Honey** (60-80% mucilage) â­ **This Coffee**:
- High mucilage retention
- Significant sweetness and body
- Fruit complexity develops
- Extended drying time (10-20 days)
- Red/brown bean color during drying (hence "red")

**Black Honey** (90-100% mucilage):
- Maximum mucilage retained (nearly natural processing)
- Highest sweetness and body
- Most fruit complexity
- Longest drying (20-30+ days)
- Highest fermentation risk

**Why Red Honey for Geisha**:
- Adds body to Geisha's naturally light body (more approachable)
- Introduces sweetness without overwhelming delicate florals
- Creates fruit complexity that complements Geisha's character
- More accessible pricing than black honey (less risky to produce)
- Balances Geisha's acidity with sweetness
- Market differentiation from Panamanian washed Geisha

### Red Honey Processing Step-by-Step

**Stage 1: Harvest** (December-March, Peak January-February)

**Selective Picking**:
- **Ripeness**: Only fully ripe cherry (deep red, 20-22Â° Brix)
- **Selection**: Geisha requires exceptional ripeness selection
- **Multiple passes**: 6-8 rounds through the trees
- **Timing**: Peak ripeness critical for honey processing
- **Quality control**: Underripe or overripe cherries significantly affect honey processing

**Labor Intensity**:
- **Skilled pickers**: Must identify optimal ripeness
- **Low yields**: Geisha's sparse production requires more picking time per kg
- **Careful handling**: Avoid cherry damage (affects drying)
- **Same-day processing**: Cherries must be processed within 12 hours

**Stage 2: Reception & Sorting** (Day 1)

**Initial Inspection**:
- **Visual check**: Color uniformity, no defects
- **Flotation tank**: Unripe and damaged cherries removed (float)
- **Brix testing**: Some producers measure sugar content (quality control)
- **Lot separation**: Geisha processed separately from other varieties

**Quality Control**:
- Only 90-95% of harvested cherries accepted for red honey
- Strict sorting ensures consistent mucilage quality
- Defective cherries affect fermentation predictability

**Stage 3: Depulping** (Day 1, Within Hours of Harvest)

**Mechanical Depulping**:
- **Equipment**: Disc depulper with adjustable gap
- **Settings**: Calibrated to remove skin and pulp only
- **Target**: 60-80% mucilage retention (red honey specification)
- **Precision**: Gap setting controls mucilage percentage
  - Wider gap = more mucilage retained
  - Narrower gap = less mucilage
- **Calibration**: Measured by visual inspection and experience

**Mucilage Layer**:
- **Composition**: Pectin, sugars, water (slippery, sticky)
- **Thickness**: Varies with ripeness (riper = thicker)
- **Color**: Clear to light yellow (fresh)
- **Importance**: Contains sugars that create honey flavor profile

**Quality Control**:
- Visual inspection of depulped beans
- Consistency check across batch
- Adjust depulper if mucilage retention inconsistent

**Stage 4: Drying - The Critical Phase** (Day 1-20)

This is where red honey processing creates its magic and faces its greatest challenges.

**Drying Method**: Raised beds or patios (sun drying essential for quality)

**Initial Phase** (Days 1-7):
- **Layer thickness**: Very thin (1-2 cm single layer)
- **Turning frequency**: Every 30-60 minutes during sun exposure
- **Coverage**: Shade cloth during hottest midday sun (prevent over-drying)
- **Nighttime**: Covered with tarps (dew prevention)
- **Humidity**: Beans are sticky, attract moisture
- **Color change**: Yellow â†’ orange â†’ red â†’ reddish-brown (hence "red honey")

**Why Constant Attention Needed**:
- High sugar content (mucilage) attracts flies, insects
- Uneven drying creates fermentation defects
- Beans stick together (must be separated by raking)
- Mold risk if drying too slow
- Over-fermentation risk from sugars
- Case-hardening risk if drying too fast (exterior dries, interior stays wet)

**Middle Phase** (Days 7-14):
- **Layer thickness**: Can increase slightly (beans less sticky)
- **Turning frequency**: Every 2-3 hours
- **Color**: Deep red-brown (characteristic red honey color)
- **Moisture loss**: Gradual, controlled (target 4-6% moisture loss per day)
- **Aroma**: Sweet, fruity, honey-like (fermentation creating flavors)

**Final Phase** (Days 14-20):
- **Moisture target**: Approaching 10-12%
- **Turning**: Once daily sufficient
- **Monitoring**: Moisture meter readings
- **Visual**: Parchment brittleness, color stabilization
- **Taste test**: Sample roasting to check development

**Drying Challenges**:
- **Weather dependency**: Rain during drying is disaster (covered beds or greenhouse essential)
- **Fermentation control**: Balancing desirable fermentation vs. defect development
- **Labor intensive**: Constant raking, turning, monitoring
- **Time**: 15-20 days typical (vs. 7-10 for washed)
- **Loss rate**: 5-10% loss to defects not uncommon

**Costa Rica Advantage for Honey Processing**:
- **Dry season**: December-April is reliably dry (harvest season alignment)
- **Micro-mill infrastructure**: Investment in covered drying areas, greenhouses
- **Expertise**: Developed and refined honey processing methods
- **Climate**: Moderate humidity during dry season (not too dry, not too wet)

**Stage 5: Resting & Moisture Equalization** (Days 20-30+)

**Post-Drying Rest**:
- **Storage**: Beans in parchment, stored in cool, dry area
- **Duration**: 2-4 weeks minimum
- **Purpose**: Moisture equalizes throughout bean
- **Benefit**: More stable coffee, better flavor development
- **Monitoring**: Periodic moisture checks

**Stage 6: Hulling & Preparation for Export**

**Hulling**:
- **Timing**: Shortly before roasting or export
- **Process**: Parchment layer removal (mechanical)
- **Result**: Green coffee exposed
- **Color**: Honey-processed beans darker than washed (yellow-green to light brown)

**Sorting**:
- **Screen sorting**: Size separation (Geisha typically screen 15-17)
- **Density sorting**: Gravity table removes low-density beans
- **Electronic color sorting**: Defect removal
- **Hand sorting**: Final quality control (multiple passes)
- **Target**: Zero defect tolerance for premium Geisha

**Grading**:
- **SHB designation**: Strictly Hard Bean (altitude-based)
- **EP (European Preparation)**: Minimal defect tolerance
- **Premium Geisha**: Often exceeds standard EP specifications

### Roasting Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge 1: Baking (Underdevelopment)**
- **Symptom**: Grassy, vegetal, peanutty, lacking sweetness
- **Cause**: Insufficient heat during drying/browning
- **Solution**: Higher charge temp, increase heat in browning phase

**Challenge 2: Destroying Aromatics**
- **Symptom**: Generic coffee flavor, floral notes absent
- **Cause**: Too-hot roast, excessive development
- **Solution**: Lower temperatures, shorter development time, gentler profile

**Challenge 3: Uneven Development**
- **Symptom**: Some beans darker than others (color variation)
- **Cause**: Uneven heat distribution, bean size variation
- **Solution**: Better airflow management, thorough destoning/sorting pre-roast

**Challenge 4: Sour/Underdeveloped Despite Light Roast**
- **Symptom**: Sour, astringent, unpleasant acidity
- **Cause**: Inadequate development (too short post-crack)
- **Solution**: Extend development 10-20 seconds, ensure proper Maillard phase

**Sample Roasting**:
- **Essential**: Always sample roast Geisha before production batches
- **Test roast**: 100-200g samples
- **Cupping**: 24 hours rest minimum, then cup
- **Adjustment**: Fine-tune profile based on cupping results
- **Cost**: Sample roasting is insurance against ruining expensive coffee
