## üìã Overview

Peru Cajamarca Caturra Organic Washed represents the modern success story of Peruvian specialty coffee. Peru has emerged as a major specialty coffee origin over the past 20 years, transitioning from bulk commodity producer to one of the world's largest organic coffee exporters. Cajamarca region in northern Peru produces exceptional high-altitude coffees with clean, balanced profiles.

**Key Characteristics**:
- Organic certification (Peru is world's #1 organic coffee exporter)
- Fair Trade and Direct Trade programs common
- Caturra variety (compact, high-quality mutation of Bourbon)
- Traditional washed processing for clarity
- Milk chocolate and citrus flavor profile
- Medium body with bright, clean acidity
- Excellent balance and drinkability
- Smallholder cooperative production model
- Sustainable agriculture and forest conservation
- Exceptional value for quality (accessible pricing)

Cajamarca, located in Peru's northern highlands near the Ecuadorian border, produces coffee at elevations from 1,400-1,900+ MASL. The region's combination of altitude, climate, volcanic-influenced soils, and traditional cultivation practices creates coffees of exceptional quality and consistency.

**Historical Significance**: Peru's coffee industry was revolutionized in the 1990s-2000s by the organic and Fair Trade movements. Previously producing low-grade commodity coffee, Peruvian farmers‚Äîoften organized in cooperatives‚Äîembraced organic certification and quality improvement. Today, Peru exports more organic coffee than any other country, and Cajamarca is at the heart of this transformation.

**Misconception Alert**: Some perceive Peruvian coffee as "basic" or commodity-grade due to its history and affordable pricing. Modern Peruvian specialty coffee, especially from high-altitude regions like Cajamarca, rivals Central American quality while remaining more accessible in price. This is exceptional value, not low quality.

## üå± Variety Information: Caturra

### Caturra Variety Origins & History

**Botanical Background**:
- **Species**: *Coffea arabica*
- **Varietal lineage**: Natural mutation of Bourbon
- **Discovery**: Brazil, 1930s (near Caturra, hence the name)
- **Characteristic**: Dwarf mutation (compact growth)
- **Introduction to Peru**: 1970s-1980s
- **Current status**: Widely planted across Latin America

**Why Caturra in Peru**:
- **Compact size**: Higher planting density possible (more trees per hectare)
- **Yield**: Higher production than Bourbon or Typica
- **Quality**: Excellent cup quality (Bourbon genetics)
- **Adaptation**: Thrives at high altitude
- **Sustainability**: Good for smallholder farms (manageable size)

**Genetic Characteristics**:
- Single-gene mutation causing dwarfism
- Otherwise genetically very similar to Bourbon
- Retains Bourbon's quality characteristics
- More productive than Bourbon (smaller but more compact)

### Caturra Plant Characteristics

**Tree Morphology**:
- **Height**: 1.5-2.5 meters (much shorter than Bourbon's 3-4 meters)
- **Shape**: Compact, conical, dense
- **Branching**: 45¬∞ branch angle, close internodes
- **Leaves**: Bronze-tipped young leaves (like Bourbon)
- **Density**: Can plant 5,000-8,000 trees/hectare (vs. 3,000-5,000 for Bourbon)
- **Management**: Easier to harvest and prune (height advantage)

**Cherry & Bean Characteristics**:
- **Cherry size**: Medium, round
- **Cherry color**: Red when ripe (Red Caturra) or yellow (Yellow Caturra, less common)
- **Beans per cherry**: Typically 2
- **Bean shape**: Round to oval, similar to Bourbon
- **Bean size**: Medium (screen 15-17 typical)
- **Density**: High at altitude (SHB equivalent)

**Yield Characteristics**:
- **Productivity**: 30-50% higher than Bourbon (more trees per area)
- **Per tree**: Lower than Bourbon per individual tree
- **Per hectare**: Higher due to planting density
- **Biennial bearing**: Less pronounced than Bourbon
- **Commercial viability**: Good balance of quality and yield

**Disease Resistance**:
- **Coffee Leaf Rust**: Susceptible (major challenge)
- **Coffee Berry Disease**: Moderately susceptible
- **Nematodes**: Moderate resistance
- **Management**: Requires good agricultural practices, shade, nutrition
- **In Peru**: Organic methods and shade reduce disease pressure

**Why Caturra Is Valued**:

## üîÑ Processing: Washed Method in Cajamarca

### Harvest Season & Timing

**Harvest Calendar**:
- **Main season**: May-September
- **Peak**: June-August
- **Duration**: 3-5 months
- **Passes**: 3-5 selective picking rounds
- **Interval**: 10-15 days between passes

**Ripeness Selection**:
- **Target**: Fully ripe cherries only
- **Brix**: 18-22¬∞ at optimal ripeness
- **Visual**: Deep red, uniform color
- **Training**: Cooperatives train pickers in selective harvesting
- **Quality control**: Pickers paid premium for ripe cherry only

**Cooperative Collection**:
- **Delivery**: Same-day or next-day to cooperative wet mill
- **Initial sorting**: Visual inspection, floatation
- **Weighing**: Payment based on ripe cherry weight
- **Traceability**: Lot tracking for quality feedback

### Washed Processing Step-by-Step

**Stage 1: Reception & Sorting** (Day 1)

- **Delivery**: Farmers bring cherries to cooperative wet mill
- **Visual inspection**: Staff check ripeness, reject defects
- **Flotation tank**: Cherries in water, floaters removed
- **Sorting effectiveness**: 2-5% removed (unripe, defects)
- **Quality commitment**: Cooperatives enforce standards

**Stage 2: Depulping** (Day 1, same day as harvest)

- **Equipment**: Mechanical disc depulpers
- **Process**: Skin and most pulp removed, parchment and mucilage remain
- **Adjustment**: Settings to avoid bean damage
- **Quality**: Unripe cherries don't depulp cleanly (easy to sort)
- **Pulp management**: Composted for organic fertilizer (circular system)

**Stage 3: Fermentation** (12-24 hours, Day 1-2)

**Method in Cajamarca**:
- **Dry fermentation**: Beans in tanks without water (common)
- **Duration**: 12-24 hours depending on temperature
- **Temperature**: 16-22¬∞C (cooler at altitude = longer fermentation)
- **Monitoring**: Experienced mill operators test mucilage breakdown
- **Goal**: Complete mucilage removal, clean acidity development

**Fermentation Science**:
- Natural enzymes and microbes break down pectin/mucilage
- Controlled fermentation enhances acidity and clarity
- Under-fermentation: Residual mucilage, processing defects
- Over-fermentation: Vinegar, sour, fermented off-flavors
- Optimal: Clean breakdown, bright acidity, sweet cup

**Stage 4: Washing** (Day 2)

- **Channels**: Running water washing channels
- **Agitation**: Beans agitated to remove fermented mucilage
- **Multiple rinses**: 2-3 rinse cycles
- **Density sorting**: Floaters removed (lower quality)
- **Water management**: Recycling systems in modern cooperatives
- **Result**: Clean parchment coffee

**Stage 5: Drying** (7-15 days, Day 2-17)

**Drying Methods in Cajamarca**:

**Sun Drying** (Primary Method):
- **Surface**: Raised beds (African beds) or patios
- **Layer thickness**: 2-3 cm for even drying
- **Raking**: Every 1-2 hours during sun exposure
- **Coverage**: Protected at night and during any rain
- **Duration**: 10-15 days to 10-12% moisture
- **Dry season advantage**: May-September is dry (reliable drying)

**Mechanical Drying** (Supplemental):
- **Equipment**: Guardiola dryers (some cooperatives)
- **Use**: Finish drying after sun pre-dry, or backup if weather threatens
- **Temperature**: Controlled 40-45¬∞C max
- **Advantage**: Quality control, weather independence

**Greenhouse Drying** (Growing Adoption):
- **Structure**: Clear plastic greenhouse over raised beds
- **Benefit**: Rain protection while allowing air circulation
- **Cost-effective**: Lower cost than mechanical
- **Quality**: Maintains sun-drying quality benefits

**Target Moisture**: 10-12% (optimal for storage and export)

**Stage 6: Storage** (Parchment Coffee)

- **Form**: Parchment coffee (protective layer retained)
- **Storage**: Cooperative warehouses, climate-controlled
- **Duration**: Weeks to months before hulling
- **Conditioning**: Moisture equalizes within beans
- **Traceability**: Lots tracked by farmer/region/date

### Dry Milling & Export Preparation

**Hulling**:
- **Timing**: Before export or sale to exporter
- **Process**: Parchment mechanically removed
- **Equipment**: Huller machines
- **Result**: Green coffee exposed

**Sorting & Grading**:
- **Screen sorting**: Size uniformity (15-17 typical)
- **Density sorting**: Gravity tables remove low-density beans
- **Electronic sorting**: Color sorters remove defects
- **Hand sorting**: Final quality control (2-3 passes)
- **Defect removal**: Broken, discolored, insect-damaged beans removed

**Organic & Fair Trade Certification**:
- **Documentation**: All processing documented for certification
- **Traceability**: Farm to export tracking
- **Inspection**: Annual certification inspections
- **Premium**: Organic (+$0.30/lb), Fair Trade (+$0.20/lb + social premium)

**Bagging & Export**:
- **Bags**: 60 kg or 69 kg jute bags (GrainPro liners for quality)
- **Marking**: Origin, grade, certifications, lot number
- **Export port**: Paita (northern Peru coast)
- **Destination**: North America, Europe, Asia specialty markets

### Impact of Washed Processing on Cup Profile

**Clean, Balanced Profile**:
- Washed processing creates clarity and balance
- Origin and variety characteristics expressed cleanly
- Chocolate and citrus notes shine
- Medium body, bright acidity
- No fruit interference from processing

**Cajamarca Quality Standards**:
- Cooperative quality control ensures consistency
- Selective harvesting improves cup quality
- Controlled fermentation creates clean acidity
- Careful drying preserves quality
- Result: Reliable, high-quality coffee
