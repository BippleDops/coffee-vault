## üìã Overview

Indonesia Sumatra Mandheling Wet-Hulled represents one of the most distinctive and polarizing coffee profiles in the specialty coffee world. This coffee is defined entirely by its unique processing method‚ÄîGiling Basah (wet-hulling)‚Äîwhich creates a flavor profile so different from other coffees that it has its own devoted following.

**Key Characteristics**:
- Unique wet-hulling (Giling Basah) processing method
- Earthy, herbal, and savory flavor profile
- Full to heavy body with syrupy mouthfeel
- Very low acidity
- Cedar, tobacco, and dark chocolate notes
- Consistent cup profile despite mixed varieties
- Traditional Indonesian coffee style
- Polarizing‚Äîloved by some, avoided by others

The Mandheling name refers to the Mandailing people of North Sumatra, though the coffee growing region has expanded beyond their original territory. Located around Lake Toba in North Sumatra, this region produces coffee with such a distinctive character that it has become synonymous with a particular style of coffee appreciated worldwide.

**Historical Significance**: Mandheling coffee has been exported since the Dutch colonial period (1600s-1900s), making it one of the longest-established specialty coffee origins. The wet-hulling process developed as a practical solution to Sumatra's high humidity climate, creating an unintentional flavor profile that has become the region's signature.

**Misconception Alert**: Many coffee professionals initially dismiss Mandheling as "defective" due to flavor notes (earthy, woody, tobacco) that would indicate defects in other coffees. However, these characteristics are intentional results of the wet-hulling process and are highly valued by Mandheling enthusiasts. This is not defective coffee‚Äîit's a completely different processing paradigm.

**Geographic Position**:
- **Province**: North Sumatra (Sumatera Utara)
- **Primary regions**: Lintong, Dolok Sanggul, Sidikalang, Takengon (Aceh)

- **Formation**: Supervolcano eruption ~74,000 years ago

- **Elevation**: Lake surface at 905 MASL
- **Impact**: Creates unique microclimate affecting coffee cultivation
- **Volcanic soil**: Extremely fertile from ancient eruption deposits
- **Cultural significance**: Home of Batak people, traditional coffee cultivators

3. **Sidikalang** (North of Lake Toba)
   - 1,200-1,500 MASL
   - Growing region reputation
   - Increasing specialty coffee production

4. **Takengon/Aceh Gayo** (North Sumatra/Aceh border)
   - Sometimes marketed as Mandheling
   - Distinct from traditional Lintong Mandheling
   - Different flavor profile tendencies

- **Slopes**: Steep volcanic mountainsides
- **Forest**: Traditional coffee grown under shade
- **Roads**: Limited infrastructure, challenging transport
- **Farms**: Scattered smallholdings, not plantations

**Climate Classification**: Tropical rainforest climate (Af)

## üå± Variety Information: Mixed Heirlooms

### Variety Context in Sumatra

Unlike many specialty coffee regions that emphasize specific varieties, Sumatra Mandheling is typically a mix of traditional varieties planted over decades. The emphasis is on processing and terroir rather than varietal purity.

**Common Varieties in Mandheling**:

1. **Typica** (Original Indonesian coffee)
   - Introduced by Dutch in 1600s-1700s
   - Tall plants, low yield
   - Quality potential high
   - Still found in older plantings

2. **Bergendal/Sidikalang** (Local Typica selections)
   - Adapted to Sumatra conditions
   - Similar to Typica but local selection
   - Named after regions
   - Traditional variety

3. **Ateng/Catimor** (Modern introductions, 1970s+)
   - Hybrid with Robusta genetics (Timor Hybrid)
   - Rust-resistant (critical in humid Sumatra)
   - Higher yielding
   - Lower cup quality than pure Arabica
   - Increasingly common due to economics
   - Controversial in specialty market

4. **Djember/S-lines** (Indonesian research varieties)
   - Developed by Indonesian coffee research
   - Mix of backgrounds
   - Rust resistance breeding focus
   - Variable quality

5. **Bourbon** (Less common)
   - Some farms cultivate
   - Better quality but lower yield
   - Less common than Typica/Ateng

**Why Variety Mixing Occurs**:
- **Historical**: Farms planted over decades with available varieties
- **Economic**: Farmers plant high-yield varieties (Ateng) among traditional
- **Practical**: Mixed planting provides income security
- **Cultural**: Varietal purity not traditional focus
- **Processing**: Wet-hulling process dominates flavor (variety less detectable)

**Grade 1 vs. Varieties**:
- Grade 1 (TP - Triple Picked) indicates defect sorting, not variety
- High-quality Mandheling minimizes Ateng content
- Best lots are Typica/traditional variety dominant
- Variety information often unavailable to buyers
- Processing method more important than variety for Mandheling character

### Plant Characteristics (Typica-dominant lots)

**Tree Morphology**:
- **Height**: 3-4 meters (traditional varieties)
- **Shape**: Tall, conical (Typica lineage)
- **Branching**: Wide branch angles
- **Leaves**: Bronze young leaves (Typica characteristic)
- **Shade tolerance**: Good (traditional agroforestry)

**Cherry & Bean Characteristics**:
- **Cherry size**: Medium to large
- **Cherry color**: Deep red when ripe
- **Beans per cherry**: Typically 2 (peaberry occasional)
- **Bean size**: Medium to large (screen 16-18 common)
- **Bean shape**: Elongated (Typica) or rounder (Ateng)
- **Density**: Good at altitude (not as dense as washed African coffees)

**Yield Characteristics**:
- **Traditional varieties**: Low (Typica 0.5-1 kg/tree)
- **Modern varieties**: Higher (Ateng 1-2 kg/tree)
- **Biennial bearing**: Common (alternate heavy/light years)
- **Sustainability**: Low input requirements in volcanic soil

**Disease Considerations**:
- **Coffee Leaf Rust**: Major issue in humid climate
- **Ateng adoption**: Primarily for rust resistance
- **Traditional varieties**: Require shade and good management
- **Organic methods**: Common (chemicals expensive, organic market demand)

### Wet-Hulling Impact on Variety Expression

**Critical Understanding**:
The wet-hulling (Giling Basah) processing method so dramatically affects flavor that variety characteristics become secondary. A Typica processed via wet-hulling tastes more like an Ateng wet-hulled than a Typica washed.

**Why Processing Dominates**:
- Parchment removal while beans at 40-50% moisture
- Exposed beans absorb environmental influences
- Enzymatic and microbial activity different from washed
- Physical bean damage during hulling affects flavor
- Drying at high moisture creates unique chemistry
- Result: "Mandheling character" regardless of variety

**Variety Still Matters For**:
- Bean size and screen size
- Overall quality potential
- Yield (economic viability)
- Disease resistance
- Subtle cup variation (expert tasters can detect)

## üîÑ Processing: Wet-Hulled Method (Giling Basah)

The Giling Basah (wet-hulling) process is what makes Sumatra Mandheling unique. This processing method exists almost nowhere else in the world and creates a flavor profile unlike any other coffee.

### Why Wet-Hulling Exists

**Historical Development**:
- Emerged during Dutch colonial period
- Response to Sumatra's high humidity and constant rain
- Economic necessity‚Äîfarmers needed fast cash flow
- Infrastructure limitations‚Äîno access to large wet mills
- Practical solution that created unique flavor

**Climate Necessity**:
- Sumatra has no reliable dry season
- Traditional sun-drying takes 2-4 weeks (impossible with frequent rain)
- Parchment coffee molds easily in high humidity
- Removing parchment allows faster drying
- Wet-hulling reduces total process time to 1-2 weeks

**Economic Drivers**:
- Smallholders need income quickly
- Cannot afford to hold coffee during long drying
- Village collectors purchase at parchment stage
- Risk transfer from farmer to collector/mill
- Faster turnover essential for farmer livelihood

### Wet-Hulling Step-by-Step Process

**Stage 1: Harvesting** (Varies by farm elevation)

- **Main harvest**: October-December (post-wet season)
- **Secondary harvest**: May-June (possible in some areas)
- **Method**: Hand-picking (terrain requires it)
- **Ripeness**: Variable (economic pressure to harvest quickly)
- **Cherry sorting**: Minimal at farm level
- **Transport**: To farmer's home for processing

**Stage 2: De-pulping** (Day 1, at farmer's small home mill)

- **Equipment**: Small hand-cranked or motorized pulper
- **Process**: Cherry skin and most pulp removed
- **Result**: Parchment coffee with mucilage attached
- **Timing**: Same day or next day after picking
- **By-product**: Pulp composted or fed to animals

**Stage 3: Overnight Fermentation** (12-24 hours, Day 1-2)

- **Method**: Beans in buckets or bags with mucilage attached
- **Duration**: 12-24 hours (not precisely controlled)
- **Purpose**: Loosen remaining mucilage
- **Environment**: Ambient temperature (warm, humid)
- **Monitoring**: Minimal‚Äîfarmer experience-based
- **Variation**: Batch-to-batch inconsistency normal

**Stage 4: Washing** (Day 2)

- **Method**: Hand-washing in buckets or local stream
- **Goal**: Remove loosened mucilage
- **Water source**: Well, stream, or irrigation water
- **Agitation**: Manual stirring and rubbing
- **Cleanliness**: Variable (not controlled like commercial wet mill)
- **Result**: Clean parchment coffee (Gabah)

**Stage 5: Pre-Drying** (Day 2-4, at farmer's home)

This is where Giling Basah begins to differ from washed processing:

- **Location**: Tarp outside home, roof, or small raised bed
- **Duration**: 1-3 days only (very short)
- **Target moisture**: 40-50% (vs. 10-12% for fully dried parchment)
- **Conditions**: Whatever weather allows
- **Coverage**: If rain threatens, brought inside
- **Inconsistency**: Major variable in process
- **Goal**: Dry just enough to transport and hull

**Stage 6: Sale to Collector** (Day 3-5, KEY ECONOMIC STEP)

- **Form**: Semi-dried parchment coffee (Gabah)
- **Moisture**: 40-50% (still quite wet)
- **Location**: Village collector or local market
- **Payment**: Immediate cash to farmer
- **Price**: Based on weight and quality visual inspection
- **Farmer exit point**: Farmer's involvement ends here

**Stage 7: Wet-Hulling** (Day 4-6, at collector's facility, THE DEFINING STEP)

This is the "Giling Basah" (wet-hulling) step that gives the process its name and creates Mandheling's unique character:

- **Equipment**: Mechanical huller (removes parchment)
- **Bean moisture**: 40-50% (extremely high vs. normal 10-12%)
- **Process**: Parchment mechanically stripped off wet beans
- **Challenge**: High-moisture beans are soft, damage easily
- **Result**: Green coffee exposed at high moisture content
- **Appearance**: Beans are whitish-green, swollen, soft
- **Defects**: Physical damage common (broken beans, hull fragments)
- **Critical impact**: Exposed beans absorb environmental influences

**Why This Creates Unique Flavor**:
- **High moisture exposure**: Beans absorb humidity, environmental microbes
- **Enzymatic activity**: Different from dry parchment-protected beans
- **Physical damage**: Hulling wet beans causes surface damage
- **Oxidation**: Exposed beans oxidize differently
- **Microbial influence**: Environmental microbes affect flavor development
- **Chemical changes**: Lipid and acid profiles altered
- **Result**: Earthy, herbal, savory notes characteristic of Mandheling

**Stage 8: Final Drying** (Day 5-15, at collector or mill)

- **Form**: Green coffee (no parchment protection)
- **Initial moisture**: 40-50%
- **Target moisture**: 11-13% (slightly higher than washed coffees)
- **Location**: Larger tarps, concrete patios, or covered areas
- **Duration**: 5-12 days depending on weather
- **Method**: Sun drying when possible, covered during rain
- **Raking**: Regular turning to prevent mold
- **Challenge**: Exposed beans at high moisture are mold-prone
- **Color change**: Whitish-green ‚Üí blue-green (characteristic Mandheling color)
- **Monitoring**: Visual and moisture meter testing

**Color Development During Drying**:
The distinctive blue-green color of Sumatra Mandheling develops during this stage:
- Wet-hulled beans start whitish-green
- Oxidation and drying create blue-green hue
- Different from bright green of washed coffees
- Color indicates proper wet-hull process
- Darker blue-green can indicate older crop

**Stage 9: Sorting & Grading** (At exporter's facility)

- **Screen sorting**: Separate by bean size (Grade 1 = large, uniform)
- **Density sorting**: Remove low-density beans (defects)
- **Hand sorting**: Multiple passes to remove defects
- **Grade 1 (TP - Triple Picked)**: Highest grade
  - Minimal defects (0-11 per 300g sample)
  - Large beans (screen 18+)
  - Uniform color
  - Three hand-sorting passes
- **Lower grades**: Grade 2, Grade 3 (more defects, smaller beans)

**Stage 10: Bagging & Export**

- **Form**: Green coffee (wet-hulled, dried, sorted)
- **Bagging**: 60 kg jute bags (export standard)
- **Marking**: Origin, grade, crop year
- **Storage**: Warehouse until shipment
- **Export**: Through Medan port (North Sumatra)
- **Destination**: Global specialty coffee market

### Wet-Hulling vs. Washed Processing Comparison

| Aspect | Wet-Hulled (Giling Basah) | Washed (Traditional) |
|--------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| **Parchment removal** | At 40-50% moisture | At 10-12% moisture (after drying) |
| **Total time** | 10-15 days | 21-30+ days |
| **Drying form** | Exposed green beans | Protected by parchment |
| **Climate suitability** | High humidity | Needs dry season |
| **Defect risk** | Higher (physical damage, mold) | Lower (parchment protection) |
| **Flavor profile** | Earthy, herbal, savory | Clean, bright, acidic |
| **Body** | Full to heavy | Light to medium |
| **Acidity** | Very low | Medium to high |
| **Farmer income** | Faster (3-5 days to payment) | Slower (3-4 weeks) |
| **Geographic distribution** | Primarily Indonesia | Worldwide |

### Impact on Cup Profile

**Flavor Characteristics Created by Wet-Hulling**:

1. **Earthy Notes**:
   - Direct result of environmental exposure during drying
   - Soil microbes and environmental influences absorbed
   - Not a defect‚Äîintentional characteristic
   - Ranges from subtle earth to pronounced mushroom/forest floor

2. **Herbal & Cedar Notes**:
   - Enzymatic changes during high-moisture hulling
   - Oxidation of exposed beans
   - Creates wood, cedar, herbaceous flavors
   - Spice notes (sage, thyme, tobacco)

3. **Low Acidity**:
   - Acid degradation during wet-hulling
   - Different acid profile than washed coffees
   - Smooth, mellow cup
   - Appeals to low-acid coffee seekers

4. **Full to Heavy Body**:
   - Lipid and protein development during process
   - Syrupy, viscous mouthfeel
   - Coating on palate
   - Mouthfeel is Mandheling hallmark

5. **Chocolate & Dark Fruit**:
   - Dark chocolate (not milk chocolate)
   - Dried fruit (raisin, prune)
   - Caramelized sugar (dark roasts)
   - Molasses, brown sugar

**Visual Bean Characteristics**:
- **Color**: Blue-green (vs. bright green of washed)
- **Appearance**: Mottled, irregular color
- **Surface**: May show damage from wet-hulling
- **Uniformity**: Less uniform than washed coffees
- **Not defects**: These are characteristics of process

### Roast Level Recommendations

**Light Roast (City)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Drop temperature**: 400-410¬∞F (204-210¬∞C)
- **Development time**: 45-60 seconds post-crack
- **Characteristics**:
  - Pronounced earthy and herbal notes
  - Grassy, vegetal risk
  - Lighter body (for Mandheling)
  - Dried fruit notes emerge
  - Acidity still very low
- **Best for**: Exploring Mandheling terroir
- **Limitations**: Can be too earthy/harsh for many preferences
- **Recommendation**: Uncommon, specialty use only

**Medium Roast (Full City)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Drop temperature**: 415-425¬∞F (213-218¬∞C)
- **Development time**: 75-90 seconds post-crack
- **Characteristics**:
  - Earthy and herbal balanced
  - Chocolate notes developed
  - Full body
  - Cedar and tobacco prominent
  - Complexity peak
- **Best for**: Exploring Mandheling character
- **Appeal**: Balance of terroir and roast development
- **Use case**: Specialty roasters, enthusiasts

**Medium-Dark Roast (Vienna)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **OPTIMAL**
- **Drop temperature**: 430-440¬∞F (221-227¬∞C)
- **Development time**: Approaching or into 2nd crack (10-20 seconds)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Earth mellowed, chocolate forward
  - Spice notes prominent (clove, pepper)
  - Syrupy body maximized
  - Dark sweetness (molasses, brown sugar)
  - Low acidity, smooth
- **Best for**: Traditional Mandheling presentation
- **Why ideal**: Showcases body and smoothness, mellows earthy intensity
- **Market**: Most common commercial roast level

**Dark Roast (French)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **TRADITIONAL**
- **Drop temperature**: 440-450¬∞F (227-232¬∞C)
- **Development time**: Well into second crack
- **Characteristics**:
  - Roast flavors prominent
  - Earth becomes background note
  - Dark chocolate, char, smoke
  - Extremely heavy body
  - Zero acidity
  - Bittersweet, rich
- **Best for**: Traditional dark roast lovers, espresso
- **Historical**: Traditional European roast style for Sumatra
- **Appeal**: Smooth, heavy, bold without harsh acidity

**Very Dark Roast (Italian/Spanish)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Drop temperature**: 450¬∞F+ (232¬∞C+)
- **Development time**: Extended second crack
- **Characteristics**:
  - Roast flavors dominate
  - Origin character minimal
  - Charred, smoky, bittersweet
  - Thin body (oils expressed)
  - Extremely low acidity
- **Use case**: Traditional espresso, dark roast specialty
- **Trade-off**: Mandheling character largely lost

### Roast Profile Development

**Bean Density Considerations**:
- Mandheling is less dense than washed high-altitude coffees
- Wet-hulling creates different bean structure
- More porous due to processing
- Absorbs heat faster than dense washed beans
- Easier to scorch if not careful
- Benefits from moderate heat application

**Moisture Content**:
- Typically 11-13% (slightly higher than washed 10-12%)
- Higher moisture from wet-hulling process
- Drying phase may be slightly longer
- Moisture meter check recommended

**Bean Appearance**:
- Blue-green color (vs. bright green)
- Irregular coloring normal
- Surface irregularities from wet-hulling
- Larger beans typical in Grade 1
- Screen 18+ common

**Suggested Profile** (Medium-Dark Roast - 12 lb batch):

**Pre-heat**: 400-425¬∞F (204-218¬∞C)

**Charge Temperature**: 370-390¬∞F (188-199¬∞C)
- Lower than dense washed beans (Mandheling less dense)

**Drying Phase** (0:00-6:30):
- **Target**: Gentle, even heat penetration
- **Rate of rise**: 15-20¬∞F/min at midpoint
- **Turning point**: 160-180¬∞F (71-82¬∞C), 1:00-1:30
- **Yellow point**: 5:30-7:00
- **Goal**: Thorough drying, avoid scorching
- **Color**: Blue-green ‚Üí yellow ‚Üí tan
- **Aroma**: Hay, grain

**Browning/Maillard Phase** (6:30-10:00):
- **Target**: Flavor development, sugar caramelization
- **Rate of rise**: 10-15¬∞F/min
- **Color development**: Tan ‚Üí brown
- **Aroma**: Toasty, nutty ‚Üí sweet
- **Heat management**: Steady heat application
- **Goal**: Develop complexity, avoid baking

**First Crack** (~10:00-11:00):
- **Onset temperature**: 380-390¬∞F (193-199¬∞C)
- **Duration**: 60-90 seconds
- **Energy**: Endothermic
- **Physical change**: Bean expansion
- **Monitoring**: Listen for crack progression

**Development Phase** (Post-Crack):
- **Target for medium-dark**: 90-120 seconds post-crack
- **Rate of rise**: 5-10¬∞F/min
- **Color**: Medium-dark brown
- **Aroma**: Chocolate, spice, sweet
- **Goal**: Reach 2nd crack onset

**Second Crack** (~12:00-13:00):
- **Onset**: 425-430¬∞F (218-221¬∞C)
- **Duration**: 10-20 seconds into 2nd crack
- **Sound**: Lighter, faster snapping
- **Smoke**: Increases
- **Decision point**: Drop soon after onset for medium-dark

**Drop**: 430-440¬∞F (221-227¬∞C)
- **Total time**: 12:00-14:00 minutes
- **Development ratio**: 15-20%
- **Visual**: Medium-dark brown, slight oil beginning
- **Aroma check**: Chocolate, spice, no burnt notes

**Cooling**:
- Immediate, aggressive cooling
- Target <100¬∞F within 4-5 minutes
- Air cooling (water misting acceptable but not necessary)

### Roasting Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge 1: Scorching (Common with Mandheling)**
- **Symptom**: Burnt, acrid flavors; tipping
- **Cause**: Too-fast heat, Mandheling's lower density
- **Solution**:
  - Lower charge temperature
  - Gentler heat during drying phase
  - Increase airflow
  - Watch rate of rise (don't exceed 20¬∞F/min in drying)

**Challenge 2: Baking (Flat, Dull Flavor)**
- **Symptom**: Flat, one-dimensional, bready/baked
- **Cause**: Stalled temperature, insufficient heat
- **Solution**:
  - Higher charge temperature
  - More aggressive heat in Maillard phase
  - Avoid temperature plateaus
  - Shorter total roast time

**Challenge 3: Excessive Earthiness**
- **Symptom**: Overwhelming muddy/dirty earth flavor
- **Cause**: Under-development, light roast on earthy coffee
- **Solution**:
  - Extend development time
  - Roast darker (medium-dark minimum)
  - Ensure complete first crack
  - Into second crack for traditional profile

**Challenge 4: Loss of Origin Character**
- **Symptom**: Tastes generic, no Mandheling character
- **Cause**: Over-roasting, too dark
- **Solution**:
  - Drop earlier (before deep 2nd crack)
  - Medium-dark is optimal, not Italian dark
  - Monitor color and aroma
  - Cup test to find sweet spot

**Challenge 5: Inconsistency Batch-to-Batch**
- **Symptom**: Different flavors each roast
- **Cause**: Variable green coffee (nature of wet-hulling)
- **Solution**:
  - Source from reliable supplier
  - Grade 1 only (better consistency)
  - Establish baseline profile and adjust
  - Accept some variation (part of Mandheling character)

### Degassing & Rest Period

**Post-Roast Degassing**:
- **CO2 release**: Mandheling releases CO2 slower than washed coffees
- **Optimal rest**: 3-7 days post-roast
- **Medium roast**: 3-5 days rest
- **Dark roast**: 5-7 days rest
- **Espresso**: 7-10 days for best results
- **Filter brewing**: 3-5 days minimum

**Flavor Development During Rest**:
- First 24 hours: CO2-dominated, sharp
- Days 2-5: Flavors integrate, body develops
- Days 5-10: Peak flavor (most balanced)
- Days 10-21: Still excellent, gradual decline
- After 21 days: Noticeably fading

## üçµ Brewing Recommendations

### Optimal Brew Methods by Roast Level

**French Press** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **IDEAL**
- **Best roast**: Medium-dark to dark
- **Why perfect**: Maximizes body, immersion suits low-acid profile
- **Ratio**: 1:14 (e.g., 32g : 450ml) - stronger than washed coffees
- **Grind**: Medium-coarse
- **Water temp**: 195-200¬∞F (90-93¬∞C)
- **Brew time**: 4:00-5:00 minutes
- **Technique**:
  - Add all water at once
  - Stir gently at 0:30
  - Place plunger on top (don't press)
  - Press slowly at 4:00-5:00
  - Pour immediately
- **Result**: Full-bodied, syrupy, rich chocolate and earth

**Pour-Over (V60, Kalita Wave)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Best roast**: Medium
- **Why good**: Explores complexity, lighter body showcases nuance
- **Ratio**: 1:15 (e.g., 20g : 300ml)
- **Grind**: Medium
- **Water temp**: 195-200¬∞F (90-93¬∞C)
- **Brew time**: 2:30-3:00 total
- **Technique**:
  - Bloom: 40g water, 30-45 seconds
  - Multiple pours to total
  - Gentle, circular pouring
  - Finish by 2:00, drain by 3:00
- **Result**: Cleaner, highlights herbal and chocolate notes, lighter body
- **Note**: Acidity still very low even with pour-over

**Cold Brew** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **EXCELLENT**
- **Best roast**: Medium-dark
- **Why excellent**: Smooth, chocolatey, naturally low-acid
- **Ratio**: 1:8 concentrate (125g : 1000ml)
- **Grind**: Coarse
- **Time**: 16-24 hours (room temp or refrigerated)
- **Method**: Immersion, strain through filter
- **Serving**: Dilute 1:1 or over ice
- **Result**: Extremely smooth, chocolate, minimal earth, syrupy body, zero acidity

**Moka Pot** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **TRADITIONAL**
- **Best roast**: Dark
- **Why excellent**: Traditional Italian preparation, suits dark roast
- **Grind**: Fine (but not espresso-fine)
- **Fill level**: Water to valve, coffee basket full (don't tamp)
- **Heat**: Medium-low, slow extraction
- **Time**: 4-6 minutes
- **Result**: Concentrated, rich, bold, heavy body, traditional

**Espresso** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Best roast**: Dark
- **Why good**: Heavy body, low acid, chocolate notes
- **Dose**: 18-20g (double shot)
- **Yield**: 36-40g (1:2 ratio)
- **Time**: 25-30 seconds
- **Water temp**: 198-200¬∞F (92-93¬∞C)
- **Pressure**: 9 bars
- **Result**:
  - Straight: Heavy, syrupy, chocolate, earth
  - Milk drinks: Rich, bold, cuts through milk
  - Traditional espresso blend component

**Drip/Auto-Drip** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Best roast**: Medium-dark
- **Why good**: Reliable, daily drinking, smooth
- **Ratio**: 1:15 (e.g., 60g : 900ml)
- **Grind**: Medium
- **Water temp**: 195-200¬∞F (SCA certified machine)
- **Brew time**: Auto (typically 5-7 minutes)
- **Result**: Smooth, rich, full-bodied, easy-drinking daily coffee

**AeroPress** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Best roast**: Medium to medium-dark
- **Why good**: Versatile, forgiving, clean cup
- **Standard recipe**:
  - Dose: 17g
  - Water: 220ml
  - Temp: 190-195¬∞F
  - Steep: 2:00
  - Press: Gentle, 30 seconds
- **Inverted method**: Recommended for longer steep
- **Result**: Clean, balanced, good body, low bitterness

**Chemex** ‚≠ê‚≠ê
- **Best roast**: Medium
- **Why limited**: Thick filter reduces body (Mandheling's strength)
- **Ratio**: 1:16
- **Grind**: Medium-coarse
- **Result**: Cleaner but loses signature body
- **Recommendation**: Use other methods to maximize Mandheling character

### Water Quality & Chemistry

**Ideal Water Profile for Mandheling**:
- **TDS**: 75-125 ppm (moderate to moderately low)
- **Hardness**: 40-80 ppm as CaCO‚ÇÉ
- **Calcium**: 20-50 ppm
- **Magnesium**: 10-20 ppm
- **Alkalinity**: 30-50 ppm (lower than for high-acid coffees)
- **pH**: 6.5-7.0

**Why This Matters**:
- Mandheling has very low acidity already
- Lower alkalinity avoids over-buffering (keeps some brightness)
- Moderate mineralization extracts chocolate and body
- Lower TDS than high-acid coffees (prevents over-extraction)

**Water Issues to Avoid**:
- **High alkalinity**: Will make Mandheling taste flat (no acid to buffer)
- **Too soft**: Under-extraction (though less critical than with bright coffees)
- **Chlorine**: Off-flavors (always filter)
- **Hard water**: Can emphasize earthy notes excessively

### Grinding Recommendations

**Grind Size Impact**:
- **Too fine**: Over-extraction, excessive bitterness, muddy
- **Too coarse**: Under-extraction, sour (though Mandheling rarely sour), weak
- **Just right**: Balanced extraction, chocolate sweetness, clean earth

**Grinder Recommendations**:
- **Burr grinder**: Essential (flat or conical)
- **Blade grinder**: Avoid (uneven particles)
- **Grind fresh**: Immediately before brewing

**Grind Size by Method**:
- **Espresso**: Fine (20-25 seconds extraction)
- **Moka Pot**: Fine-medium
- **AeroPress**: Medium-fine to medium
- **Pour-over**: Medium
- **Drip**: Medium
- **French Press**: Medium-coarse
- **Cold brew**: Coarse

### Brewing Troubleshooting

**Problem**: Muddy, dirty, excessively earthy
- **Causes**: Over-extraction, too-fine grind, low-quality green
- **Solutions**:
  - Coarser grind
  - Lower water temperature
  - Shorter brew time
  - Source Grade 1 only
  - Roast darker (mellows earth)

**Problem**: Flat, dull, boring
- **Causes**: Under-extraction, old roast, stale coffee
- **Solutions**:
  - Finer grind
  - Hotter water
  - Longer brew time
  - Use fresher coffee (within 3 weeks of roast)

**Problem**: Bitter, harsh
- **Causes**: Over-extraction, too-fine grind, too-hot water
- **Solutions**:
  - Coarser grind
  - Lower water temp (195¬∞F vs 205¬∞F)
  - Shorter brew time

**Problem**: Weak, thin body
- **Causes**: Too-coarse grind, too-low coffee ratio
- **Solutions**:
  - Finer grind
  - More coffee (stronger ratio)
  - Use immersion method (French press)
  - Ensure medium-dark or darker roast

## üèÜ Quality Markers & Selection

### Identifying High-Quality Sumatra Mandheling

**Green Bean Indicators**:
- **Color**: Blue-green (characteristic Mandheling)
- **Uniformity**: Consistent color (mottling normal, but not extreme variation)
- **Size**: Large beans (Grade 1 = screen 18+)
- **Aroma**: Sweet, earthy (not moldy or sour)
- **Defects**: Minimal (Grade 1 = 0-11 defects per 300g)
- **Moisture**: 11-13% (slightly higher than washed)
- **Density**: Moderate (less than washed high-altitude)

**Label Information to Verify**:
- **Region**: "Lintong" or "Mandheling" (specific is better)
- **Grade**: Grade 1, TP (Triple Picked), or Grade 1 DP (Double Picked)
- **Processing**: "Wet-Hulled," "Giling Basah," or "Semi-Washed"
- **Organic**: If certified (verify certification)
- **Crop year**: Recent (current or previous year)
- **Altitude**: 1,200-1,600 MASL
- **Roast date**: Within 2-4 weeks (roasted coffee)

**Grade Breakdown**:
- **Grade 1 (TP - Triple Picked)**: 0-11 defects per 300g, screen 18+
- **Grade 1 (DP - Double Picked)**: 0-11 defects, screen 17-18
- **Grade 2**: 12-25 defects per 300g
- **Grade 3**: 26-44 defects per 300g
- **Always choose Grade 1**: Quality difference is significant

**Red Flags**:
- Old crop (2+ years)
- Grade 2 or 3 (excessive defects)
- Moldy or fermented aroma (defect, not characteristic)
- Extremely low price (<$3/lb green)
- No grade listed
- Dusty or faded color (old beans)

### Cupping & Evaluation

**Expected SCA Cupping Score**: 83-85 (high-quality Grade 1 lot)

**Tasting Notes to Expect**:
- Earth (clean, forest floor)
- Cedar, tobacco, herbaceous
- Dark chocolate, cocoa
- Syrupy, full body
- Warm spices (clove, pepper)
- Very low acidity
- Long finish

**Evaluation Tips**:
- **Don't use washed coffee standards**: Earthy is characteristic, not defect
- **Look for clean earth**: Not dirty, moldy, or medicinal
- **Assess body**: Should be notably full and syrupy
- **Check for defects**: Sour, vinegar, mold, phenolic = defects
- **Temperature evolution**: Cup hot, warm, and cool (changes significantly)
- **Compare to benchmark**: Use known good Mandheling as reference

**Defects to Watch For** (vs. Characteristics):
- **Characteristic**: Clean earth, forest floor ‚Üí **Defect**: Dirty, muddy, compost
- **Characteristic**: Herbal, cedar, tobacco ‚Üí **Defect**: Medicinal, chemical, harsh wood
- **Characteristic**: Low acidity ‚Üí **Defect**: Sour, vinegar, fermented
- **Characteristic**: Syrupy body ‚Üí **Defect**: Thin, watery
- **Characteristic**: Dark chocolate ‚Üí **Defect**: Burnt, harsh bitterness

## üìö Cultural & Historical Context

### History of Coffee in Sumatra

**Early Introduction** (1600s-1700s):
- Dutch East India Company brought coffee to Indonesia
- Initial plantings in Java (1696)
- Spread to Sumatra by early 1700s
- Colonial cash crop development

**Arabica to Sumatra** (1800s):
- Typica variety introduction
- Plantations established in highlands
- Export to Europe begins
- "Java" becomes generic term for coffee (though much was Sumatran)

**Coffee Leaf Rust Devastation** (1876-1890s):
- Hemileia vastatrix (coffee rust) arrived in Indonesia
- Decimated Arabica plantations
- Most production switched to Robusta (rust-resistant)
- Arabica survived in highlands of Sumatra, Sulawesi
- Traditional Typica cultivars maintained in North Sumatra

**Post-Colonial Era** (1945-1970s):
- Indonesian independence (1945)
- Smallholder coffee farming increases
- Wet-hulling process established as standard
- Mandheling name becomes recognized export term

**Modern Specialty Era** (1990s-Present):
- Specialty coffee market discovers Sumatra
- Wet-hulling recognized as unique processing
- Organic and direct trade programs develop
- Quality improvement initiatives
- Mandheling becomes specialty staple despite unconventional profile

### Mandheling Name & Culture

**Name Origin**:
- **Mandailing people**: Ethnic group in North Sumatra
- **Region**: Originally Mandailing region, now broader
- **Language**: Mandailing is Austronesian language
- **Spelling**: "Mandheling" is Dutch-influenced spelling
- **Marketing**: Name now applies to broader North Sumatra coffee

**Cultural Context**:
- **Batak people**: Dominant ethnic group around Lake Toba
- **Traditional farming**: Smallholder family farms
- **Agroforestry**: Coffee under shade of native trees
- **Community processing**: Village-level collectors and processors
- **Economic importance**: Coffee is primary cash crop for rural families

**Local Coffee Culture**:
- Indonesians traditionally drink coffee very sweet
- Kopi Tubruk: Traditional preparation (coffee grounds in cup, settles)
- Warung Kopi: Traditional coffee shops/stalls
- Social gathering around coffee
- Robusta more common for local consumption (Arabica for export)

### Economic & Social Impact

**Smallholder Economics**:
- Average farm: 0.5-2 hectares
- Coffee primary income source
- Wet-hulling enables fast payment (critical for cash flow)
- Price volatility impacts livelihoods
- Cooperative membership growing but still minority

**Labor & Seasonality**:
- Family labor primary
- Harvest season employment (October-December)
- Processing labor at village collector level
- Export facility employment (sorting, grading)

**Challenges**:
- **Price volatility**: Commodity market dependence
- **Quality improvement**: Wet-hulling limits specialty potential
- **Climate change**: Rainfall pattern changes affecting processing
- **Generational shift**: Youth leaving coffee farming
- **Infrastructure**: Poor roads increase costs

**Opportunities**:
