## üìã Overview

Panama Boquete Geisha Washed represents the absolute pinnacle of specialty coffee achievement. This is the coffee that revolutionized the specialty coffee world in 2004 when Hacienda La Esmeralda's Geisha scored unprecedented cupping scores (90-94 points) and sold for record-breaking auction prices, launching "Geisha mania" that continues today. This coffee defined what ultra-premium specialty coffee means in the 21st century.

**Key Characteristics**:
- **Most famous coffee**: 2004 Best of Panama changed specialty coffee forever
- **Geisha variety at origin**: Panama perfected Geisha cultivation and expression
- **Washed processing**: Clean, articulate expression of Geisha's ethereal character
- **Jasmine and bergamot**: Signature floral notes (Earl Grey tea character)
- **Tea-like delicacy**: Light body, refined elegance, complex aromatics
- **Extreme brightness**: Very high acidity, sweet and smooth despite intensity
- **Competition-grade**: Regularly scores 88-95+ points in professional cupping
- **Ultra-premium pricing**: $30-150+/12oz roasted ($15-100+/lb green at auction)
- **Limited production**: Tiny volumes from select farms, high demand
- **Cultural icon**: Symbol of specialty coffee's potential and evolution

**Historical Significance**:

In 2004, Hacienda La Esmeralda entered a lot of coffee from their Jaramillo section in the Best of Panama competition. The coffee, from the Geisha variety planted decades earlier as a windbreak, scored so highly that judges recupped it multiple times to verify. The subsequent auction sale at $21/lb green (5-10x normal specialty prices) sent shockwaves through the industry.

That moment transformed specialty coffee. It proved that variety mattered as much as or more than origin. It showed consumers would pay extreme premiums for exceptional quality. It launched a global search for Geisha plantings and sparked investment in ultra-premium coffee production. Every high-scoring, high-priced specialty coffee since 2004 owes a debt to that first Esmeralda Geisha lot.

**Why Panama Geisha Is Special**:

While Geisha originated in Ethiopia and was distributed throughout Central America for research in the 1960s-70s, Panama's specific terroir‚Äîparticularly Boquete's volcanic soils, high altitude, cool temperatures, and microclimate‚Äîproved ideal for expressing Geisha's unique genetic potential. The variety that languished in obscurity elsewhere found its perfect home in Panama's highlands.

Washed processing allows Geisha's intrinsic character to shine without interference from fruit fermentation. The clean, bright, articulate cup showcases the variety's extraordinary aromatic compounds (especially linalool, which creates floral jasmine notes) and unique acid profile (citric, malic, tartaric) that together create the unmistakable Geisha experience.

**Market Position**:

Panama Geisha commands the highest prices in specialty coffee. Competition lots at Best of Panama auctions have sold for $100-1,000+/lb green. Retail roasted Geisha typically ranges $30-65/12oz for good lots, $65-150+ for competition lots. This isn't coffee for daily drinking‚Äîit's liquid art, a special occasion experience, an educational investment, or a collector's item.

**Misconception Alert**: 

Is Geisha worth the money? For most people, as daily coffee, no. As a special experience, educational reference, or gift for a serious coffee enthusiast, absolutely. The chemistry is real (measurably different aromatic compounds), the flavor is genuinely unique, and the cultural/historical significance justifies trying at least once. But expecting Geisha to be "100 times better" than $15/12oz coffee because it's 4x the price is setting up disappointment. It's different, special, and memorable‚Äînot magic.

### Climate & Weather Patterns

**Climate Classification**: Tropical highland climate with strong oceanic influence

**Temperature**:
- **Average annual**: 16-20¬∞C (61-68¬∞F) at coffee altitudes
- **Daytime highs**: 22-26¬∞C (72-79¬∞F)
- **Nighttime lows**: 12-16¬∞C (54-61¬∞F) at high altitude
- **Diurnal variation**: 8-12¬∞C (critical for quality)
- **Frost**: Never (tropical latitude, not high enough)
- **Cool season**: December-March (coffee harvest period)
- **Warm season**: April-November (vegetative growth)

**Critical for Geisha**: Cool nighttime temperatures (12-16¬∞C) slow plant respiration, allowing sugar accumulation rather than consumption. This extends flavor development and creates the intensity and complexity Geisha is known for.

**Rainfall & Seasons**:
- **Annual precipitation**: 3,000-4,500mm (extremely high, one of wettest coffee regions)
- **Wet season**: April-November (260+ rainy days/year)
- **Dry season**: December-March ("veranillo," relatively dry but still occasional rain)
- **Mist & cloud**: Year-round orographic clouds (moisture, temperature regulation)
- **Humidity**: High (70-90% year-round)

**Precipitation Pattern**:
- **Heaviest rain**: May, October, November (200-400mm/month)
- **Driest months**: January-March (50-150mm/month, harvest period)
- **Distribution**: Afternoon thunderstorms characteristic (orographic lifting)
- **Microclimates**: Rainfall varies dramatically by elevation and orientation

**Why High Rainfall Works** (Unlike Most Coffee Regions):
- **Excellent drainage**: Steep volcanic slopes prevent waterlogging
- **Defined dry season**: Harvest period (Dec-Mar) has lower rainfall (processing possible)
- **Moisture balance**: High rainfall + good drainage = optimal water availability without excess
- **Disease challenge**: High humidity = high rust pressure (Geisha's main vulnerability in Panama)

**Bajareque (Local Wind Phenomenon)**:
- **Definition**: Misty drizzle characteristic of Boquete
- **Frequency**: 200+ days/year
- **Effect**: Moderate temperature, increase humidity, reduce sun intensity
- **Benefit for Geisha**: Protects delicate plants from sun stress
- **Challenge**: Increases disease pressure (rust management critical)

**Sunlight & Cloud Cover**:
- **Cloud formation**: Daily orographic clouds at 1,400-1,800 MASL (afternoon)
- **Sunlight**: Filtered through clouds much of year
- **Intensity**: Strong at altitude when clear
- **Shade**: Traditional shade trees essential (protect Geisha, manage humidity)

**Seasonal Coffee Calendar**:
- **Flowering**: March-May (triggered by early wet season rains)
- **Cherry development**: May-November (wet season growth)
- **Ripening**: November-February (transition to drier season)
- **Harvest**: December-March (dry season, peak January-February)
- **Processing**: December-March (driest period enables washed processing)

### Volcanic Soil Composition: Volc√°n Bar√∫'s Gift

**Soil Parent Material**: Volcanic ash and tephra from Volc√°n Bar√∫ eruptions

**Volc√°n Bar√∫ Background**:
- **Type**: Stratovolcano (composite volcano)
- **Activity**: Last eruption ~400-500 years ago (recently dormant)
- **Deposits**: Multiple ash layers from periodic eruptions over millennia
- **Influence**: Entire Boquete region sits on volcanic deposits

**Soil Type**: Andisols (volcanic ash-derived soils)

**Characteristics**:
- **Color**: Dark brown to black (high organic matter, volcanic minerals)
- **Texture**: Loamy, friable, excellent structure
- **Drainage**: Exceptional (porous volcanic material despite high rainfall)
- **Water retention**: Good when needed (volcanic ash property, holds water despite drainage)
- **pH**: Moderately acidic (5.0-6.0), ideal for coffee
- **Depth**: Deep (2-5+ meters volcanic ash/soil over bedrock in many sites)
- **Organic matter**: Very high (5-12% in surface, climate + volcanic base)

**Mineral Content** (Volcanic Soil Richness):
- **Phosphorus**: High (volcanic minerals, critical for flowering and cherry development)
- **Potassium**: Abundant (volcanic source, essential for fruit quality)
- **Calcium & Magnesium**: Good levels (volcanic minerals, pH buffering)
- **Trace minerals**: Complete spectrum (iron, zinc, boron, manganese, copper)
- **Nitrogen**: Moderate (high rainfall leaches, but organic matter + fertilization compensate)
- **Silicon**: Very high (volcanic ash, disease resistance support)

**Allophane Content** (Unique to Volcanic Soils):
- **What it is**: Amorphous aluminum-silicate clay mineral (unique to young volcanic soils)
- **Properties**:
  - Enormous surface area (nutrient and water holding despite drainage)
  - Phosphorus fixation (binds P, requires higher fertilization)
  - Unique chemistry (creates distinctive terroir)
- **Impact on coffee**: Believed to contribute to Geisha's unique mineral complexity

**Terroir Expression in Cup**:
- **Mineral complexity**: Volcanic terroir creates layered, complex flavor
- **Floral aromatics**: Specific volcanic minerals may enhance aromatic compound production (hypothesis)
- **Clean flavors**: Excellent drainage prevents off-flavors from waterlogging
- **Intensity**: Nutrient-rich soil supports Geisha's flavor compound development
- **Unique fingerprint**: Panama Geisha tastes different from Costa Rican or Colombian Geisha (terroir)

**Soil Management for Geisha**:
- **Fertilization**: High inputs required (Geisha is nutrient-demanding, phosphorus fixed by soil)
- **Organic matter**: Shade trees, mulching maintain organic content
- **pH management**: Occasional liming (maintain 5.5-6.0 range)
- **Micronutrients**: Zinc, boron supplementation common (Geisha requirements)
- **Erosion control**: Cover crops, terracing on steepest slopes (preserve precious topsoil)

### Why Boquete Is Geisha's Perfect Home

**The "Terroir Lock" for Geisha**:

When Geisha was planted across Central America in the 1960s-70s for coffee leaf rust research, it was grown everywhere from lowland plantations to highland farms. Most places, it underperformed (low yields, no distinctive cup quality). In Boquete, specifically at high altitudes (1,500-1,900 MASL) on Volc√°n Bar√∫'s slopes, something clicked.

**The Key Factors**:

1. **Altitude**: 1,600-1,900 MASL creates slow maturation (7-9 months flower to ripe cherry)
   - Extended time allows complex flavor development
   - High altitude = cooler temps = slower cherry ripening = sugar accumulation
   - Geisha's genetic potential unlocked by altitude stress (eustress, beneficial stress)

2. **Cool Temperatures**: 12-16¬∞C nights critical
   - Geisha evolved in cool Ethiopian highlands (genetic adaptation to cool conditions)
   - Panama's high-altitude cool temps match evolutionary history
   - Cool nights = low respiration = sugar accumulation not consumption
   - Day/night temperature swing creates flavor intensity

3. **Volcanic Soil**: Complete nutrition + excellent drainage
   - Geisha is nutrient-demanding (requires rich soil)
   - Volcanic minerals create terroir complexity
   - Drainage prevents disease (critical for rust-susceptible Geisha)
   - Deep soil allows extensive root systems (stress tolerance, flavor concentration)

4. **Cloud Cover & Humidity**: Protects delicate Geisha
   - High altitude sun is intense (would stress Geisha without protection)
   - Orographic clouds provide natural filtered light
   - Bajareque mist moderates temperature extremes
   - Humidity challenge (rust) managed through fungicides and careful cultivation

5. **Water Availability**: Critical for washed processing
   - Clean mountain springs (processing water purity)
   - Year-round water (washing processing requires significant volumes)
   - Quality water = clean fermentation = clean cup

6. **Tradition & Expertise**: 20+ years Geisha specialization
   - Panama growers have refined Geisha cultivation (pruning, nutrition, harvesting)
   - Processing expertise (fermentation timing, drying for Geisha)
   - Lot selection (knowing which farm sections produce best Geisha)
   - Post-2004, Boquete became Geisha university (knowledge concentration)

**Economic Environment**:
- **Best of Panama competition**: Annual showcase drives quality and innovation
- **Auction prices**: Reward excellence (farmers can justify Geisha investment)
- **International buyers**: Direct relationships, premium pricing
- **Tourist destination**: Boquete's beauty attracts coffee tourism (farm revenue diversification)
- **Infrastructure**: Good roads, electricity, communication (despite remote location)

**Historical Timeline**:

### Geisha Cultivation in Panama (Boquete Methods)

**Planting & Establishment**:
- **Altitude selection**: 1,500-1,900 MASL ideal (lower altitude = generic cup, higher = Geisha magic)
- **Site selection**: Well-drained slopes, east to southeast aspect (morning sun, afternoon cloud protection)
- **Soil testing**: Volcanic soil with good depth required
- **Density**: 2,000-3,500 trees/hectare (lower than typical varieties, accounting for low yields)
- **Spacing**: 2.5-3m rows, 1-1.5m within row (hand harvesting access)
- **Shade trees**: Essential - Inga, Por√≥, fruit trees (30-50% canopy cover)

**Geisha Tree Management**:
- **Pruning**: Intensive (Geisha grows tall and leggy, requires management)
  - Height control pruning (keep trees 2-2.5m for harvesting)
  - Selective pruning (remove weak branches, open canopy for air circulation)
  - Renovation pruning (stumping rotation every 7-10 years)
- **Nutrition**: Very high fertilization requirements
  - 4-6 applications per year (NPK + micronutrients)
  - Geisha is nutrient-demanding (low yields but high quality requires nutrition)
  - Foliar sprays (micronutrients, especially zinc and boron)
  - Organic matter (compost, shade tree leaf litter)
- **Disease management**: Critical (rust is main challenge)
  - **Coffee Leaf Rust**: Geisha moderately susceptible, Panama's humidity = high pressure
  - **Fungicide applications**: 3-5 per year typical (copper-based, systemic)
  - **Monitoring**: Regular scouting, early detection
  - **Sanitation**: Remove infected leaves, clean harvest debris
- **Shade management**: Balance rust pressure vs. tree protection
  - More shade = lower rust (cooler, but also more humidity)
  - Less shade = more sun stress but better air circulation
  - Panama optimal: 30-40% shade (compromise)

**Harvesting Geisha** (Critical for Quality):
- **Timing**: December-March (dry season)
- **Ripeness**: Absolutely critical‚Äîonly perfectly ripe cherries (18-22¬∞ Brix)
- **Method**: 100% selective hand-picking
- **Passes**: 6-10 selective passes through trees (Geisha ripens unevenly)
- **Training**: Pickers trained specifically for Geisha (recognize optimal ripeness)
- **Daily picking**: Small volumes (pickers can't fill bags quickly, scattered ripe cherries)
- **Same-day processing**: Ripe cherries must be processed within hours (quality preservation)
- **Lot separation**: Geisha kept completely separate from other varieties (entire chain)

**Yield Reality** (Why Geisha Is Expensive):
- **Typical yield**: 3-8 quintales/hectare (1 quintal = 100 lbs = 45.4 kg)
  - Equivalent to: 135-360 kg cherry/hectare
  - Green coffee: ~27-72 kg/hectare (assuming 20% yield cherry to green)
- **Comparison**: Standard variety yields 20-40 quintales/hectare
- **Result**: Geisha yields 15-25% of normal varieties
- **Economic reality**: Only viable with 5-10x prices
- **Best lots**: Even lower yields (extreme altitude, selective picking = tiny volumes)

**Quality Variations** (Even Within Geisha):
- **Altitude**: Higher altitude (1,700-1,900 MASL) = better scores (more pronounced florals)
- **Farm section**: Specific sections of farms produce better Geisha (microclimate factors)
- **Tree age**: 10-20 year old trees often peak quality (established roots, balanced vigor)
- **Harvest timing**: Peak ripeness picking produces best lots (requires perfect timing)
- **Processing precision**: Fermentation timing critical (hours matter for Geisha)

## üîÑ Processing: Washed (Wet) Process - Panama Method for Geisha

### Why Washed Processing for Geisha

**Philosophy**: Washed processing removes all fruit before drying, allowing the bean's intrinsic character to express without fruit interference. For a variety as distinctive as Geisha, washed processing is a clear window to the genetic potential.

**Benefits for Geisha**:
- **Clarity**: Floral and tea-like notes unmasked by fruit
- **Brightness**: Natural high acidity fully expressed
- **Complexity**: Geisha's aromatic compounds shine (no fruit competition)
- **Consistency**: Easier to achieve uniform quality batch-to-batch
- **Classic**: The 2004 revolution coffee was washed (established the standard)

**Comparison**:
- **vs. Natural Geisha**: Natural adds fruit sweetness and body, but can overwhelm delicate florals
- **vs. Honey Geisha**: Honey balances florals with sweetness (excellent but different)
- **Washed as benchmark**: Most Geisha judged by washed processing standard

### Panama Washed Processing for Geisha (Premium Standard)

**Stage 1: Harvest - The Foundation** (December-March)

**Selective Picking Requirements**:
- **Ripeness**: Only deep red cherries (yellow for Yellow Geisha), 20-22¬∞ Brix minimum
- **Selection intensity**: 6-10 passes (Geisha ripens very unevenly, sparse production)
- **Daily volumes**: Small (10-30 kg per picker typical, vs. 60-100 kg for other varieties)
- **Timing**: Harvest only during optimal ripeness window (2-3 days per cherry cluster)
- **Skill**: Pickers trained specifically for Geisha (premium paid for careful selection)

**Post-Harvest Handling**:
- **Transport**: Immediate (cherries must reach mill within 3-6 hours)
- **Protection**: Shade during transport (prevent sun fermentation)
- **Volume management**: Small lots (20-100 kg typical for ultra-premium lots)
- **Lot separation**: Geisha tagged from field through export (traceability, prevent mixing)

**Stage 2: Reception & Sorting** (Day 1, Within Hours of Harvest)

**Initial Inspection**:
- **Visual check**: Color uniformity, size, no defects
- **Brix measurement**: Some farms test sugar content (verify ripeness, 20¬∞ Brix minimum)
- **Volume recording**: Exact cherry weight (traceability, yield calculation)

**Flotation Sorting** (Premium Standard):
- **Method**: Cherries in water tanks (fresh, clean mountain spring water)
- **Separation**:
  - **Floaters**: Unripe, dried, insect-damaged, low-density (discarded or lower grade)
  - **Sinkers**: Perfectly ripe, dense, high-quality (premium Geisha lot)
- **Geisha standards**: Only sinkers used for ultra-premium lots (floaters can be 5-15% even with good picking)
- **Water quality**: Clean, temperature controlled (15-20¬∞C optimal)

**Additional Sorting** (Competition-Grade Lots):
- **Hand sorting**: Post-flotation visual inspection (remove any questionable cherries)
- **Color sorting**: Electronic color sorters (some high-tech farms)
- **Size sorting**: Screens separate by size (uniformity for even depulping)

**Stage 3: Depulping** (Day 1, Immediately After Sorting)

**Precision Depulping**:
- **Equipment**: Eco-pulpers (gentle, water-efficient) or disc depulpers (adjustable gap)
- **Settings**: Calibrated for Geisha (bean size, cherry skin thickness)
- **Goal**: Remove skin and most pulp, leave mucilage intact, zero bean damage
- **Monitoring**: Check depulped beans continuously (adjust if seeing damaged beans)
- **Water use**: Minimal water (modern eco-pulpers use 1-2 liters/kg vs. 10+ liters traditional)

**Pulp Removal**:
- **Skin**: Completely removed
- **Pulp**: Mostly removed (some pulp residue normal)
- **Mucilage**: 100% retained (pectin-rich, sticky layer critical for fermentation)
- **Parchment**: Intact (protective layer remains until dry milling)

**Quality Control**:
- **Visual inspection**: Depulped beans examined (check for unripe "floaters" that slipped through)
- **Damaged beans**: Any damaged in depulping removed immediately
- **Consistency**: Batch uniformity (even mucilage coverage)

**Stage 4: Fermentation** (12-48 hours, Day 1-3)

This is the most critical stage for Geisha quality‚Äîfermentation timing is measured in hours, not days.

**Fermentation Method**:
- **Dry fermentation** (most Panama Geisha): Beans in tanks without added water
  - Natural enzymes (pectinase) break down mucilage
  - Exothermic process (generates heat, must monitor)
  - Faster than wet fermentation (Panama's warmer climate)
- **Wet fermentation** (some farms): Beans submerged in water
  - Slower, cooler fermentation
  - Different flavor development (cleaner but less complex)
  - More water use (sustainability concern)

**Duration Factors for Geisha**:
- **Temperature**: 18-25¬∞C ambient (higher altitude = cooler = longer fermentation)
- **Altitude correlation**: 
  - 1,400-1,500 MASL: 12-18 hours typical
  - 1,600-1,700 MASL: 18-30 hours
  - 1,800-1,900 MASL: 24-48 hours (coolest temps)
- **Mucilage thickness**: Riper cherries = thicker mucilage = longer fermentation
- **Batch size**: Larger batches generate more heat (faster fermentation)
- **Target**: Complete mucilage breakdown, clean parchment

**Monitoring** (Geisha-Specific Precision):
- **Temperature tracking**: Digital thermometers, every 2-4 hours
- **Touch test**: Mucilage texture changes (slippery ‚Üí rough, sticky ‚Üí clean)
- **Visual inspection**: Color and consistency
- **Sample washing**: Test beans in water (check if mucilage releases cleanly)
- **pH measurement** (high-tech farms): pH drops during fermentation (acidity indicator)
- **Time tracking**: Exact start time recorded (precision critical)

**The Fermentation Window for Geisha**:
- **Underfermented**: Residual mucilage, difficult drying, potential defects (slimy, grassy)
- **Optimal**: Clean parchment, complex acidity, floral clarity, maximum cup quality
- **Overfermented**: Vinegar notes, fermented flavor, stinker beans, ruined lot

**For ultra-premium Geisha**: Fermentation timing precision is 2-4 hours window (miss it = ruined lot worth $thousands)

**Stage 5: Washing** (Day 2-3, Post-Fermentation)

**Purpose**: Remove fermented mucilage completely, leaving clean parchment coffee

**Washing Process**:
- **Method**: Running water channels (traditional) or washing tanks with agitation
- **Water source**: Clean mountain spring water (Boquete's volcanic aquifer)
- **Water temperature**: 12-18¬∞C (cool mountain water, slows re-fermentation)
- **Agitation**: Gentle stirring or flow-induced movement (mechanical breaking of mucilage)
- **Duration**: 5-15 minutes washing, 2-3 rinse cycles
- **Quality check**: Visual inspection (parchment should be clean, light yellow-green)

**Final Density Sorting**:
- **Washing channels**: Gradient design (water flow separates by density)
- **Floaters**: Light beans (defects, immature) float to surface (removed)
- **Sinkers**: Dense, high-quality beans (premium lot)
- **Geisha standard**: Only sinkers proceed to drying

**Water Management** (Sustainability):
- **Volume used**: 10-20 liters water per kg cherry (significant)
- **Recycling**: Modern farms recycle washing water (treatment, reuse)
- **Wastewater treatment**: Settling ponds, biological treatment (environmental responsibility)
- **Pulp composting**: Byproducts composted (return to farm as fertilizer)

**Stage 6: Drying** (7-21 days, Day 3-24)

**Critical Drying Goals for Geisha**:
- **Slow, even drying**: Preserves delicate aromatics
- **Target moisture**: 10-11% (optimal for Geisha storage and green coffee stability)
- **No defects**: Over-drying, under-drying, or uneven drying ruins Geisha

**Drying Methods**:

**Raised Beds (African Beds)** - Premium Standard:
- **Structure**: Mesh screens on elevated frames (1-1.5m high, airflow underneath)
- **Surface**: Nylon or polypropylene mesh (food-grade, cleanable)
- **Layer thickness**: 1-2 cm (single layer, maximum air exposure)
- **Coverage**: Shade cloth during midday sun, tarps at night (dew prevention)
- **Why best for Geisha**:
  - Superior air circulation (even drying, preserves aromatics)
  - No ground contact (contamination prevention)
  - Visual monitoring (easy to spot issues)
  - Slow drying (7-21 days typical, controlled)

**Patio Drying** (Some Farms):
- **Surface**: Concrete or brick patios
- **Layer**: 2-3 cm
- **Challenge**: Ground contact, heat absorption (can over-dry)
- **Best practice**: Only for non-premium lots (top Geisha uses raised beds)

**Mechanical Drying** (Guardiola - Finish Only):
- **Use**: Final drying stage (patio/bed dry to 14-16% moisture, mechanical finish to 10-11%)
- **Temperature**: 35-40¬∞C maximum (higher damages Geisha aromatics)
- **Duration**: 8-16 hours (final moisture equalization)
- **Advantage**: Moisture precision (exact target moisture)
- **Limitation**: Aromatics can be damaged (use sparingly, low temps)

**Drying Timeline** (Raised Beds, Boquete Climate):

**Week 1 (Days 1-7)**:
- **Moisture**: 55-60% ‚Üí 30-35%
- **Turning frequency**: Every 30-60 minutes during sun exposure (8am-4pm)
- **Color**: Parchment light yellow-green, slight darkening
- **Aroma**: Fresh, grassy ‚Üí sweet, developing complexity
- **Coverage**: Shade cloth 12pm-3pm (prevent scorching), tarps overnight
- **Monitoring**: Temperature checks (parchment surface shouldn't exceed 35¬∞C)

**Week 2 (Days 8-14)**:
- **Moisture**: 30-35% ‚Üí 16-20%
- **Turning frequency**: Every 2-3 hours
- **Color**: Golden yellow parchment
- **Aroma**: Sweet, floral notes emerging
- **Coverage**: Tarps overnight (dew), occasional midday shade
- **Monitoring**: Daily moisture readings (handheld meter)

**Week 3 (Days 15-21)** - Final Drying:
- **Moisture**: 16-20% ‚Üí 10-11%
- **Turning frequency**: Once or twice daily
- **Color**: Light golden, stable
- **Aroma**: Developed, sweet, floral
- **Testing**: Moisture meter daily, parchment brittleness (breaks cleanly when bent)
- **Endpoint**: Precise 10-11% moisture target

**Boquete Drying Challenges**:
- **Humidity**: Even during "dry" season (Dec-Mar), humidity 60-80% (slows drying)
- **Bajareque**: Misty drizzle common (must cover immediately)
- **Rain risk**: Occasional rain even in dry season (covered beds or greenhouses essential)
- **Solution**: Many top farms use greenhouse-style covered raised beds (protection + airflow)

**Stage 7: Resting (Descanso)** (30-60 days post-drying)

**Purpose**:
- **Moisture equalization**: Moisture distributes evenly throughout bean
- **Flavor development**: Some chemical changes occur during rest (flavor improvement)
- **Stability**: Rested coffee is more stable for storage and export

**Storage Conditions**:
- **Form**: Parchment coffee (protective layer maintained)
- **Temperature**: 15-20¬∞C (cool storage)
- **Humidity**: 60-65% RH
- **Monitoring**: Periodic moisture checks (ensure stable 10-11%)
- **Protection**: Grain pro bags or controlled atmosphere (prevent moisture uptake)

**Stage 8: Dry Milling & Sorting** (Pre-Export or Pre-Roasting)

**Hulling**:
- **Timing**: Shortly before export or roasting
- **Process**: Mechanical hullers remove parchment layer
- **Result**: Green coffee bean exposed (blue-green color, elongated Geisha shape)

**Sorting** (Ultra-Premium Geisha Standard):

1. **Screen sorting**: Size separation
   - Geisha typically screen 14-17 (elongated beans, size varies)
   - Uniform size ensures even roasting

2. **Gravity sorting**: Density table
   - Removes low-density beans (defects, immature)
   - Only highest density beans in premium lots

3. **Electronic color sorting**: High-tech machines
   - Detects and removes discolored beans (quakers, defects)
   - Multiple passes (2-3) for premium lots

4. **Hand sorting**: Human visual inspection
   - Final quality control (zero defects for competition lots)
   - 3-5 hand sorting passes for ultra-premium Geisha
   - Experienced sorters remove:
     - Quakers (pale beans that didn't develop)
     - Broken beans, insect damage
     - Discolored beans, fungal damage
     - Any visual defects

**Final Quality Control**:
- **Sample roast**: Small batch roasted and cupped (verify quality)
- **Cupping score**: Professional cupping (SCA protocol)
- **Grading**: Based on defect count and cup score
- **Lot designation**: Competition, premium, or standard (price tiers)

**Packaging**:
- **Bags**: GrainPro bags (moisture barrier), 30-60 lb bags
- **Labeling**: Farm, section, variety, processing, lot number, harvest year
- **Storage**: Climate controlled until export or roasting

## üî• Roasting Panama Geisha Washed

### The Sacred Rule: Light Roast Only

**Non-Negotiable Truth**: Roasting Panama Geisha beyond light-medium is criminal waste of expensive coffee.

**Why**:
- **Aromatics destroyed**: Linalool (jasmine compound) degrades rapidly above 410¬∞F
- **Origin character lost**: Geisha's uniqueness comes from aromatics (destroyed by roast development)
- **Waste of money**: Paying $20-50/lb green for coffee roasted like $5/lb commercial is illogical
- **Philosophy**: You're buying Geisha for its unique character (preserve it)

**Acceptable Range**: Light to light-medium (City to City+)
**Absolute Maximum**: Light-medium+ (Full City-, 415¬∞F max)
**Beyond that**: Why did you buy Geisha?

### Roast Level Recommendations

**Light Roast (City)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **OPTIMAL**
- **Drop temperature**: 398-406¬∞F (203-208¬∞C)
- **Development time**: 45-65 seconds post-crack
- **Characteristics**:
  - Maximum jasmine, bergamot, floral intensity
  - Extreme brightness (citric acidity)
  - Tea-like delicacy fully expressed
  - All Geisha aromatics preserved
  - Light body, silky
- **Best for**: Cupping, pour-over, understanding Geisha, competition roasting
- **Why optimal**: This is what Geisha is about (if you want dark chocolate, buy Brazilian coffee)

**Light-Medium Roast (City+)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **ALSO EXCELLENT**
- **Drop temperature**: 406-413¬∞F (208-212¬∞C)
- **Development time**: 65-80 seconds post-crack
- **Characteristics**:
  - Jasmine and florals still dominant (slightly less intense)
  - Stone fruit more developed
  - Acidity slightly smoothed (still very bright)
  - Touch more body
  - More approachable for Geisha newcomers
- **Best for**: Daily drinking (if using Geisha daily), slightly more accessible
- **Why excellent**: Slight development adds approachability without destroying character

**Medium Roast (Full City-)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê **MAXIMUM ACCEPTABLE**
- **Drop temperature**: 413-418¬∞F (212-214¬∞C)
- **Development time**: 80-95 seconds post-crack (approaching too long)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Florals diminished (still present but no longer dominant)
  - Stone fruit, some caramel developing
  - Acidity moderate-high (brightness fading)
  - Body medium-light (increased slightly)
- **Concern**: Losing Geisha's unique character
- **Use case**: Only if you absolutely must have less acidity
- **Warning**: At this roast, Geisha doesn't justify its cost (good but not special)

**Medium+ to Dark** ‚≠ê **DO NOT DO THIS**
- You've destroyed what you paid for
- Roast character dominates variety character
- Wasted money
- Buy Brazilian coffee if you want medium-dark roast

### Roast Profile Development

**Geisha-Specific Challenges**:
- **Delicate aromatics**: Volatile compounds (linalool, geraniol) destroyed easily
- **Elongated bean shape**: May roast unevenly vs. round beans
- **High density**: Panama SHB Geisha is dense (requires adequate heat)
- **Expensive**: Mistakes cost $20-50/lb (sample roast first, always)
- **Unforgiving**: Small roast variations create large cup variations

**Recommended Profile** (Light Roast - 5 lb batch):

**Pre-heat**: 390-410¬∞F (199-210¬∞C)

**Charge Temperature**: 365-380¬∞F (185-193¬∞C)
- Conservative charge (protect delicate aromatics)
- Lower charge than typical coffee

**Drying Phase** (0:00-7:30):
- **Goal**: Gentle, even heat penetration (no baking, no scorching)
- **Rate of rise**: 10-15¬∞F/min at midpoint (gentle curve)
- **Turning point**: 130-150¬∞F (54-66¬∞C), 0:50-1:30
- **Yellow point**: 6:30-8:00 (patient drying)
- **Aroma**: Grassy ‚Üí sweet hay ‚Üí light grain
- **Color**: Green ‚Üí yellow ‚Üí light tan

**Browning/Maillard Phase** (7:30-11:00):
- **Goal**: Flavor development without destroying aromatics
- **Rate of rise**: 6-10¬∞F/min (moderate, controlled)
- **Heat management**: Gentle increase, avoid heat spikes
- **Aroma**: Grain ‚Üí floral hints ‚Üí jasmine emerging (you'll smell Geisha before crack)
- **Color**: Tan ‚Üí light brown
- **Critical**: This phase develops sweetness and body without destroying florals

**First Crack** (~11:00-12:00):
- **Onset temperature**: 380-392¬∞F (193-200¬∞C)
- **Crack character**: May be subtle (listen carefully)
- **Duration**: 60-90 seconds (should be distinct, audible)
- **Monitoring**: Watch and listen (Geisha crack can be quieter than other varieties)

**Development Phase** (Post-Crack):
- **Target**: 45-65 seconds post-crack for light roast
- **Rate of rise**: 2-5¬∞F/min (declining, slowing)
- **Aroma monitoring**: Should smell intensely floral, jasmine, bergamot (if roasting correctly)
- **Color**: Light brown (cinnamon to milk chocolate)
- **Endpoint decision**: Aroma is best guide (when it smells like jasmine tea, it's ready)

**Drop**: 398-406¬∞F (203-208¬∞C)
- **Total time**: 11:45-13:30 minutes
- **Development ratio**: 11-14% (shorter than typical varieties)
- **Visual**: Light brown, dry surface (no oil)
- **Aroma**: Intensely floral, jasmine, sweet

**Cooling**:
- **Immediately**: Maximum airflow (stop development instantly)
- **Target**: <100¬∞F within 3-4 minutes
- **Method**: Aggressive air cooling (water quench if equipment allows and roaster experienced)

**For Light-Medium (City+)**:
- **Extended development**: 65-80 seconds post-crack
- **Drop**: 406-413¬∞F (208-212¬∞C)
- **Total time**: 12:30-14:00 minutes
- **Visual**: Light-medium brown
- **Development ratio**: 13-16%

### Critical Roasting Tips

**Tip 1: Sample Roast First** (Non-Negotiable)
- **Always**: Roast 100-200g sample before production batch
- **Cup**: 24 hours rest minimum, evaluate
- **Adjust**: Fine-tune profile based on cupping
- **Insurance**: Don't risk $100+ green coffee without testing

**Tip 2: Aroma Is Your Guide**
- **During roast**: If you smell intense jasmine, you're on track
- **If smelling generic roast flavor**: You've gone too far (next batch pull earlier)
- **Trust your nose**: Your nose tells you more than thermometers for Geisha

**Tip 3: Err Light**
- **Philosophy**: Better 5¬∞F under than 2¬∞F over
- **Underdeveloped Geisha**: Still interesting (bright, floral, slightly grassy)
- **Overdeveloped Geisha**: Generic, lost character, wasted money
- **Safe side**: Pull early, taste, adjust next batch

**Tip 4: Cool Quickly**
- **Why**: Development continues during cooling (stop it immediately)
- **Method**: Maximum airflow, spread thin, cool aggressively
- **Target**: Under 100¬∞F within 4 minutes (faster than typical coffee)

**Tip 5: Rest Before Cupping**
- **Minimum**: 24 hours rest
- **Optimal**: 3-5 days rest (Geisha develops flavor during rest)
- **Maximum**: 2-3 weeks peak (then slow decline)
- **Don't judge**: Fresh off roaster (wait for degassing)

### Roasting Defects to Avoid

**Baking** (Underdevelopment):
- **Symptom**: Grassy, vegetal, hay-like, peanutty, lack of sweetness
- **Cause**: Insufficient heat during drying/browning phase
- **Fix**: Higher charge temp, increase heat in browning, shorter total time

**Destroyed Aromatics**:
- **Symptom**: Generic coffee flavor, no jasmine/floral, tastes like typical coffee
- **Cause**: Too-hot roast, over-development, too-dark roast
- **Fix**: Lower temps, shorter development, lighter roast (prevent, can't fix roasted batch)

**Scorching**:
- **Symptom**: Burnt, charred flavor, tipping (burnt bean tips), phenolic
- **Cause**: Excessive heat, too-fast temperature rise
- **Fix**: Lower charge temp, reduce gas during drying, increase airflow
- **Note**: Geisha's elongated beans may be more prone to tipping (tips burn before centers develop)

**Uneven Development**:
- **Symptom**: Facing (one side darker), quakers (underdeveloped beans), inconsistent color
- **Cause**: Uneven heat distribution, bean size variation, poor roaster setup
- **Fix**: Better drum movement, airflow management, sort green coffee by size pre-roast

**Environmental Note**: First few roasts in a roaster for the day may differ (machine temp stabilization). With expensive Geisha, wait until roaster is fully warmed up, or accept first batch as test.

**Cupping (SCA Protocol)** ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê **PROFESSIONAL EVALUATION**

### Water Chemistry - Critical for Geisha

**Geisha is the most water-sensitive coffee**:
- **Why**: Delicate aromatics and bright acidity affected dramatically by water composition
- **Recommendation**: Use bottled water with known composition OR remineralize RO water

**Ideal Water Profile**:
- **TDS**: 75-110 ppm (lower than typical recommendations)
- **Hardness**: 35-60 ppm as CaCO‚ÇÉ
- **Calcium**: 20-40 ppm
- **Magnesium**: 8-18 ppm (lower end, Geisha acidity already very high)
- **Alkalinity**: 25-45 ppm (low, preserve Geisha's brightness)
- **pH**: 6.5-7.0

**Why Lower Mineralization**:
- **Delicate aromatics**: High minerals can overpower subtle florals
- **High acidity**: Geisha already very acidic (don't need magnesium extraction boost)
- **Clarity**: Lower TDS allows complex flavor separation (notes don't muddle)

**Bottled Water Recommendations**:
- **Crystal Geyser**: ~70 ppm TDS (excellent for Geisha)
- **Volvic**: ~109 ppm (good)
- **Third Wave Water "Light" profile**: Formulated for light roast specialty (excellent option)

**Water Issues**:
- **Too hard** (>150 ppm): Overwhelms delicate Geisha florals
- **Too soft** (<50 ppm): Under-extraction, thin, sour (even for Geisha)
- **High alkalinity** (>60 ppm): Mutes Geisha's signature brightness
- **Chlorine**: Absolutely ruins aromatic coffee (always filter)

**Water Investment**: Spending $20/lb on Geisha and using tap water is false economy. Invest $5 in bottled water.

### How to Identify High-Quality Panama Geisha

**Green Coffee Indicators** (If Buying Green):
- **Bean shape**: Elongated, narrow (distinctive Geisha shape, instantly recognizable)

- **Uniformity**: Consistent bean size and color

**Label Information to Verify**:
- **Origin**: "Panama" and "Boquete" both specified (not just "Panama")
- **Variety**: "Geisha" or "Gesha" clearly stated (not "Geisha blend" or ambiguous)
- **Farm/Estate**: Specific farm named (traceability)

- **Lot information**: Specific lot or section (e.g., "Jaramillo section" for Esmeralda)
- **Harvest year**: Recent (current or previous year, freshness matters for aromatics)
- **Roast date**: Within 2-4 weeks (roasted coffee, freshness critical for florals)
- **Cupping score** (if listed): 87+ is good, 89+ is excellent, 91+ is outstanding

**Red Flags - Avoid**:
- **"Geisha blend"**: Not 100% Geisha (often 10-30% Geisha with filler, marketing gimmick)

- **Suspiciously low price**: <$25/12 oz roasted is questionable (good Geisha costs more)

### Geisha Authenticity - The Fraud Problem

**The Reality**: Geisha's extreme pricing creates incentive for fraud.

**Common Fraud Types**:
1. **Varietal fraud**: Other varieties sold as Geisha
   - Similar-looking elongated varieties (e.g., Laurina, Maragogipe)
   - Standard varieties mislabeled
2. **Blend fraud**: Small percentage Geisha (10-30%) sold as "Geisha"
3. **Origin fraud**: Other-origin Geisha sold as "Panama"
4. **Grade fraud**: Lower-altitude or lower-grade Geisha sold as premium

**How Common**: Industry estimates 10-30% of "Geisha" sold is mislabeled (some intentional fraud, some confusion).

**Verification Methods**:

**Genetic Testing**:
- **DNA testing**: Labs can verify variety genetically
- **Who tests**: Some importers DNA test (Caf√© Imports, Royal Coffee)
- **Consumer access**: Rarely available to end consumers
- **Cost**: Expensive ($100-500 per test)

**Visual Identification**:
- **Bean shape**: Geisha is distinctively elongated and narrow
- **Expertise required**: Experienced green buyers can identify by sight
- **Limitation**: Some varieties look similar (not foolproof)

**Cup Verification**:
- **Cupping**: Experienced cuppers can identify Geisha by flavor
- **Markers**: Jasmine, bergamot, tea-like character (if absent, likely not Geisha)
- **Limitation**: Poorly grown Geisha may not express well (could be real but bad quality)
- **Expertise**: Requires extensive cupping experience

**Source Verification** (Best Consumer Method):
- **Buy from reputable roasters**: Established specialty roasters with transparency
- **Direct trade**: Roasters with farm relationships (can verify source)
