## üìã Overview

Honduras Marcala Pacas Washed represents the success story of Honduran specialty coffee. Over the past 20 years, Honduras has transformed from an overlooked origin to Central America's largest coffee producer and a specialty coffee powerhouse. Marcala, Honduras's first Denomination of Origin (2005), leads this quality revolution.

**Key Characteristics**:
- **Marcala DO**: Honduras's premier coffee region, protected designation
- **Pacas variety**: Compact Bourbon mutation, excellent quality
- **SHG grade**: Strictly High Grown (1,200+ MASL)
- **Washed processing**: Clean, balanced, traditional Central American profile
- **Chocolate-caramel flavor**: Sweet, balanced, approachable
- **Medium-full body**: Satisfying mouthfeel
- **Medium-high acidity**: Bright but balanced
- **Cooperative production**: Strong cooperative tradition
- **Excellent value**: High quality at accessible prices
- **Consistency**: Reliable year-to-year cup profile

Marcala, located in the mountains of La Paz department in southwestern Honduras, has been producing coffee since the late 1800s. Altitude (1,300-1,700+ MASL), climate, soil, and traditional cultivation create ideal conditions for specialty coffee. The region's 2005 Denomination of Origin certification protects and promotes Marcala coffee's reputation.

**Historical Significance**: Honduras surpassed Guatemala in 2011 to become Central America's #1 coffee producer. This growth was driven by quality improvement, cooperative organization, and specialty market development. Marcala led this transformation, becoming the first Honduran coffee region to achieve international quality recognition.

**Misconception Alert**: Some perceive Honduran coffee as commodity-grade due to its historical bulk production. Modern Honduran specialty coffee, especially from Marcala and other designated regions, equals or exceeds neighboring countries' quality. Honduras offers exceptional value‚Äîquality comparable to Guatemala or Costa Rica at lower prices.

## üå± Variety Information: Pacas

### Pacas Variety Origins & History

**Botanical Background**:
- **Species**: *Coffea arabica*
- **Varietal lineage**: Natural mutation of Bourbon
- **Discovery**: El Salvador, 1949 (Pacas family farm)
- **Characteristic**: Dwarf mutation (compact growth, like Caturra)
- **Genetics**: Similar to Caturra (both Bourbon mutations)
- **Introduction to Honduras**: 1970s-1980s
- **Current status**: Widely planted in Central America, especially Honduras and El Salvador

**Pacas vs. Caturra**:
- Both are compact Bourbon mutations
- Discovered independently (Caturra in Brazil 1930s, Pacas in El Salvador 1949)
- Genetically very similar (both single-gene dwarf mutations)
- Pacas is slightly more common in Honduras and El Salvador
- Cup quality essentially identical (both excellent)
- Slight morphological differences (minor)

**Why Pacas in Honduras**:
- **Compact size**: Higher planting density (more trees per hectare)
- **Yield**: Higher productivity than Bourbon (per hectare basis)
- **Quality**: Excellent cup quality (retains Bourbon genetics)
- **Altitude adaptation**: Thrives at Marcala's 1,300-1,700 MASL
- **Smallholder fit**: Manageable size for small farms
- **Economic viability**: Good balance of quality and productivity

### Pacas Plant Characteristics

**Tree Morphology**:
- **Height**: 1.5-2.5 meters (compact, short)
- **Shape**: Conical, dense, bushy
- **Branching**: Close internodes, 45¬∞ branch angle
- **Leaves**: Bronze-tipped young leaves (Bourbon characteristic)
- **Density**: 4,000-7,000 trees/hectare possible
- **Management**: Easy to harvest and prune (low height)

**Cherry & Bean Characteristics**:
- **Cherry size**: Medium, round
- **Cherry color**: Red when ripe
- **Beans per cherry**: Typically 2
- **Bean shape**: Round to oval (Bourbon-like)
- **Bean size**: Medium (screen 15-17 typical)
- **Density**: High at altitude (SHG)

**Yield Characteristics**:
- **Productivity**: 30-50% higher than Bourbon (per hectare)
- **Per tree**: Lower than Bourbon per tree
- **Per hectare**: Higher due to planting density
- **Biennial bearing**: Less pronounced than Bourbon
- **Economic advantage**: Good yields with quality

**Disease Resistance**:
- **Coffee Leaf Rust (CLR)**: Susceptible (major challenge)
- **Coffee Berry Disease**: Moderately susceptible
- **Management**: Requires shade, nutrition, fungicide if needed
- **In Honduras**: Rust increasingly problematic (climate change)
- **Cooperative support**: Technical assistance for disease management

### Pacas Cup Quality

## üîÑ Processing: Washed Method in Marcala

### Harvest Season & Timing

**Harvest Calendar**:
- **Main season**: December-March
- **Peak**: January-February
- **Duration**: 3-4 months
- **Passes**: 3-5 selective picking rounds
- **Interval**: 10-15 days between passes

**Cooperative Collection**:
- **Delivery**: Farmers bring cherries to cooperative wet mill
- **Same-day processing**: Quality preservation
- **Payment**: Based on ripe cherry weight and quality
- **Traceability**: Lot tracking for quality control

### Washed Processing‚ÄîMarcala Cooperative Method

**Stage 1: Reception & Sorting** (Day 1)

- **Visual inspection**: Cooperative staff check ripeness
- **Flotation**: Cherries in water tanks, floaters removed
- **Quality control**: Reject underripe and defects
- **Sorting effectiveness**: 3-5% removed
- **Farmer education**: Feedback on cherry quality

**Stage 2: Depulping** (Day 1)

- **Equipment**: Mechanical disc depulpers
- **Process**: Skin and pulp removed
- **Result**: Parchment + mucilage beans
- **Quality settings**: Adjusted to prevent bean damage
- **Pulp management**: Composted for organic fertilizer

**Stage 3: Fermentation** (12-24 hours)

**Cooperative Fermentation Standards**:
- **Method**: Dry fermentation (tanks without water) typical
- **Duration**: 14-20 hours at Marcala altitude (cooler temps)
- **Temperature**: 18-22¬∞C (altitude moderation)
- **Monitoring**: Experienced operators test timing
- **Quality control**: Proper fermentation = clean acidity
- **Timing critical**: Under or over-fermentation creates defects

**Stage 4: Washing** (Day 2)

- **Channels**: Running water washing systems
- **Agitation**: Mechanical stirring
- **Multiple rinses**: 2-3 wash cycles
- **Density sorting**: Continuous (floaters removed)
- **Clean water**: Mountain spring water
- **Result**: Clean parchment coffee

**Stage 5: Drying** (10-15 days)

**Drying Methods**:

**Sun Drying** (Primary):
- **Surface**: Raised beds (African beds) or patios
- **Layer**: 2-3 cm thickness
- **Raking**: Every 1-2 hours during sun
- **Coverage**: Covered at night, protected from rain
- **Duration**: 12-15 days to 10-12% moisture
- **Dry season advantage**: Reliable December-March weather

**Mechanical Drying** (Supplemental):
- **Equipment**: Guardiola dryers (some cooperatives)
- **Use**: Finish drying, weather backup
- **Temperature**: 40-45¬∞C maximum
- **Quality**: Careful temperature control

**Target**: 10-12% moisture content

**Stage 6: Hulling & Grading**

**Dry Milling**:
- **Storage**: Parchment stored until milling
- **Hulling**: Parchment mechanically removed
- **Timing**: Before export or sale

**Sorting & Grading**:
- **Screen sorting**: Size uniformity (15-17 typical for Pacas)
- **Gravity tables**: Density sorting removes low-density beans
- **Electronic sorting**: Color sorters remove defects
- **Hand sorting**: 2-3 final passes
- **Defect removal**: Broken, discolored, damaged beans removed

**SHG Grading** (Strictly High Grown):
- **Definition**: Coffee grown 1,200+ MASL (4,000+ feet)
- **Marcala qualification**: All proper-altitude Marcala is SHG
- **Honduran grades**: SHG (highest) > HG > CS
- **Quality indicator**: Altitude-based grading

**Export Preparation**:
- **Bagging**: 60 kg or 69 kg jute bags (GrainPro liners)
- **Marking**: Marcala DO, grade, lot number, certifications
- **Documentation**: Traceability, certifications
- **Port**: Puerto Cort√©s (Caribbean coast)
- **Destination**: Specialty markets globally
