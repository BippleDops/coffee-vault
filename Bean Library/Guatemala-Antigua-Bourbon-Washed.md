## üìã Overview

Guatemala Antigua Bourbon Washed represents one of the most classic and celebrated coffee profiles in the specialty coffee world. This coffee embodies the perfect intersection of ideal terroir, traditional variety, and time-honored processing methods that have made Antigua one of the most renowned coffee-growing regions globally.

**Key Characteristics**:
- Classic Guatemalan volcanic terroir expression
- Traditional Bourbon variety with excellent cup quality
- SHB (Strictly Hard Bean) grade indicating high altitude
- Traditional washed processing for clarity and balance
- Chocolate and spice-forward flavor profile
- Medium-full body with bright acidity
- Exceptional consistency and reliability
- Foundation coffee for understanding Central American profiles

The Antigua valley, surrounded by three volcanic peaks including the active Volc√°n de Fuego, creates one of the world's most distinctive coffee terroirs. The volcanic soils, high altitude, consistent climate, and traditional cultivation practices combine to produce coffees of remarkable depth, complexity, and consistency year after year.

**Historical Significance**: Antigua has been producing coffee since the 1750s, making it one of the oldest coffee regions in the Americas. The region's coffee has been so highly regarded that "Antigua" became a protected denomination of origin in Guatemala, similar to wine appellations in Europe.

**Misconception Alert**: While many modern specialty coffees emphasize exotic varieties and experimental processing, Antigua Bourbon Washed demonstrates that traditional combinations can still produce world-class coffee. This is not "basic" coffee‚Äîit's the foundation upon which specialty coffee appreciation is built.

## üå± Variety Information: Bourbon

### Bourbon Variety Origins & History

**Botanical Background**:
- **Species**: *Coffea arabica*
- **Varietal lineage**: Natural mutation of Typica
- **Origin**: R√©union Island (formerly √éle Bourbon), Indian Ocean
- **Introduction to Americas**: 1800s via Brazil
- **Guatemala introduction**: Early 1900s, became dominant by mid-century
- **Current status**: Still valued for quality despite lower yields

**Genetic Characteristics**:
- Distinct from Typica in plant morphology and bean shape
- More compact growth than Typica
- Higher yield potential than Typica (but still low vs. modern varieties)
- Greater genetic diversity than Typica (more stable production)
- Natural mutations created color variants (Red, Yellow, Pink Bourbon)

**Why Bourbon in Guatemala**:
- Introduced for superior cup quality over other varieties available
- Adapted well to Guatemalan highland conditions
- Thrives at high altitudes (4,000-6,000 feet)
- Cold tolerance for highland nights
- Established before rust-resistant varieties developed
- Cultural preference for traditional varieties in Antigua

### Bourbon Plant Characteristics

**Tree Morphology**:
- **Height**: 2.5-3.5 meters (typically kept pruned to 2-2.5m)
- **Shape**: Conical, relatively compact
- **Branching**: 45-50¬∞ branch angle (vs. 50-80¬∞ in Typica)
- **Internodes**: Shorter than Typica (more compact)
- **Leaves**: Broad, wavy edges, light green to bronze young leaves
- **Roots**: Deep taproot system (good for volcanic soils)

**Cherry & Bean Characteristics**:
- **Cherry size**: Medium, round to oval
- **Cherry color**: Deep red when ripe (Red Bourbon)
- **Beans per cherry**: Typically 2 (normal for arabica)
- **Bean shape**: Round to oval, less elongated than Typica
- **Bean size**: Medium (screen 15-17 typical)
- **Density**: High at altitude (characteristic of SHB)
- **Parchment color**: Yellowish-green

**Yield Characteristics**:
- **Productivity**: 20-30% higher than Typica
- **Still low**: Compared to modern high-yield varieties (Caturra, Castillo)
- **Biennial bearing**: Tendency toward alternate heavy/light years
- **Production per tree**: 0.5-1.5 kg cherries/tree/year (altitude dependent)
- **Commercial viability**: Requires premium prices to justify cultivation

**Disease Resistance**:
- **Coffee Leaf Rust (CLR)**: Susceptible (major challenge)
- **Coffee Berry Disease**: Moderately susceptible
- **Nematodes**: Moderate resistance (deep roots help)
- **Bacterial diseases**: Susceptible
- **Management**: Requires shade, proper nutrition, fungicide applications
- **Climate dependency**: Performs better in drier climates (Antigua's dry season helps)

### Bourbon Cup Quality Characteristics

**Why Bourbon Is Prized for Quality**:

**Flavor Complexity**:
- Complex sugar development during slow maturation
- Balanced acidity with sweetness
- Clean cup with clarity
- Distinctive varietal character
- Terroir expression (shows origin characteristics clearly)

**Sensory Attributes**:
- **Sweetness**: High, caramel and brown sugar notes
- **Acidity**: Bright but balanced, malic and citric acids
- **Body**: Medium to full, round mouthfeel
- **Flavor notes**: Chocolate, nuts, caramel, fruit (depends on terroir)
- **Balance**: Exceptional, no single attribute dominates
- **Complexity**: Layered, evolves as coffee cools
- **Clean cup**: Very high, few off-flavors when well-processed

**Chemical Composition** (studies on Bourbon):
- **Sucrose content**: Higher than many varieties (12-14% green bean)
- **Chlorogenic acids**: Moderate levels (affects acidity and bitterness)
- **Trigonelline**: Good levels (contributes to aroma development)
- **Caffeine**: Standard arabica levels (1.2-1.5%)
- **Lipids**: 15-17% (contributes to body and mouthfeel)

**Roast Response**:
- Even development across roast spectrum
- Excellent at light roasts (origin character shines)
- Superb at medium roasts (balanced development)
- Good at medium-dark (sweetness comes forward)
- Retains character even into dark roasts (unusual for quality varieties)

### Bourbon in Antigua Context

**Why Bourbon + Antigua = Excellence**:

1. **Altitude adaptation**: Bourbon thrives at Antigua's 1,500-1,700 MASL
2. **Volcanic soil**: Deep roots access mineral-rich subsoil
3. **Slow maturation**: Cool temperatures extend ripening (flavor development)
4. **Dry season**: Reduces rust pressure on susceptible variety

**Traditional Shade Management**:
- **Inga trees**: Most common shade tree in Antigua

  - Moderate shade (30-40% cover)
  - Prune-able to manage light levels
- **Gravilea**: Some farms use for shade
- **Fruit trees**: Avocado, citrus provide income diversity
- **Shade benefits for Bourbon**:
  - Reduces leaf rust pressure
  - Moderates temperature extremes
  - Slows cherry ripening (quality improvement)
  - Protects from excessive sun (high altitude intensity)
  - Creates microclimate humidity (reduces stress)

**Antigua Bourbon Cultivation Practices**:
- **Planting density**: 3,000-5,000 trees/hectare
- **Pruning**: Systematic renewal pruning on 4-7 year cycles
- **Fertilization**: Organic matter plus mineral supplementation
- **Pest management**: Integrated approach, selective fungicide for rust
- **Harvesting**: Multiple selective passes (4-6 per season)
- **Quality control**: Only ripe cherry accepted at mills

## üîÑ Processing: Washed Method in Antigua

### Harvest Season & Timing

**Harvest Calendar**:
- **Main season**: December-March (peak January-February)
- **Duration**: 3-4 months for full harvest
- **Passes**: 4-6 selective picking rounds
- **Interval between passes**: 10-15 days
- **Late harvest**: Some farms extend into April for highest altitude lots

**Ripeness Selection**:
- **Target**: Fully ripe cherry, deep red color
- **Brix levels**: 18-22¬∞ Brix at optimal ripeness
- **Visual cues**: Uniform deep red, no green undertones
- **Feel**: Slight softness, not mushy
- **Rejection**: Underripe, overripe, defective cherries left on tree or sorted out

**Labor & Harvesting**:
- **Method**: 100% hand-picking (terrain requires it)
- **Workforce**: Mix of family labor and seasonal workers
- **Skill**: Experienced pickers select only ripe cherries
- **Daily harvest**: 40-100 kg per picker (terrain-dependent)
- **Transport**: Same-day delivery to wet mill (quality preservation)
- **Timing**: Morning picking preferred (cooler temperatures)

### Washed Processing Step-by-Step

Antigua's traditional washed processing is critical to the region's quality reputation.

**Stage 1: Reception & Sorting** (Day 1, within 6 hours of picking)

- **Delivery**: Cherries arrive at mill in sacks or trucks
- **Initial inspection**: Visual check for quality, defects
- **Flotation sorting**:
  - Cherries poured into water-filled tanks
  - Ripe beans sink (higher density)
  - Floaters removed (unripe, defective, dried cherries)
  - Debris (leaves, sticks) skimmed off
- **Sorting effectiveness**: Removes 2-5% of delivered cherries
- **Water source**: Spring water or wells (volcanic aquifer, pure)

**Stage 2: Depulping** (Day 1, same day as harvest)

- **Equipment**: Disc depulpers (mechanical)
- **Process**:
  - Cherries fed through adjustable gap between discs
  - Skin and most pulp removed
  - Parchment-covered beans emerge
  - Mucilage layer (pectin) remains attached
- **Quality control**:
  - Unripe beans don't depulp cleanly (easier to sort out)
  - Adjustable settings prevent bean damage
- **By-product management**: Pulp composted for fertilizer
- **Timing critical**: Must occur within 24 hours of picking

**Stage 3: Fermentation** (24-48 hours, Day 1-3)

This is where washed processing creates Antigua's signature clean, bright profile.

- **Method**: Dry fermentation (common in Antigua)
  - Beans in tanks without added water
  - Natural enzymes break down mucilage
  - Exothermic process (generates heat)
- **Alternative**: Wet fermentation (some mills)
  - Beans submerged in water
  - Slower fermentation (cooler)
  - Different flavor development

- **Duration factors**:
  - Temperature: 18-22¬∞C in Antigua (altitude moderates)
  - Altitude: Cooler = slower fermentation (24-48 hours typical)
  - Ambient temperature: Season affects timing
  - Batch size: Larger batches generate more heat
  - Mucilage thickness: Varies with ripeness

- **Monitoring**:
  - Touch test: Mucilage changes from slippery to rough
  - Visual: Color change and texture
  - Water test: Sample washed, check if mucilage releases cleanly
  - Experience: Mill workers sense optimal timing

- **Risks**:
  - **Under-fermentation**: Residual mucilage, hinders drying, potential defects
  - **Over-fermentation**: Vinegar/fermented flavors, stinker beans
  - **Uneven fermentation**: Inconsistent batches

- **Impact on flavor**:
  - Clean fermentation = bright acidity, clarity
  - Optimal fermentation = sweetness development
  - Controlled fermentation = balanced cup

**Stage 4: Washing** (Day 2-3)

- **Purpose**: Remove fermented mucilage completely
- **Method**:
  - Running water channels (traditional Antigua design)
  - Beans agitated in flowing water
  - Multiple rinse cycles
  - Gradient channels (density separation continues)
- **Water requirements**: Large volumes (recycling/treatment critical)
- **Quality check**: Visual inspection for clean parchment
- **Final density sort**: Lighter beans (lower quality) float and are removed
- **Result**: Clean parchment coffee, ready for drying

**Stage 5: Drying** (7-21 days, Day 3-24)

Antigua's climate allows traditional sun-drying during harvest season's dry weather.

**Sun-Drying (Traditional Method)**:
- **Surface**: Patios (concrete), raised beds (some farms), greenhouse dryers
- **Initial thickness**: 2-3 cm layer (thin for even drying)
- **Raking**: Every 30-60 minutes during sun exposure
- **Coverage**: Tarps at night, during midday heat, if rain threatens
- **Duration**: 12-21 days to target moisture
- **Advantages**:
  - Best flavor development (slow, even drying)
  - Lower cost (no fuel)
  - Traditional quality
- **Challenges**:
  - Weather dependent
  - Labor intensive
  - Space requirements

**Mechanical Drying (Guardiola - Some Farms)**:
- **Equipment**: Rotating drum dryers
- **Fuel**: Wood, coffee pulp, or propane
- **Temperature**: Controlled 40-50¬∞C max
- **Duration**: 24-48 hours
- **Use cases**:
  - Finish drying after sun pre-drying (common)
  - Emergency drying if weather threatens
  - High-volume operations
- **Advantages**: Weather-independent, faster, consistent
- **Disadvantages**: Risk of over-drying or baked flavor if mismanaged

**Target Moisture Content**: 10-12% (optimal for storage and export)

**Drying Monitoring**:
- **Moisture meters**: Regular testing
- **Visual cues**: Parchment color (golden), texture (brittle)
- **Sound test**: Parchment rattles when shaken
- **Bite test**: Parchment breaks cleanly

**Storage Before Milling**:
- **Form**: Parchment coffee (protective layer retained)
- **Storage**: Cool, dry warehouses
- **Duration**: Can be stored weeks to months
- **Conditioning**: Allows moisture to equalize within beans
- **Quality preservation**: Parchment protects green coffee

### Dry Milling & Preparation for Export

**Hulling**:
- **Process**: Mechanical removal of parchment layer
- **Equipment**: Huller machines
- **Result**: Green coffee exposed
- **Timing**: Done before export or roasting

**Sorting & Grading**:
- **Screen sorting**: Beans separated by size
  - Screens 15-19 typical for Bourbon
  - Uniform size ensures even roasting
- **Density sorting**: Gravity tables remove low-density beans
- **Electronic sorting**: Color sorters remove defects
- **Hand sorting**: Final quality control
  - Experienced sorters remove visual defects
  - Quakers, insect damage, discolored beans removed
  - Multiple passes for premium lots

**SHB Grading**:
- **Definition**: Strictly Hard Bean
- **Requirement**: Grown at 1,350+ MASL (4,500+ feet)
- **Significance**: Altitude designation, not quality grade per se
- **Antigua qualification**: All Antigua at proper altitude qualifies
- **Export standard**: SHB is Guatemala's premium export designation

**Final Preparation**:
- **Bagging**: 60 kg or 69 kg bags (export standards)
- **Marking**: Origin, grade, lot information
- **Documentation**: Traceability, certifications
- **Storage**: Climate-controlled until shipment
- **Export**: Through Puerto Quetzal or Santo Tom√°s ports

### Impact of Washed Processing on Cup Profile

**Flavor Clarity**:
- Washed processing removes all fruit before drying
- Bean flavors expressed cleanly without fruit interference
- Terroir characteristics more apparent
- Variety characteristics shine through
- Acidity is bright and clean

**Antigua's Traditional Excellence**:
- Generations of processing expertise
- Infrastructure investment in quality wet mills
- Clean water sources (volcanic springs)
- Ideal climate for controlled drying
- Quality-focused culture (reputation protection)

**Comparison to Other Processing**:
- **vs. Natural**: Washed is cleaner, brighter, less fruity
- **vs. Honey**: Washed is clearer, honey is sweeter with more body
- **vs. Wet-hulled**: Washed is cleaner, wet-hulled is earthier with more body

### Roast Profile Development

**Bean Density Considerations**:
- SHB designation = high density (hard bean)
- Requires adequate heat to penetrate dense structure
- Can handle higher charge temperatures
- Benefits from extended development time

**Suggested Profile** (Medium Roast - 12 lb batch):

**Pre-heat**: 425-450¬∞F (218-232¬∞C)

**Charge Temperature**: 390-410¬∞F (199-210¬∞C)
- Higher charge for dense SHB beans

**Drying Phase** (0:00-6:00):
- **Target**: Smooth temperature rise
- **Rate of rise**: Declining curve, 15-25¬∞F/min at midpoint
- **Turning point**: 150-170¬∞F (66-77¬∞C), 0:45-1:15
- **Yellow point**: 5:00-6:30
- **Goal**: Even heat penetration, no baking
- **Color**: Green ‚Üí yellow ‚Üí tan

**Browning/Maillard Phase** (6:00-9:30):
- **Target**: Sugar caramelization, Maillard reactions
- **Rate of rise**: 8-15¬∞F/min
- **Color development**: Tan ‚Üí light brown ‚Üí medium brown
- **Aroma**: Toasted grain ‚Üí sweet, nutty
- **Critical period**: Flavor development
- **Heat management**: Increase airflow if needed to prevent stalling

**First Crack** (~9:30-10:30):
- **Onset temperature**: 385-395¬∞F (196-202¬∞C)
- **Duration**: 60-90 seconds for full crack
- **Energy**: Endothermic (temperature rise slows)
- **Physical change**: Cell structure expansion
- **Monitoring**: Listen for crack, watch for smoke

**Development Phase** (Post-Crack):
- **Target for medium roast**: 75-90 seconds post-crack
- **Rate of rise**: 3-8¬∞F/min (declining)
- **Color**: Medium brown, no oil
- **Aroma**: Sweet, chocolate, spice
- **Goal**: Develop complexity and sweetness

**Drop**: 415-425¬∞F (213-218¬∞C)
- **Total time**: 11:00-13:00 minutes
- **Development ratio**: 15-18% (development time / total time)
- **Visual**: Medium brown, no surface oil
- **Aroma check**: Sweet, complex, no burnt notes

**Cooling**:
- Immediate, aggressive cooling to lock in roast
- Target <100¬∞F within 4-5 minutes
- Air cooling or water misting (if equipment allows)

### Roasting Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge 1: Uneven Development (Baking)**
- **Symptom**: Grassy, papery flavors; lack of sweetness
- **Cause**: Insufficient heat during drying/browning phase
- **Solution**:
  - Higher charge temperature
  - Increase gas/heat during browning phase
  - Shorter total roast time (faster development)

**Challenge 2: Scorching**
- **Symptom**: Burnt, charred flavors; tipping (burnt bean tips)
- **Cause**: Excessive heat, too-fast temperature rise
- **Solution**:
  - Lower charge temperature
  - Reduce gas during drying phase
  - Increase airflow to moderate heat
  - SHB is dense, less prone to this than lighter beans

**Challenge 3: Under-Development**
- **Symptom**: Sour, astringent, vegetal notes
- **Cause**: Too-short development time post-crack
- **Solution**:
  - Extend development 15-30 seconds
  - Ensure beans reach full development temperature
  - Visual check: beans should be uniformly colored

**Challenge 4: Flat Profile**
- **Symptom**: One-dimensional flavor, lack of complexity
- **Cause**: Poor heat management during Maillard phase
- **Solution**:
  - Better heat application during browning (7:00-9:00)
  - Avoid temperature stalls
  - Optimize airflow

### Batch-to-Batch Consistency

**Variables to Control**:
- Ambient temperature and humidity (affects roasting)
- Bean moisture content (target 10-12%)
- Batch size (affects heat mass)
- Equipment pre-heat time
- Cooling efficiency

**Documentation**:
- Record every roast (temperature, time, sensory notes)
- Track development ratios
- Cup each batch
- Adjust profile based on feedback

### Water Quality & Chemistry

**Ideal Water Profile for Antigua Bourbon**:
- **TDS**: 100-150 ppm (moderate mineralization)
- **Hardness**: 50-100 ppm as CaCO‚ÇÉ
- **Calcium**: 30-70 ppm
- **Magnesium**: 10-30 ppm
- **Alkalinity**: 40-75 ppm (buffers acidity slightly)
- **pH**: 6.5-7.5

**Why this matters**:
- Calcium extracts chocolate and body compounds
- Magnesium extracts acidity and brightness
- Moderate alkalinity balances Antigua's bright acidity
- TDS range provides extraction without over-extraction

**Water Issues to Avoid**:
- **Too soft** (<50 ppm TDS): Under-extraction, sour, weak
- **Too hard** (>200 ppm TDS): Over-extraction, bitter, muddy
- **High alkalinity**: Mutes acidity (dulls Antigua brightness)
- **Chlorine**: Off-flavors (use filtered water)

### Grinding Recommendations

**Grind Size Impact**:
- **Too fine**: Over-extraction, bitter, astringent, muddy
- **Too coarse**: Under-extraction, sour, weak, thin
- **Just right**: Balanced extraction, clarity, sweetness

**Grinder Type**:
- **Burr grinder**: Essential for consistency (flat or conical)
- **Blade grinder**: Not recommended (uneven particle size)
- **Hand grinder**: Excellent for small batches, travel

**Freshness**:
- Grind immediately before brewing
- Oxidation begins instantly after grinding
- Pre-ground loses 60%+ aroma within 15 minutes

### Brewing Troubleshooting

**Problem**: Sour, acidic cup
- **Causes**: Under-extraction, too-coarse grind, low water temp, short time
- **Solutions**: Finer grind, hotter water, longer brew time

**Problem**: Bitter, harsh cup
- **Causes**: Over-extraction, too-fine grind, high water temp, long time
- **Solutions**: Coarser grind, cooler water, shorter brew time

**Problem**: Weak, watery cup
- **Causes**: Too-low coffee:water ratio, too-coarse grind
- **Solutions**: More coffee, finer grind

**Problem**: Flat, one-dimensional
- **Causes**: Stale coffee, poor water quality, wrong brew method
- **Solutions**: Fresh coffee, filtered water, optimize method
