## üìã Overview

Tanzania Kilimanjaro Peaberry Washed represents a unique combination of exceptional terroir and rare bean morphology. Grown on the slopes of Africa's highest mountain, Mount Kilimanjaro, this coffee features "peaberry" beans‚Äîa natural mutation where the coffee cherry produces a single, round bean instead of the typical two flat-sided beans.

**Key Characteristics**:
- **Peaberry beans**: Single round bean (5-8% of harvest)
- **Mount Kilimanjaro terroir**: Volcanic soil, high altitude (1,400-2,000 MASL)
- **African heirloom varieties**: Bourbon, Kent, Typica genetics
- **Washed processing**: Clean, bright, complex profile
- **Winey, berry-forward flavor**: Blackcurrant, red wine, citrus
- **High acidity**: Bright, structured, wine-like
- **Medium body**: Elegant mouthfeel
- **Peaberry premium pricing**: 20-30% higher than regular beans
- **Specialty market favorite**: Unique, sought-after
- **Tanzania coffee heritage**: Kilimanjaro is Tanzania's premier coffee region

Mount Kilimanjaro (5,895m/19,341 ft), Africa's tallest peak, creates a unique coffee terroir. The volcanic slopes, altitude, climate, and rich soils produce coffee of exceptional brightness and complexity. When combined with the concentrated flavor intensity of peaberry beans, the result is one of Africa's most distinctive coffees.

**Historical Significance**: Coffee was introduced to Tanzania (then German East Africa, later Tanganyika) in the late 1800s by missionaries. Mount Kilimanjaro became the premier growing region. Tanzania's coffee industry was nationalized post-independence (1960s-1980s), leading to quality decline. Liberalization in the 1990s and specialty coffee movement have revitalized Tanzanian coffee, with Kilimanjaro leading the renaissance.

**Misconception Alert**: Many believe peaberry is a variety or that all peaberries taste better. Reality: Peaberry is a bean mutation (not variety), occurring in all varieties. The alleged superior flavor is debated‚Äîsome claim concentrated flavor from single-bean development, others say it's selection bias (only best lots are sorted as peaberry). What's certain: peaberry beans roast more evenly due to round shape, justifying some price premium.

## üå± Variety Information & Peaberry Phenomenon

### African Heirloom Varieties in Tanzania

Tanzania Kilimanjaro coffee is typically a mix of African heirloom varieties:

**Primary Varieties**:

1. **Bourbon** (Most common)
   - French Mission Bourbon introduction (late 1800s)
   - Excellent cup quality
   - Moderate yield
   - Genetic base for Tanzania coffee

2. **Kent** (Significant presence)
   - Selected in India (1920s) from Typica
   - Better disease resistance than Typica/Bourbon
   - Good cup quality
   - Adapted to Tanzanian conditions

3. **Typica** (Less common now)
   - Original introduction variety
   - Tall, low-yielding
   - Excellent quality but economically challenging
   - Being replaced by more productive varieties

**Modern Varieties** (Increasing):
- **N39** (Bourbon selection): Tanzanian selection, good quality
- **KP423**: Kenyan variety, rust-resistant
- **Catimor types**: Rust resistance but lower cup quality (avoided in specialty)

### Peaberry: The Special Bean

**What Is Peaberry?**

**Normal Coffee Cherry**:
- Contains two flat-sided beans facing each other
- Each bean has one flat side, one curved side
- 92-95% of cherries produce normal beans
- Graded and sold as regular coffee

**Peaberry Cherry**:
- Contains ONE round bean instead of two flat beans
- Bean is completely round (both sides curved)
- 5-8% of cherries are peaberries (varies by variety, conditions)
- Requires sorting to separate from normal beans

**Why Peaberry Occurs**:
- **Natural mutation**: One ovule fails to develop, other ovule grows round
- **Genetic factors**: Some varieties have higher peaberry frequency
- **Environmental stress**: Stress may increase peaberry occurrence
- **Random**: Not fully understood, occurs naturally in all varieties

**Peaberry Characteristics**:
- **Shape**: Completely round, oval
- **Size**: Similar weight to single normal bean (half of two-bean pair)
- **Density**: Often perceived as denser (debated)
- **Screen size**: Slightly smaller than normal beans (round shape)
- **Appearance**: Distinctive round shape, easily identified

### Peaberry Flavor Profile‚ÄîMyth vs. Reality

**Claims About Peaberry**:
- **Proponents claim**: Concentrated flavor, better quality, sweeter, brighter
- **Skeptics claim**: No inherent difference, marketing, selection bias

**Scientific Perspective**:
- **Flavor concentration**: Theoretically, single bean gets all the cherry's nutrients (vs. sharing with second bean). However, this is speculative‚Äîlimited scientific evidence.
- **Roasting advantage**: CONFIRMED‚ÄîRound shape roasts more evenly than flat beans (no thin edges to scorch or thick centers to under-develop). This IS a legitimate quality factor.
- **Grading selection**: Peaberry sorting is labor-intensive and expensive, so only high-quality lots are sorted. This creates selection bias‚Äîpeaberry lots are often from better farms/cherries to begin with.

**Reality Check**:
Peaberry is NOT automatically superior, but:
1. Even roasting (round shape) is genuine advantage
2. Sorting process means quality coffee to start with
3. Concentrated flavor is plausible but not scientifically proven
4. Market perception creates demand, justifying price premium
5. Uniqueness and rarity add value regardless of cup quality

**Peaberry from Kilimanjaro**:
Tanzania Kilimanjaro Peaberry has strong market reputation for:
- Brightness and acidity (Kilimanjaro terroir)
- Berry and winey notes (East African characteristic)
- Clean, complex cup (washed processing)
- Even roast development (peaberry shape)
- Consistent quality (cooperative quality control)

## üîÑ Processing: Washed Method for Peaberry

### Harvest & Peaberry Selection

**Harvest Season**:
- **Main harvest**: June-September (long dry season)
- **Secondary harvest**: December-January (short dry season)
- **Peak**: July-August
- **Duration**: 3-4 months total

**Harvesting**:
- **Method**: Selective hand-picking
- **Passes**: 3-5 selective rounds
- **Ripeness**: Only ripe cherries (quality focus)
- **Labor**: Family and hired seasonal workers
- **Delivery**: Same-day to cooperative wet mill

### Washed Processing

**Stage 1: Reception & Flotation** (Day 1)

- **Delivery**: Farmers deliver to KNCU cooperative mills
- **Weighing**: Payment based on ripe cherry weight
- **Flotation**: Cherries in water tanks, floaters removed
- **Visual sorting**: Defects and unripe removed
- **Quality check**: Cooperative staff enforce standards

**Stage 2: Depulping** (Day 1)

- **Equipment**: Disc pulpers
- **Process**: Skin and pulp removed, parchment + mucilage remains
- **Quality**: Settings prevent bean damage
- **Pulp**: Composted for fertilizer

**Stage 3: Fermentation** (12-36 hours)

**Method**:
- **Dry fermentation**: Beans in tanks without added water (common)
- **Duration**: 12-36 hours depending on temperature
- **Temperature**: 15-20¬∞C at altitude (cooler = longer fermentation)
- **Monitoring**: Regular testing for mucilage breakdown
- **Goal**: Complete mucilage removal, bright acidity development

**Stage 4: Washing** (Day 2-3)

- **Channels**: Running water washing channels
- **Agitation**: Mechanical or manual stirring
- **Multiple rinses**: 2-3 wash cycles
- **Density sorting**: Floaters removed at each stage
- **Clean water**: Mountain stream water (pure, cold)

**Stage 5: Drying** (7-14 days)

**Sun Drying** (Primary method):
- **Surface**: Raised drying tables (African beds)
- **Initial thickness**: 2-3 cm layer
- **Raking**: Every 1-2 hours during sun
- **Coverage**: Covered at night, if rain threatens
- **Duration**: 10-14 days to 10-12% moisture
- **Dry season**: June-September perfect for drying

**Stage 6: Hulling & Peaberry Sorting** (Critical Step)

**Dry Milling**:
- **Storage**: Parchment stored until dry milling
- **Hulling**: Parchment mechanically removed
- **Screen sorting**: Beans separated by size and shape

**Peaberry Separation**:
- **Gravity/screen tables**: Round peaberries roll differently than flat beans
- **Size screens**: Peaberry size different from paired beans
- **Hand sorting**: Final separation and quality check
- **Labor intensive**: Explains premium pricing
- **Yield**: 5-8% of total harvest becomes peaberry

**Grading**:
- **Peaberry grade**: "PB" designation
- **Size**: Usually screen 15-16 (round measurement)
- **Defect sorting**: Hand-sorted to remove defects
- **Quality levels**: PB Premium, PB Grade 1, etc.

**Regular Bean Grades** (For comparison):
- **AA**: Largest screen size (screen 18+)
- **AB**: Medium-large (screen 15-17)
- **C**: Smaller beans
- **E**: Elephant beans (very large peaberries, rare)

### Processing Impact on Flavor

**Washed Processing**:
- Creates clean, bright cup
- Acidity is pronounced and structured
- Terroir and variety clearly expressed
- Berry and wine notes shine through
- Medium body (lighter than natural process)

**Peaberry Selection**:
- Uniformity in roasting (even development)
- Potential flavor concentration (debated)
- Consistency in cup profile (uniform bean size)
- Premium pricing justified by sorting labor

### Roasting Peaberry: The Advantage

**Peaberry Roasting Benefits**:
- **Even heat absorption**: Round shape heats uniformly (no thin edges)
- **No scorching**: Flat beans' thin edges can scorch; peaberry avoids this
- **Consistent development**: Every bean develops evenly
- **Easier roasting**: Less technique required for even roast
- **Less defects**: Fewer tipped or scorched beans
- **Justifies premium**: Even roasting is genuine quality advantage

**Roast Profile Adjustments**:
- **Slightly faster**: Round beans heat faster than flat beans
- **Watch closely**: First crack may come earlier
- **Airflow**: May need adjustment (round beans behave differently)
- **Consistency**: Peaberry batches more consistent than mixed sizes

**Suggested Profile** (Light Roast - 12 lb batch):

**Charge**: 395-410¬∞F (medium-high for dense African beans)
**Drying**: 6:00-7:00 (thorough drying)
**First crack**: ~9:30-10:30
**Development**: 45-75 seconds (short for light roast)
**Drop**: 405-415¬∞F
**Total time**: 10:30-12:00
**Development ratio**: 12-15%
